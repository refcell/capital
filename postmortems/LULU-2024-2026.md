# Lululemon 2024-2026: The Premium Athleisure Multiple Break

Date prepared: 2026-05-24
Company: lululemon athletica inc.
Ticker: NASDAQ: LULU
Event window: 2024-03-22 through 2026-05-22
Event type: premium-brand rerating / North America comp break / product-execution break / tariff and governance reset

This is investment research, not personalized financial advice.

## Snapshot

Lululemon was not killed by one quarter. It was re-rated in stages as the market
moved from "premium global athleisure compounder" to "great brand with a
shrinking North America growth algorithm, product execution problems, tariff
pressure, and a lower terminal multiple."

Using Yahoo Finance chart data, LULU traded as high as $516.39 on 2023-12-29.
It closed at $127.18 on 2026-05-22. That is a roughly 75% decline from the
late-2023 high. StockAnalysis showed a 2026-05-22 market cap of about
$14.55 billion, 114.43 million shares outstanding, a trailing P/E of 9.6x, and
a forward P/E of 10.4x. At the late-2023 high, using FY2023 adjusted EPS of
$12.77, the stock was capitalizing earnings at roughly 40x. Using the original
FY2024 guidance midpoint of $14.10, it was roughly 37x forward earnings.

|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| 2024-03-22 | FY2024 guide and U.S. slowdown | $478.84 | $403.19 | -15.8% |
| 2024-07-25 | Breezethrough product backlash / product-execution scare | $272.06 | $247.32 | -9.1% |

The table understates the psychological damage because it only shows event-day
moves. The bigger change was valuation. Lululemon went from a scarce premium
consumer compounder worth 35x-40x earnings to a challenged apparel retailer
worth about 10x earnings. The stock did not collapse because the company became
unprofitable. It collapsed because the market stopped believing the North
America profit pool deserved a premium terminal multiple.

## The Old Underwriting Model

The old LULU thesis had four pillars:

| Pillar | What the market believed |
|---|---|
| Premium brand | Lululemon could charge premium prices without relying on heavy discounting. |
| Product engine | Fabric, fit, community feedback, and newness would keep the brand culturally relevant. |
| North America runway | The U.S. and Canada could keep growing from stores, e-commerce, men's, casual, and new categories. |
| Global expansion | China and Rest of World would add a second growth leg without damaging the brand. |

That was the premium multiple. Lululemon did not need to be the fastest-growing
apparel company forever, but it did need to prove that North America remained a
healthy high-margin base while China, men's, and international expansion added
incremental growth.

The company's own strategic framework reinforced this. Power of Three x2 called
for revenue to double from $6.25 billion in 2021 to $12.5 billion by 2026,
driven by product innovation, guest experience, and market expansion. The plan
included doubling men's, doubling digital, and quadrupling international.

By late 2023, the market was still paying for that story. FY2023 net revenue
grew 19% to $9.6 billion, Americas comparable sales rose 8%, international
comparable sales rose 35%, gross margin was 58.3%, adjusted operating margin was
23.2%, and adjusted EPS was $12.77. Q4 2023 still looked strong on paper:
revenue rose 16%, Americas comparable sales rose 7%, international comparable
sales rose 43%, and gross margin reached 59.4%.

The hidden issue was that the first pillar to crack was the most important one:
North America.

## The Comp Break

Comparable sales are the simplest way to see the rerating. Lululemon's global
reported growth stayed positive because China and international markets were
still growing. The market cared more about the Americas because that was the
core brand proof point and the bulk of revenue.

| Period | Global comparable sales | Americas comparable sales | International / ex-Americas comparable sales |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Q4 2023 | +12% | +7% | +43% |
| Q1 2024 | +6% | flat | +25% |
| Q2 2024 | +2% | -3% | +19% |
| FY2024 | +4% | -1% | +22% |
| Q1 2025 | +1% | -2% | +6% |
| Q2 2025 | +1% | -4% | +15% |
| Q3 2025 | +1% | -5% | +18% |
| Q4 2025 | +3% | -1% | +20% |
| FY2025 | +2% | -3% | +15% |

That is the whole postmortem in one table. A premium multiple can survive slower
growth. It struggles to survive a two-year North America comp recession when the
brand is already priced as a category leader.

The 2025 10-K makes the mechanism explicit. Net revenue in the Americas
decreased 1% in FY2025 and Americas comparable sales decreased 3%. Management
attributed the decline to lower conversion rates, lower store traffic, and lower
average order value, partially reflecting lower demand in certain product
categories, including core categories. Product margin in the Americas declined
340 basis points, primarily because of tariffs and increased markdowns.

That matters because negative comps were not just a traffic problem. The chain
was:

1. Guests visited less often or converted less frequently.
2. Average order value fell.
3. Core categories saw weaker demand.
4. Inventory rose faster than sales.
5. Markdown pressure increased.
6. Tariffs and de minimis changes hit product cost.
7. Gross margin fell.
8. EPS guidance went down.
9. The terminal multiple went down.

## Why Same-Store Sales Went Down

The first answer is product. The deeper answer is that product and social
context changed together.

Lululemon's original advantage was not only technical fabric. It was technical
fabric plus taste, community, scarcity, and a direct feedback loop with
high-value guests. The brand made leggings, running gear, yoga apparel, and
casual athleisure feel like premium identity products rather than commodity
apparel.

By 2024-2025, that advantage was harder to see in the U.S. assortment. The
Breezethrough launch became a visible symbol of the issue. Lululemon paused
sales of the product after customer criticism, including complaints about fit
and appearance. A single failed legging does not destroy a brand, but it damages
the market's confidence in the feedback loop. If Lululemon's core skill is
understanding premium athletic women before competitors do, a high-profile
product miss in bottoms is not a normal miss.

The Q2 2025 call was the explicit admission. Management said the team had tried
to restore newness partly by increasing seasonal color in core styles, but
guests did not respond as expected. Calvin McDonald said product lifecycles had
run too long in many core categories, particularly lounge and social, that the
casual offering had become too predictable, and that Lululemon had missed
opportunities to create new trends. He also said the premium athletic-wear
market in the U.S. remained challenging, consumers were spending less on apparel
and performance activewear, and shoppers were being more selective and seeking
truly new styles.

That is why the September 2025 reaction was so severe. The market was not
reacting only to Americas comparable sales down 4%. It was reacting to
management's explanation for why they were down. The explanation converted the
problem from "temporary U.S. softness" into "the product engine is stale in the
most important market."

## Why Product Became A Social Problem

The social trend underneath Lululemon's rerating is not "people stopped wearing
leggings." It is more specific:

| Social / consumer shift | Why it hurt Lululemon |
|---|---|
| Athleisure became mainstream | What was once differentiated became a crowded uniform. |
| Consumers became more value-conscious | A $98-$128 core item needs more proof of novelty and quality. |
| TikTok and creator-led discovery accelerated trend cycles | Core color refreshes are weak currency when shoppers want visible newness. |
| Challenger brands segmented the market | Alo, Vuori, Athleta, Skims, Nike, On, Hoka, Set Active, and others each attack a different use case. |
| Fashion moved from pure leggings to wider silhouettes and lifestyle wardrobes | Lululemon's lounge/social lifecycle became a bigger issue. |
| Fitness culture fragmented | Yoga, Pilates, running, strength, wellness clubs, tennis, and lifestyle creators each have different uniforms. |

Lululemon used to sit at the center of the premium active lifestyle. The center
then fragmented. Alo became more of a celebrity/wellness studio and fashion
statement. Vuori became a softer coastal performance and men's lifestyle brand.
Athleta offered more accessible value within Gap's ecosystem. Skims trained
consumers to think about bodywear, shape, and social-led drops differently.
Nike, On, Hoka, and other running brands benefited from performance footwear and
run-club culture. Fast-fashion and Amazon dupes reduced the social penalty of
buying lookalikes.

This fragmentation matters because Lululemon's old moat was partly cultural
default status. If the customer says "this is the uniform," the brand can sell
core styles for years. If the customer says "this is one expensive option among
many," the brand has to compete on novelty, silhouette, community, and price
every season.

The company effectively said this itself. It did not say guests no longer like
Lululemon. It said guests were less responsive to refreshed seasonal colors and
wanted truly new styles. That is a very different demand environment. The brand
health can be strong while the stock still deserves a lower multiple, because
brand awareness is not the same as urgent full-price conversion.

Outside data points fit that diagnosis. McKinsey and BoF's 2025 fashion work
framed the broader market as low-growth and more difficult after the
post-pandemic boom, with challengers pressing incumbents in sportswear. Piper
Sandler's teen work showed Nike and Lululemon losing some youth mindshare, with
UGG overtaking Lululemon as the top fashion trend among upper-income female
teens in spring 2025 after Lululemon had held that slot since spring 2018. That
does not mean teens drive Lululemon's whole P&L. It means the status signal was
no longer as clean as it had been.

The dupe economy matters for the same reason. TikTok made it socially normal to
compare premium products against cheaper substitutes in public. A younger
consumer can now buy an alternative, post the comparison, and receive social
credit for value hunting rather than embarrassment for trading down. That
weakens the aura around premium basics unless the premium brand keeps producing
obvious design, fit, fabric, or cultural superiority.

## Why The First Break Happened In March 2024

The March 2024 break was the moment investors realized North America was no
longer a clean compounder.

Q4 2023 results were good. The problem was the FY2024 setup. Management guided
to FY2024 revenue of $10.7 billion to $10.8 billion, or 11%-12% growth including
the 53rd week, and EPS of $14.00 to $14.20. Q1 2024 revenue was expected to grow
only 9%-10%. More importantly, the Americas had already slowed from the prior
growth cadence.

The stock fell 15.8% the next day. That was rational because the multiple had
not been set for "good apparel company." It had been set for "scarce premium
retail compounder." When Americas growth slowed, investors had to ask whether
international growth was additive or merely masking a maturing home market.

Q1 2024 confirmed the concern. Revenue grew 10%, but Americas revenue grew only
3% and Americas comparable sales were flat. International revenue grew 35% and
international comparable sales rose 25%. The divergence was not temporary noise.
It was the new shape of the business.

## Why The Product Break Mattered More Than The Q2 2024 Print

Q2 2024 reported Americas comparable sales down 3%. Revenue guidance was cut,
and FY2024 revenue guidance moved down to $10.375 billion to $10.475 billion.
But the stock did not have a clean event-day gap on 2024-08-30 because a lot of
the damage had already happened. The stock had fallen from above $500 at the
start of the year to an August 2024 intraday low near $226.

The more important event was the product-execution scare around Breezethrough.
On 2024-07-25, LULU fell 9.1% as the market processed the visible product
backlash. The issue was not the revenue contribution of one launch. The issue
was symbolic. Lululemon's women's bottoms franchise is where the brand should
have the most customer intimacy. A failed high-profile launch suggested the
company's product feedback loop and taste filter were not as strong as the old
multiple assumed.

This is the first key lesson: when the stock's multiple is based on brand magic,
product misses are not small. They are evidence about the magic.

## Why The 2025 Break Was Worse

The 2025 rerating was worse because it combined U.S. demand, tariff economics,
inventory, and management credibility.

At the March 2025 FY2024 release, the company guided to FY2025 revenue of
$11.15 billion to $11.30 billion and EPS of $14.95 to $15.15. That sounded
fine in isolation, but it implied only 5%-7% reported growth, or 7%-8% excluding
the 53rd week. It also sat next to FY2024 Americas comparable sales down 1%.
The stock fell 14.2% on 2025-03-28 because the market wanted evidence of a
reacceleration and got a cautious growth framework instead.

Q1 2025 made the framework weaker. Net revenue grew 7%, but Americas comparable
sales fell 2%. Inventories increased 23% year over year while revenue grew 7%.
The company repurchased $430 million of stock in the quarter, but the market
was no longer willing to underwrite buybacks as a fix for weakening comps and
inventory risk. FY2025 EPS guidance was cut from $14.95-$15.15 to
$14.58-$14.78, and the stock fell 19.8% the next day.

Then Q2 2025 broke the remaining defense. Revenue grew 7%, but revenue fell
short of guidance because of the U.S. business. Americas comparable sales fell
4%. Gross margin fell 110 basis points. Operating margin fell 210 basis points.
Inventories increased 21%. Management cut FY2025 revenue guidance to
$10.85 billion to $11.00 billion and EPS guidance to $12.77-$12.97. The guide
included an estimated $240 million gross-profit reduction from higher tariffs
and removal of the de minimis exemption, net of mitigation efforts.

This was the stage where "cheap quality growth" stopped being the market's
default frame. If the U.S. core is negative, inventories are elevated, gross
margin is under pressure, and management is admitting product predictability,
then the stock is not simply a temporarily cheap compounder. It is a turnaround.
Turnarounds do not get the same terminal multiple.

## Tariffs Were The Accelerator, Not The Root Cause

Tariffs did not cause the brand issue. They made the brand issue financially
visible.

The 2025 10-K says tariff and de minimis changes had a significant adverse
effect on the business. The company said most products sourced for the U.S. are
now subject to higher tariffs, and that the majority of U.S. e-commerce orders
were historically fulfilled from Canadian distribution centers, with a
significant portion qualifying for the de minimis exemption. Removal of the
exemption increased the cost of fulfilling those orders. The unmitigated impact
of increased tariffs and de minimis removal reduced FY2025 gross profit by
approximately $275 million.

Tariffs hurt more when the brand cannot fully pass price increases through.
That is why they mattered to valuation. A premium brand should have pricing
power. But if conversion, traffic, and AOV are already falling in North America,
then price increases risk pushing more consumers toward competitors, discounts,
or dupes. The tariff shock made the market ask whether Lululemon still had
enough pricing power to protect premium margins.

## International Growth Could Not Save The Multiple

International growth stayed impressive. In FY2025, China Mainland revenue grew
29%, Rest of World revenue grew 16%, China Mainland comparable sales rose 20%,
and Rest of World comparable sales rose 9%. International is real, and it is the
best bull-case evidence.

The problem is mix and proof. In FY2025, the Americas still generated
$7.85 billion of Lululemon's $11.10 billion of revenue, or about 71%. China
Mainland generated $1.75 billion. The market can value China growth, but it will
not give the same enterprise multiple if the largest and most mature region is
declining and the product engine in that region looks stale.

There is also a quality-of-growth issue. If international growth is the only
growth leg, the stock starts to look more exposed to China execution,
geopolitics, store expansion, local competition, and the risk that overseas
growth eventually follows the North America maturity curve. The old thesis was
"North America is still strong and international is extra." The new thesis is
"international must offset North America while management fixes product."
Those are different multiples.

## The Governance Break

Governance did not start the rerating, but it confirmed it.

In December 2025, the company announced a CEO succession plan and a $1 billion
increase to the repurchase authorization. Q3 2025 results were better than some
feared, and the stock bounced 9.6% the next day. But the underlying numbers
still showed Americas comparable sales down 5%, gross margin down 290 basis
points, and operating margin down 350 basis points.

The April 2026 CEO announcement then became another stock break. The market had
already moved the company into a turnaround frame. A long transition, founder
criticism, public governance tension, and continued product questions made it
harder to underwrite a fast fix. The stock fell 13.3% on 2026-04-23.

The founder criticism matters because it was not random activism. Chip Wilson's
public complaints focused on the same issue the market was already debating:
product, brand direction, and board accountability. A founder can be biased, and
his governance preferences may not be the right solution. But when the founder's
complaint rhymes with falling comps and management's own product admission, it
becomes part of the market's evidence set.

## The Market Debate

The public market debate now has three camps.

| Camp | Argument | Evidence they use |
|---|---|---|
| Bull / valuation reset | LULU is now too cheap for a profitable, debt-light, global brand. | 9x-10x earnings, cash balance, buybacks, China growth, strong gross margin history, product action plan. |
| Turnaround neutral | The stock is optically cheap, but North America needs proof before the multiple expands. | Negative Americas comps, traffic/conversion/AOV weakness, inventory, markdowns, tariff uncertainty. |
| Bear / terminal impairment | The old brand edge is gone and the company is becoming an expensive basics retailer. | Product misses, stale casual assortment, challenger brands, dupe culture, founder criticism, delayed leadership reset. |

The analyst commentary has broadly followed that structure. Public sell-side
summaries and target changes in 2025-2026 focused on U.S. sales weakness,
product execution, tariffs, and lower valuation multiples. Morningstar's public
coverage continued to describe a valuable global brand but emphasized the need
to repair U.S. product momentum and compete with Athleta, Vuori, Alo, Nike, and
Skims. Piper Sandler's public target-cut summary in May 2026 was more bearish:
its expert call and channel work pointed to product and execution issues that
were not yet fixed. Seeking Alpha-style public deep dives tended to split the
difference: many liked the valuation after the collapse, but most made North
America comp recovery the necessary proof point.

The sequence of public analyst moves is important:

| Source / period | What changed | Why it mattered |
|---|---|---|
| CNBC / September 2025 analyst roundup | Analysts moved to the sidelines after the Q2 2025 outlook miss; Telsey cut its rating to market perform and cut its target to $200 from $360, while Oppenheimer's Brian Nagel had previously had a $500 target. | The sell side stopped treating the decline as a temporary setup and started cutting the multiple. |
| BofA / Telsey / September 2025 summaries | Downgrades and target cuts cited the U.S. turn being pushed out, product assortment execution, tariffs, and a tougher activewear market. | The debate broadened from one-quarter U.S. weakness to operating model and brand risk. |
| Piper Sandler / May 2026 public summary | Target cut to $130 from $190, neutral rating, following an expert call. | The current bear case is that product/execution issues remain live even after the stock is already down. |
| Seeking Alpha bull deep dives / May 2026 | The bull side argues the selloff is overdone because international growth, balance sheet strength, gross margins, and a 12-18 month recovery path create asymmetric upside. | This is the "cheap enough for a turnaround" camp. |
| Seeking Alpha bear deep dives / May 2026 | The bear side argues North America weakness and a stale product line make LULU still too expensive versus other apparel or luxury names. | This is the "terminal multiple is still too high" camp. |
| Morningstar / MarketWatch CEO commentary | Heidi O'Neill's Nike background is not automatically positive because Nike itself has had product and distribution challenges. | The new CEO helps only if she repairs product momentum; pedigree alone is not proof. |
| Forbes / Chip Wilson coverage | Wilson's five-pillar plan pushed product, creative leadership, and board accountability into the public debate. | Governance became a product-quality debate, not just a boardroom fight. |

That is a major change from the old debate. Investors used to ask "how long can
Lululemon compound?" Now they ask "is this a busted growth stock becoming a
value stock, or a value trap with a great logo?"

## Why The Multiple Collapsed

The multiple collapsed because every part of the old premium equation got less
certain at the same time.

| Old premium assumption | New evidence | Multiple impact |
|---|---|---|
| North America can keep comping positively | FY2025 Americas comps -3%; Q2 -4%; Q3 -5% | Lower revenue durability. |
| Product engine is hard to replicate | Breezethrough backlash; management admits lifecycles too long and casual product too predictable | Lower brand moat confidence. |
| Premium pricing protects margins | Tariffs, de minimis, increased markdowns, lower AOV | Lower margin durability. |
| International is incremental upside | International growth now offsets U.S. weakness | Lower quality of growth. |
| Buybacks add per-share value | Repurchases happened far above current price while EPS fell | Lower capital allocation credit. |
| Management has a clear long-term plan | CEO transition, founder criticism, action plan still unproven | Lower execution confidence. |

The old multiple was not only about earnings. It was about the market's belief
that earnings could compound with high confidence. Once the confidence fell,
the right multiple fell even faster than EPS.

The simple math:

| Period | Price / EPS reference | Implied multiple |
|---|---:|---:|
| 2023-12-29 high / FY2023 adjusted EPS | $516.39 / $12.77 | ~40x |
| 2023-12-29 high / original FY2024 guide midpoint | $516.39 / $14.10 | ~37x |
| 2025-06-06 close / post-Q1 FY2025 guide midpoint | $265.27 / $14.68 | ~18x |
| 2025-09-05 close / post-Q2 FY2025 guide midpoint | $167.80 / $12.87 | ~13x |
| 2026-05-22 close / FY2026 guide midpoint | $127.18 / $12.20 | ~10x |

That is the market's reasoning in numbers. The company went from premium
compounder to uncertain turnaround. The EPS denominator also fell, but the
multiple compression did most of the damage.

## Why This Was Not Just "Consumer Weakness"

A lazy explanation is that consumers are weak. That is incomplete.

If this were only macro, Lululemon would not need a product-creation action
plan. If this were only tariffs, Americas comps would not have been decelerating
before the tariff shock. If this were only competition, management would not
have described its own casual assortment as too predictable.

The better explanation is layered:

1. The U.S. premium activewear market matured after the pandemic.
2. Consumers became more selective because apparel budgets were under pressure.
3. Social media shortened trend cycles and rewarded visible newness.
4. Competitors and dupes made premium activewear less scarce.
5. Lululemon relied too heavily on core franchises, color refreshes, and long
   product lifecycles in lounge/social.
6. Product misses weakened the brand-feedback-loop narrative.
7. Lower traffic, conversion, AOV, and core-category demand showed up in
   Americas comps.
8. Tariffs and de minimis removal compressed margin just as pricing power was
   less certain.
9. Governance tension made the market wait for proof instead of trusting the
   plan.

That chain is why the valuation collapsed.

## What A Skeptical Investor Could Have Watched

The warning signs were visible before the stock reached $127.

| Signal | Why it mattered |
|---|---|
| Americas comparable sales slowing from +7% to flat to negative | The home-market comp engine broke first. |
| International growth masking U.S. weakness | Global growth quality was deteriorating. |
| Product launch backlash | The brand-feedback-loop moat was being questioned. |
| Inventory growth above revenue growth | Markdown risk and demand-forecasting risk. |
| Increasing repurchases while the stock was still expensive | Buybacks were not a substitute for product proof. |
| Management language around newness and color | Newness was being generated through color refreshes, not enough new trend creation. |
| Lower traffic, conversion, and AOV | The issue was not just channel mix. |
| Tariff and de minimis exposure | Margin pressure was likely to arrive before demand had recovered. |
| Founder / board tension | Product complaints were becoming governance complaints. |

The cleanest early signal was Q1 2024 Americas comparable sales being flat while
international comps were up 25%. That was the first quarter where the premium
multiple should have been interrogated harder.

## What Would Prove The Bull Case

The bull case is not dead. Lululemon is still profitable, global, premium, and
cash generative. It has no obvious debt crisis, it still has high gross margins
relative to most apparel retailers, and international growth remains real.

The bull case needs proof in a specific order:

1. Americas comparable sales turn positive without heavy markdowns.
2. Conversion and traffic improve, not just AOV from price increases.
3. Inventory growth falls below revenue growth for multiple quarters.
4. Gross margin stabilizes after tariff mitigation without brand-damaging price
   actions.
5. New product lines create real demand, not just color rotation in old core
   styles.
6. Women's bottoms, lounge, social, and casual categories show renewed
   momentum.
7. The new CEO can articulate a product-led plan that the market believes.
8. China and Rest of World continue to grow without requiring investors to
   ignore North America.

If those show up, a 9x-10x earnings multiple can be too low. A debt-light global
brand with positive comps and restored product momentum is worth more than a
turnaround multiple.

## What Would Prove The Bear Case

The bear case also has a clear proof path:

1. Americas comps remain negative through FY2026.
2. Management needs more markdowns to move inventory.
3. Newness continues to show up as color refreshes rather than trend creation.
4. Competitors take more mindshare in women's lifestyle, men's, and performance
   use cases.
5. Tariff mitigation requires price increases that hurt conversion.
6. China growth slows before North America recovers.
7. The CEO transition becomes a long strategic vacuum.
8. Buybacks continue without evidence that the core business is stabilizing.

If those happen, LULU is not simply cheap. It is a low-growth apparel retailer
with a premium-brand memory and a still-high absolute price point.

## Case Pattern

This is the "premium brand becomes an expensive basic" pattern.

The stock market often overpays for consumer brands when three things happen at
once: the brand has cultural heat, the product cycle feels repeatable, and the
financial model converts that heat into high-margin comp growth. The danger is
that cultural heat can decay before reported revenue does. A brand can still be
popular, still be profitable, and still have high awareness while the marginal
consumer becomes less urgent, less loyal, and more price-sensitive.

That is what happened to Lululemon. The company did not lose all brand value.
It lost enough product urgency in the core market that the old multiple no
longer worked.

The lesson is to underwrite premium brands from the customer outward:

1. Is the product still creating new desire, or merely refreshing old franchises?
2. Is the core market still comping, or is growth coming from new geographies
   that mask maturity?
3. Are margins protected by pricing power, or temporarily protected by price
   points that consumers are starting to resist?
4. Are buybacks being made after the multiple reset, or before the business
   problem is understood?
5. Are social trends expanding the brand's default status, or fragmenting it?

Lululemon's rerating was the market answering those questions in real time.
