# ServiceNow Q1 2026: The SaaS Terminal-Value Rerating

Date prepared: 2026-05-24
Company: ServiceNow, Inc.
Ticker: NYSE: NOW
Event date: 2026-04-23
Event type: sector rerating / SaaS terminal-value break

This is investment research, not personalized financial advice.

## Snapshot

ServiceNow fell roughly 19% intraday and closed down roughly 18% on 2026-04-23,
the trading day after it reported Q1 2026 results. Using Yahoo Finance chart
data, NOW closed at $103.07 on 2026-04-22, traded as low as $83.58 on
2026-04-23, and closed that day at $84.78.

This was not really a ServiceNow quarter-specific postmortem. It was a
public-market test of whether SaaS terminal values still deserved the same
underwriting framework after agentic AI started looking like a workflow
replacement rather than a feature upgrade.

The company-specific news mattered, but mostly as the catalyst. ServiceNow beat
the high end of its own Q1 guidance across highlighted topline and profitability
metrics, reported 22% subscription revenue growth, grew cRPO 22.5%, and said
Now Assist customers spending more than $1 million in ACV grew more than 130%
year over year. If the market had only been judging the current quarter, that
should not have produced an 18% drawdown.

The market reaction makes more sense as a terminal-value rerating. CNBC
reported that software stocks broadly sold off that day as fears escalated that
AI tools and services could disrupt software businesses. Salesforce, HubSpot,
Adobe, Intuit, Oracle, and Workday all fell hard, and the iShares Expanded
Tech-Software ETF fell roughly 6%. Reuters framed the same day as a widening AI
winner-loser divide: ServiceNow and IBM reignited AI disruption fears in
software while Texas Instruments lifted chipmakers.

## Corrected Thesis

The market had been capitalizing SaaS businesses as durable workflow toll roads.
Agentic AI forced investors to ask whether those toll roads were actually just
front ends, forms, seats, and workflow steps that an AI agent could abstract
away. That is a terminal-value question, not a quarter question.

In a normal DCF, a high-quality SaaS company gets a lot of its value from cash
flows far beyond the explicit forecast period. That terminal value depends on
durable growth, high retention, expanding seat counts, pricing power, high gross
margins, and a defensible workflow position. If AI agents compress seats, move
pricing from users to outcomes, raise compute costs, reduce product
differentiation, or shift workflow control to a model/orchestration layer, then
the terminal growth rate and terminal FCF multiple both have to come down.

| Assumption set | Terminal FCF multiple |
|---|---:|
| 8% discount rate / 3% terminal growth | 20.6x |
| 9% discount rate / 3% terminal growth | 17.2x |
| 10% discount rate / 2% terminal growth | 12.8x |
| 11% discount rate / 2% terminal growth | 11.3x |

That table is not meant to be a precise NOW valuation. It shows the mechanism.
If the market moves a software company from "durable 20x terminal FCF
compounder" to "still good but structurally less certain 13x terminal FCF
business," the equity can gap down even when current-period revenue and EPS beat
expectations.

## Setup

Before the event, the high-quality SaaS underwriting model was built on the idea
that enterprise workflows would keep moving into SaaS systems of record and
systems of engagement, that seat-linked pricing would remain a durable way to
capture value, and that net retention would be supported by more users, more
modules, and more workflow penetration. AI was largely treated as a product
tailwind because incumbents could package it into existing platforms. In
ServiceNow's case, the bullish version was that workflow data, process context,
integrations, and enterprise trust would let the company become an AI control
tower rather than an AI casualty.

That was the premium multiple. The issue was not whether ServiceNow was a weak
company. The issue was whether the old SaaS terminal-value bridge still applied.

## Breakpoint

The breakpoint was the market's confidence in SaaS terminal economics. Oliver
Wyman described the broader shift directly: investors had largely underwritten
AI as a software tailwind, but that framing was breaking down as agentic AI moved
from augmenting human workflows to executing them end-to-end. The same piece
said public markets had begun to reprice software risk because seat-based
pricing, expansion-driven growth, and durable product differentiation were being
called into question.

Deloitte made the same structural point from the operating side: as AI agents
pervade SaaS, the way businesses buy and use software may shift dramatically,
with subscriptions and seat-based licensing giving way to hybrid usage- and
outcome-based pricing. BCG similarly argued that AI agents are pushing buyers
toward outcome-based pricing and noted that 40% of surveyed IT buyers cite seat
reduction as the primary lever to lower software spending. Bain's framing is the
cleanest investment version: SaaS vendors need to figure out where AI enhances,
compresses, outshines, or cannibalizes their workflows.

ServiceNow is right in the blast radius of that debate because it is a workflow
company. The bull case says ServiceNow owns the process graph, business context,
permissions, integrations, and governance layer that agents need. The bear case
says an agent can increasingly execute across the workflow stack and turn parts
of the SaaS application layer into plumbing.

The Q1 release supplied the spark. ServiceNow beat Q1 guidance and raised
full-year subscription revenue guidance, so the current business was not
obviously broken. At the same time, the release showed a Q2 cRPO growth guide
below the Q1 reported rate, Armis added near-term margin headwinds, and
management cited delayed Middle East deal closings as a subscription-revenue
headwind. Those facts mattered, but they were not large enough by themselves to
explain the stock and sector move. They mattered because the market was already
looking for evidence that the SaaS model was less clean than previously assumed.

## Why ServiceNow Was the Rerating Vehicle

ServiceNow sits at the center of the AI-SaaS debate. It is workflow-heavy, so AI
agents can plausibly either strengthen it or attack parts of it. It has a
premium multiple, so terminal assumptions matter more than near-term beats. It
reports cRPO, RPO, large-customer count, AI ACV, and margins, which investors
use as proxies for durability. It also sells mission-critical enterprise
software whose value is partly embedded in routing, orchestration, tickets,
forms, approvals, and process automation. Those are exactly the areas agentic AI
claims to reshape.

The stock reaction was the market saying: "We no longer know whether the
terminal state is ServiceNow as the AI control tower, or ServiceNow as one of
many systems an AI control layer abstracts over."

That distinction is enormous. In the first case, ServiceNow deserves a premium
terminal multiple because AI increases workflow volume and platform strategic
importance. In the second case, the company may still grow, but the terminal
economics are less certain: lower seat growth, more outcome-based pricing
pressure, more AI compute cost, more competition from foundation-model vendors
and hyperscalers, and less confidence that product differentiation lasts.

## Signals and Questions

The pre-event signals were already visible in the broader software tape.
February 2026 software selloffs tied to Anthropic's Claude Cowork and other
agentic AI tools showed that the market was beginning to treat agents as
possible SaaS substitutes rather than pure feature upgrades. Buyer commentary
about reducing seats, consolidating software spend, and shifting toward
outcome-based pricing also mattered. For ServiceNow specifically, the key
question was whether AI ACV was clearly incremental or mainly defensive, whether
cRPO growth held up after adjusting for acquired contribution, and whether AI
compute and acquired product mix would pressure long-term gross margin and free
cash flow margin.

The open underwriting question is whether ServiceNow is the AI control tower or
a workflow application layer that external agents can increasingly bypass. The
answer depends on whether NOW can convert AI usage into incremental ACV without
cannibalizing seats or modules, whether customers expand ServiceNow because AI
increases workflow volume, and whether the company can move pricing toward value
and outcomes without losing the clean SaaS economics investors used to
underwrite.

## Case Pattern

This is the "terminal value broke before the P&L broke" pattern. The reported
quarter did not prove the business was impaired. The market was reacting to a
more dangerous possibility: the SaaS valuation framework itself might need a
lower terminal multiple if AI agents shift value away from seat-based workflow
applications and toward agentic orchestration, models, data platforms, or
infrastructure.

The lesson is that premium SaaS companies can beat near-term numbers and still
collapse if the market loses confidence in the terminal state. For these
businesses, the most important underwriting question is no longer just "Can
revenue grow 20% next year?" It is "Who captures workflow value when software
users become AI agents?"
