## Bear Projection

Terminal P/B anchor: 0.90x FY2036 book value per share. Revenue CAGR 2.0%, ROE 8%.

The bear case assumes ECB rate cuts compress NIM by 30-50 basis points simultaneously. The rate tailwind that drove NII expansion in 2023-2024 is completely reversed. Non-interest income decelerates from its recent 11% trajectory because economic weakness impacts fee and IB revenue. ROE drops to 8%, which is below cost of equity, so the market re-prices P/B back toward 1.0x. Credit costs rise — NPLs move from sub-2% toward 3% — and provisions erode retained earnings. Buybacks slow as management conserves capital. BVPS growth drops to ~6% annually (retention × ROE = 73% × 8% = 5.8% + ~1% buyback drag).

## Base Projection

Terminal P/B anchor: 1.40x FY2036 book value per share. Revenue CAGR 5.0%, ROE 12%.

The base case assumes ECB rate cuts are managed incrementally — 25 bps steps through 2026-2027 rather than aggressive reductions. NII plateaus or declines modestly (2-3% per year). Non-interest income continues to grow 7-10% annually, partially compensating. ROE holds in the 11-13% range, which justifies 1.3-1.5x P/B. Buybacks continue at ~3-4% annual share reduction, mechanically boosting BVPS even as absolute net income growth is modest. BVPS compounds at roughly 9% annually (ROE × retention + buyback effect = 12% × 73% + 1.6% ≈ 10.4%; rounded down to 9% for conservatism).

## Bull Projection

The bull case requires ROE to sustain above 13-15% through a combination of (a) non-interest income growth accelerating to 12%+ annually, (b) expense discipline continuing to compress the cost/income ratio, (c) Brazil expansion driving above-average ROE in the Latin American unit, and (d) buybacks continuing aggressively at below-book-value prices. At 15% ROE, the market re-rates P/B to 1.7-2.0x, consistent with the stock's peak valuation levels historically. BVPS compounds at ~14% annually (15% ROE × 73% retention + 1.1% buyback effect = 11.0% + buyback ~3% ≈ 14%).

## Key Assumptions And Extrapolation Notes

1. Revenue projections beyond FY2027 are extrapolated. Visible public estimates from StockAnalysis cover FY2026 (EUR 63.08B revenue, EUR 1.01 EPS) and FY2027 (EUR 68.01B revenue, EUR 1.22 EPS). FY2028+ is behind StockAnalysis Pro paywall and not visible. Years 3-10 use CAGR assumptions consistent with the model workbook scenarios.
2. Bank valuation methodology: P/B exit multiples are used rather than P/E or FCF multiples because bank cash flows are structurally different from industrial companies. Deposits are operating liabilities, loans are earning assets, and FCF as calculated by standard frameworks (OCF - Capex) produces meaningless results for banks. The model workbook explicitly states "Enterprise Value: N/A (bank — deposits not debt)" and uses P/B methodology throughout.
3. BVPS growth is calculated as ROE × retention rate + buyback effect. At current payout ratio of ~26.7%, the retention rate is ~73.3%. Buyback effect adds approximately 1-4% annually depending on scenario (bear: minimal, base: moderate, bull: aggressive).
4. FX assumption: EUR to USD at 1.10, consistent with June 2026 levels (SAN trading at $13.50 with EUR financials showing 14.54B shares × $13.50 = $196.3B / €178.5B ≈ 1.10).
5. Shares outstanding assumed to decline gradually in base/bull cases due to continued buyback programs. Stats page shows -3.64% YoY share reduction in FY2025. Bear case assumes buybacks slow to 1% or stop entirely as management conserves capital.
6. Net income margins assume effective tax rate near the FY2025 level of 25.28%. TTM tax rate spiked to 33.23% — assumed to be a one-time adjustment related to UK divestiture or deferred tax normalization.
7. Credit cost data is missing from StockAnalysis for FY2024-FY2025 (shows as blank/dash), which limits independent verification of NPL trajectories. NPL assumptions are inferred from sector benchmarks and are a key uncertainty.
8. The 10-year P/B ratios shown in the tables represent a gradual P/B normalization toward the exit anchor as ROE proves its durability over time. Early-year P/B can be slightly higher than the terminal anchor because the market front-runs the ROE sustainability story.
9. The displayed P/E column is intentionally omitted because P/E is less meaningful for banks than P/B — the earnings denominator includes non-cash provisions and rate-cyclicality that make P/E ratios misleading for cross-year comparison.
