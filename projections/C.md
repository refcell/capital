## Read

- The bear case is not a collapse. It assumes Citi remains a well-capitalized large bank, but fails to materially improve returns on equity. ROE stays at 7%, credit costs remain elevated at ~$10B, and NII growth slows meaningfully when the rate cycle turns. The stock barely grows in price despite moderate earnings and buybacks because the multiple compresses to 1.0x book value. This is entirely plausible if the turnaround proves mechanical rather than structural.
- The base case assumes Citi successfully transitions from restructuring mode to organic compounding. ROE improves to 11% — not spectacular but credible for a simplified, focused franchise. Revenue grows at 5% CAGR, driven by the Services franchise and moderate NII growth. At 1.30x book, the stock earns a 10.7% annualized price CAGR combined with the 1.7% dividend yield for a 12.4% total return. This requires the bank to genuinely earn 11% on equity, which is the main risk variable.
- The bull case is more about transformation than compounding. ROE of 14% puts Citi in the top tier of U.S. bank returns — that's a Goldman or JPM level of franchise quality. At 1.60x book and 7% revenue CAGR, the stock earns a 14.6% price CAGR. This is achievable but would require a genuine transformation of Citi from a turnaround name into a premium franchise.
- The terminal P/E ratios in the bull case decline to 6.0x by FY2036 because the earnings denominator grows faster than the price numerator in high-ROE/high-cash-return scenarios. The declining P/E is a feature of P/B-based valuation where book value compounds rapidly at high ROE.

## Key Assumptions

- Public estimate visibility for Citigroup is limited. The free StockAnalysis page shows 22 analysts averaging $146.93 with a $125-$170 range, but it does not show detailed FY2026 revenue or EPS consensus figures. The forward PE of 12.59x prices in about 38% EPS growth from the trailing $8.04, which implies FY2026 EPS of approximately $8.72. Revenue projections are extrapolated from recent trends: TTM revenue of $88.26B and FY2025 revenue of $85.2B suggest about 8-10% growth in FY2026, and the base case uses 5% CAGR as a sustainable rate after post-restructuring growth normalization.
- The local workbook uses P/B exit multiples for scenario modeling because that is the standard bank valuation approach rather than FCF multiples. Bank cash flows are structurally different from industrial companies — deposits, loan growth, and regulatory capital constraints make P/B the cleaner framework.
- `research/C.md` contributes the qualitative debate and medium-term valuation framing. The research report concludes watch rather than own because the 84% stock rally has already priced in the turnaround narrative, and ROE of 7.65% is below what would justify a higher multiple.
- Book value per share projections assume 1.79B shares outstanding in later years (modest dilution from potential capital raises over 5-10 years), with book value growing via retained earnings at the assumed ROE rate.
- The displayed P/E column uses net income per share = ROE × book value per share / shares outstanding, so the P/E naturally declines at high ROE/book-volatility. The P/E for a bank is less useful than P/B anyway.
- Revenue growth YoY for each row is the annualized revenue CAGR from the prior displayed fiscal year.
- Interim stock prices are walked back from each case's FY2036 terminal price to the current $139.83 price on a constant annualized price-return path, exactly as requested in `projections/README.md`.
