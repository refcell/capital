## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

SpaceX is now a public mega-cap. The first question is not whether the business is exceptional. It is. SpaceX has the dominant orbital launch franchise, the only LEO broadband network with real global scale, a subscriber base that has moved from startup economics to meaningful operating profit, and a founder-led culture that has repeatedly turned impossible-sounding engineering goals into working systems.

The harder question is whether the stock can work from a $2.1 trillion equity value one trading day after IPO. On 2025 numbers, the close was roughly 110x revenue, 311x adjusted EBITDA, and 18x pro forma book value. On a TTM basis through Q1 2026, revenue was about $19.301 billion and free cash flow was about negative $19.775 billion because $7.105 billion of operating cash flow was more than consumed by $26.880 billion of capital expenditures. Connectivity is already a strong business, but the public stock is not being priced on Starlink broadband alone. It is being priced on a portfolio of very large outcomes: scaled Starship, global Starlink Mobile, orbital AI compute, Grok/X monetization, Tesla/Intel chip collaboration, and possibly a Cursor acquisition.

The central debate is whether SpaceX is the only public company that can physically build the next infrastructure layer for space, connectivity, and AI, or whether public investors are paying today for several unproven capital-intensive futures while Starlink funds the burn.

### Current View

SPCX is a watchlist security, not a clean buy at $160.95. The business deserves serious study because SpaceX has a launch/constellation loop that no other public company can match: cheap reusable launch enables Starlink scale; Starlink creates internal launch demand and cash flow; scale improves launch and satellite manufacturing; Starship could widen the loop.

The problem is that the first-day close leaves little room for ordinary execution. My base-case fair value range is roughly $95 to $130 per share, my bear case is roughly $45 to $75, and my bull case can reach $180 to $230 if Starship scales, Starlink Mobile works, and AI revenue compounds without permanently consuming cash. That makes the stock investable only for someone who believes the bull case is more likely than the base case.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

SpaceX was founded in 2002 to reduce the cost of access to space and make humanity multiplanetary. The first public-company version is much broader than the historical private-company narrative. The final prospectus presents three reportable segments: Space, Connectivity, and AI.

The Space segment includes Falcon 9, Falcon Heavy, Dragon, customer launches, crew/cargo missions, and Starship development. The Connectivity segment is primarily Starlink, including consumer broadband, enterprise connectivity, government/Starshield, maritime, aviation, land mobility, fixed site, and Starlink Mobile. The AI segment came through the February 2026 xAI merger and includes Grok, X, terrestrial AI compute, and the planned orbital AI compute strategy.

This is important because the IPO did not sell only a launch company. It sold a vertically integrated infrastructure thesis: launch plus satellites plus broadband plus direct-to-cell plus AI models plus AI compute plus real-time X data plus future orbital data centers.

### Why The Company Matters

SpaceX matters because it controls scarce infrastructure. Launch is the choke point for orbital businesses. Broadband access in remote and mobile settings is a global need that terrestrial networks often cannot solve economically. AI compute is becoming a physical infrastructure race around power, chips, data centers, model quality, and distribution.

The company is trying to connect those choke points. Starship is the hinge. If Starship becomes a reliable, rapidly reusable heavy-lift vehicle, it can lower deployment costs for Starlink V3 satellites, mobile satellites, government payloads, lunar cargo, and eventually orbital compute. If Starship disappoints, many parts of the prospectus remain possible but become much more expensive and slower.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

SpaceX now makes money from three very different economic engines. The Space segment is launch and space transportation. Connectivity is Starlink subscription and enterprise/government connectivity. AI is a combination of X, Grok subscriptions, enterprise/government AI products, API access, advertising, and compute infrastructure.

| Revenue Stream | Description | 2025 Size | Economics | Risk |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Space | Launch, cargo, crew, government, commercial payloads, rideshare, Starship-adjacent services. | $4.086B revenue | Scarce capability; high strategic value; R&D-heavy. | Starship delays, launch mishaps, regulation, customer timing. |
| Connectivity | Starlink broadband, mobile, enterprise, aviation, maritime, government, Starshield. | $11.387B revenue | Recurring revenue, scale economics, strong segment EBITDA. | ARPU decline, spectrum, local licensing, orbital capacity, competition. |
| AI | Grok, X, subscriptions, advertising, enterprise/government AI, API, compute. | $3.201B revenue | Massive TAM if model/compute/distribution work. | Heavy capex, model competition, customer concentration, governance. |

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: reusable launch lowers access cost -> low-cost launch enables rapid Starlink deployment -> Starlink creates recurring broadband and enterprise cash flow -> internal demand raises launch cadence -> cadence improves reliability and cost -> Starship can increase payload capacity and lower cost per kg -> larger constellations enable direct-to-cell and orbital compute -> AI/Grok/X can consume the compute and distribution layer.

The weak link is capital intensity. The 2025 operating business generated $6.785 billion of operating cash flow, but total capital expenditures were $20.737 billion. SpaceX can plausibly build things others cannot. That does not automatically mean public shareholders earn attractive returns from the current price.

## 7. Forward Projections And Scenario Model

### Analyst Expectations

Street coverage is immature because the IPO just priced. Data providers already show early price targets around the first-day close, but underwriter research quiet periods and limited public history make consensus unreliable. I do not treat the first analyst target set as a real valuation anchor.

The useful expectations anchor is the reverse-engineering problem. At a roughly $2.045 trillion EV, the market is not paying for 2025 SpaceX. It is paying for 2030-plus SpaceX.

### Scenario Projections

This is a simple underwriting frame, not a precise forecast. It assumes SpaceX keeps investing aggressively and that free cash flow lags adjusted EBITDA in all scenarios because Starship, Starlink, spectrum, mobile, AI compute, and possible M&A require heavy capital.

| Scenario | 2030 Revenue | 2030 Adj. EBITDA | 2030 FCF | Key Assumption |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| Bear | $60B-$90B | $10B-$20B | Negative to $5B | Starship delayed, AI remains cash burn, Starlink ARPU compresses. |
| Base | $120B-$180B | $35B-$60B | $5B-$25B | Starlink scales, Starship improves deployment cost, AI grows but remains investment-heavy. |
| Bull | $250B-$400B | $100B-$160B | $40B-$90B | Starship works, Starlink Mobile scales, Grok/X/compute becomes a large profitable platform. |

### What The Current Price Implies

At $2.045 trillion of EV, discounting a 2030 terminal value back at 10% implies a 2030 enterprise value requirement of about $3.1 trillion before giving credit for interim free cash flow. If one applies a 20x to 25x 2030 adjusted EBITDA multiple, SpaceX needs roughly $125 billion to $160 billion of 2030 adjusted EBITDA to make the current price look normal on a terminal-multiple basis. That compares with $6.584 billion of adjusted EBITDA in 2025 and $1.127 billion in Q1 2026.

That math does not prove the stock is wrong. It does show the burden of proof. To justify $160.95 without relying on greater-fool multiple expansion, SpaceX probably needs Starlink to remain a world-class infrastructure business, Starship to work, and AI to become a large profitable platform rather than a permanent capital sink.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

### Management

Elon Musk is founder, CEO, CTO, and chairman. Gwynne Shotwell is president, COO, and a director. Bret Johnsen is CFO. The management team has an exceptional record in launch and satellite deployment, and the company culture is a real asset.

The governance risk is just as real. Class A shares have one vote each; Class B shares have 10 votes each. Class B holders elect a majority of the board. The prospectus cover says Musk will hold about 82.4% of voting power after the offering, while the beneficial ownership table shows a basis that can take his combined voting power higher depending on treatment. The practical conclusion is simple: public shareholders do not control SpaceX.

### Capital Allocation

SpaceX raised about $75.0 billion of gross proceeds in the IPO before the over-allotment option. The final prospectus shows $74.5 billion of proceeds before expenses, after underwriting discounts. That liquidity is a major advantage. It also creates room for extremely aggressive capital allocation.

Current and possible capital calls include Starship, next-generation Starlink satellites, Starlink Mobile spectrum and satellites, terrestrial AI data centers, orbital AI compute, the EchoStar spectrum transaction, the Tesla/Intel Terafab collaboration, and the Cursor option. The Cursor option alone contemplates a possible acquisition at a $60.0 billion implied equity value, payable in SpaceX Class A stock. At the IPO price, the prospectus example equals about 444 million Class A shares.

### Related Parties

The related-party section deserves more attention than in a normal industrial IPO. SpaceX discloses relationships with Tesla, The Boring Company, Musk Industries, a Musk-owned security company, Valor Equity Partners-affiliated equipment leases, and aircraft arrangements. The single most important disclosed item is the Valor equipment-lease cluster: three xAI equipment leases with about $20.2 billion of aggregate cash payments, guaranteed by SpaceX or a subsidiary, where Valor founder Antonio Gracias sits on the SpaceX board. The broader issue is the structural overlap of Musk-controlled or Musk-affiliated businesses and the scale of AI infrastructure decisions now inside SpaceX.

## 9. Ownership, Insider Activity, Short Interest, And Analyst Sentiment

### Ownership And Control

Musk is the only clearly relevant controlling holder. Class B voting control and controlled-company status are central to the investment case. Antonio Gracias / Valor appears as a large Class A holder but does not change the combined-vote control conclusion.

The June 11 Form 3 filings confirm the insider-heavy setup. Musk's filing shows large Class A holdings through trust/GRAT structures and substantial Class B / derivative exposure. Gwynne Shotwell, Bret Johnsen, Luke Nosek, Antonio Gracias / Valor and other directors also filed initial ownership statements. These filings are most useful as confirmation of control and overhang, not as a normal insider-buying signal.

### Lock-Ups And Float

Founder shares are locked until immediately after the close of trading on the 366th day after the prospectus date and are not subject to early release. Several other shareholders agreed to extended lock-ups through the second full trading day after Q2 2027 results, with staged early releases beginning after Q4 2026 results. The prospectus says founder and extended-lock-up shares together represent about 7.8 billion shares, or more than 63% of shares outstanding immediately prior to the offering.

This matters for two reasons. First, scarcity can support the stock in the early months. Second, staged unlocks can become real supply events if the stock continues to trade far above IPO price.

### Short Interest And Institutions

There is no meaningful official short-interest read yet. This is a first-weekend IPO. FINRA and Nasdaq short interest are reported after the relevant settlement-date cycle. Same-day short volume and borrow chatter are not the same as official short interest.

There is also no useful post-IPO 13F ownership base yet. Any pre-IPO `SPCX` institutional ownership data should be ignored because Nasdaq noted the ticker was reused for a new security.

### Analyst Sentiment

Initial analyst pages show price targets around the first-day price, but that is not a mature consensus. More useful public debate comes from valuation practitioners and IPO commentators. Aswath Damodaran's post-prospectus work reportedly framed value below the first-day public valuation. Other public bull views rely on very aggressive Starship, Starlink, and AI revenue scenarios.

## 11. Bull Case

The bull case is that SpaceX is one of the few companies where a very large public valuation can still be rational because it controls a physical infrastructure stack that gets more valuable as the world becomes more data-, compute-, and satellite-dependent.

In that version, Starlink keeps scaling from 10.3 million subscribers toward tens of millions of users while enterprise, aviation, maritime, government, and mobile services lift revenue mix. ARPU can decline at the consumer level and the company can still win if subscriber growth, lower terminal cost, better satellite capacity, and enterprise mix drive total gross profit higher.

Starship is the key operating lever. If Starship reaches frequent payload delivery and reuse, SpaceX can deploy V3 satellites and mobile satellites much faster than Falcon can, improving Starlink capacity and lowering the cost of network growth. That would make Starlink Mobile more realistic and could create the infrastructure base for future orbital compute.

AI is the biggest financial upside if it works. The company has a distribution layer through X, a model/product layer through Grok, and a physical compute layer through data centers and future orbital compute. If AI revenue compounds into hundreds of billions of dollars and capital intensity moderates, the current price can be justified. This is not the base case, but it is the path to a stock that works from $160.95.

## 12. Bear Case

The bear case is not that SpaceX is fake or low quality. The bear case is that the stock is already priced for too many wins.

At $160.95, the market is paying about $2.045 trillion of EV for a company with $18.674 billion of 2025 revenue, $6.584 billion of 2025 adjusted EBITDA, and about negative $19.775 billion of TTM FCF. Connectivity is excellent, but it cannot carry the entire valuation at ordinary broadband or telecom infrastructure multiples. The valuation needs Starship and AI to create new profit pools.

The second bear point is capital allocation. The 2025 and Q1 2026 financials show a company converting high operating ambition into very large capex. AI capex was $12.727 billion in 2025 and $7.723 billion in Q1 2026. If AI demand, model quality, or compute utilization disappoint, public shareholders could own a cash-burning AI infrastructure project funded by the profitable Starlink engine.

The third bear point is governance. Musk's control may be an operating advantage, but it is also a minority-shareholder risk. Related-party transactions, Tesla collaboration, Valor equipment leases, the potential Cursor acquisition, and X/Grok integration all require unusually high trust in board oversight and capital allocation.

## 13. Base Case

My base case is that SpaceX remains an extraordinary company but the stock needs time. Starlink keeps growing, but ARPU continues to fall as the subscriber base globalizes. Connectivity revenue and EBITDA compound, though not in a straight line. Space segment economics improve after Starship starts payload delivery, but Starship takes longer and costs more than bulls expect. AI grows, but profitability lags revenue because compute, chips, data centers, model training, and customer acquisition remain expensive.

That supports a very valuable company, but not necessarily a $2.1 trillion entry price. A base-case SOTP can get to roughly $1.2 trillion to $1.7 trillion of equity value if Starlink is worth $600 billion to $900 billion, Space/launch/Starship is worth $150 billion to $300 billion, AI/X/Grok is worth $300 billion to $500 billion, and net cash/other assets add value. That is still an exceptional outcome. It is also below or around the current price depending on the assumptions.

## 17. Variant Perception

The market may be treating SPCX as one story. It is really three clocks.

Starlink is the current business. It already has subscribers, revenue, operating income, and a plausible path to more scale. This is the part that can support a large but not unlimited valuation.

Starship is the execution hinge. It affects launch economics, Starlink capacity, mobile satellite service, lunar ambitions, and orbital compute. It is not a side project.

AI is the option. It can make the valuation rational if Grok/X/compute becomes a huge profitable platform. It can also destroy value if it keeps absorbing capital without clear returns. The bear case is not that SpaceX cannot build. It is that public investors are paying today for future projects whose economics are not yet visible.

## 18. What Would Change My Mind

I would become more constructive if SpaceX reports several quarters with:

| Evidence | Why It Would Matter |
|---|---|
| Starlink subscriber growth remains high while ARPU stabilizes. | Confirms the Connectivity engine is not just lower-price expansion. |
| Connectivity EBITDA grows faster than capex needed to sustain it. | Moves Starlink closer to clean owner earnings. |
| Starship delivers payloads, reuses hardware, and deploys V3 satellites economically. | Converts the biggest operational option into evidence. |
| AI revenue grows with improving gross margin and lower capex intensity. | Reduces the concern that xAI/X is a capital sink. |
| FCF turns positive at consolidated level while growth remains high. | Makes valuation less dependent on terminal assumptions. |
| Board disclosure around related-party capital allocation is unusually transparent. | Lowers governance discount. |

I would become more bearish if Starship timelines slip materially, AI capex rises faster than revenue, Starlink ARPU keeps falling without margin improvement, or the company uses stock for large ecosystem deals before proving public-company capital discipline.

## 19. Monitoring Checklist

| Metric / Event | Good Signal | Bad Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Starlink subscribers | Continued adds with low churn. | Adds slow sharply after global expansion. |
| Starlink ARPU | Stabilizes or mix improves. | Continues falling faster than cost per subscriber. |
| Connectivity adjusted EBITDA | Compounds with high incremental margins. | Margin compression from service costs or competition. |
| Space launches / mass to orbit | Cadence and mass continue rising. | Mishaps, regulatory delays, or cadence stalls. |
| Starship | Payload delivery, reuse, V3 deployment. | Repeated delays or high refurbishment cost. |
| AI revenue | Large, durable, diversified customers. | One-off compute revenue or cancellable deals. |
| AI capex | Capex per revenue dollar falls. | Capex accelerates ahead of demand. |
| OCF and FCF | OCF rises and FCF losses narrow. | FCF burn remains extreme. |
| Related-party disclosure | Clear economics and board process. | Opaque Tesla/Valor/Cursor/X transactions. |
| Lock-up supply | Absorbed without major price pressure. | Heavy selling into unlocks. |

## 20. Conclusion

SpaceX is probably the highest-quality infrastructure IPO in years and one of the few companies with a credible claim to creating several new markets. The launch moat is real. Starlink is real. The integration of launch, satellites, connectivity, and compute is strategically unique.

The stock is much less straightforward. At $160.95, public investors are paying not just for what SpaceX has proven, but for what SpaceX might become if Starship, Starlink Mobile, AI, X/Grok, and orbital compute all work. That may happen, but it is not a conservative base case. My current stance is watch / pass at the first-day close, with the stock becoming more interesting if fundamentals catch up, post-IPO supply creates a better price, or the next few quarters show that AI capex can convert into durable revenue rather than permanent burn.
