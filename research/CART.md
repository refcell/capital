## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Instacart is the dominant grocery delivery platform in North America, connecting over 80 retailers with consumers through an on-demand delivery and pickup network of roughly 100,000 part-time shoppers. The company went public in March 2023 at $30 per share and has since turned profitable while growing both total transaction volume and revenue. The stock has pulled back from highs near $53 to current levels around $40, down roughly 25% from peak.

The surface narrative is that Instacart had a great IPO run, burned cash for years, and is now finally profitable — but the market worries that revenue growth at ~3% TTM and GTV growth at ~6% signal a company that has peaked in terms of growth potential. The market is assigning a multiple that says "profitable but slow-growing commerce platform."

The real question underneath is whether Instacart is a terminal-growth delivery company (like DoorDash after its growth inflection) or a still-accelerating platform with years of take rate expansion, advertising monetization, and non-grocery category penetration ahead. The take rate is roughly 9% today — that leaves significant room for expansion if the company raises merchant fees, adds advertising (which is already growing at 16%), and expands into non-food retail.

The thesis works if the take rate expands from 9% toward 11-12% (which would be very modest compared to Amazon's effective take rate on its marketplace), the operating margin crosses into the mid-teens toward 20%, and the stock re-rates from a 1.9x revenue commerce platform to a 3-4x revenue software-like platform because it is fundamentally an advertising + merchant services business with high margins. It fails if revenue stays in single-digit growth, the take rate stalls at 9%, regulatory interference on merchant fees or pricing data increases, and consumer grocery delivery habits permanently revert to in-store shopping post-pandemic.

The company is approaching a critical inflection point. Instacart turned profitable in 2025, generated $427 million in net income on $3.74 billion of revenue, and has been aggressively repurchasing stock ($349 million in Q1 alone, $3.5 billion total authorization). The question is whether the market will eventually treat Instacart as a high-margin SaaS-like platform with recurring revenue characteristics, or whether it will remain categorized alongside DoorDash as a food-delivery business with limited margin expansion potential.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Instacart was founded in 2012 by Apoorva Mehta and Brandon Lewis. The original pitch was simple: consumers want the convenience of Amazon-style delivery for groceries, but every grocery retailer is a different platform with different inventory, logistics, and storefronts. Instead of building inventory like Amazon Fresh, Instacart built a network — connecting retailers directly to consumers through a platform of part-time "shoppers" who physically shop, pack, and deliver orders.

The early years were the classic burn-to-grow marketplace story. The company raised $1.6 billion in private capital, led by Andreessen Horowitz, Tiger Global, and others. Revenue was tiny and losses were massive — the company lost over $1 billion cumulatively before its 2023 IPO. The strategy was clear: grow GTV, expand the retailer network, build the shopper gig economy, and invest in technology and advertising.

Instacart went public in March 2023 at $30 per share via a direct listing, with a pre-IPO valuation of roughly $8.5 billion. It was one of the most anticipated IPOs of 2023 and one of the highest-profile platform listings. The stock rallied hard to a high near $53 in early 2024 before pulling back as the market questioned whether grocery delivery growth would sustain post-pandemic.

The financial trajectory has been a textbook marketplace arc from cash burn to profitability. The company went from a $2.55 billion revenue run rate at the IPO in 2022 to $3.74 billion in FY2025, while achieving meaningful profitability for the first time. Operating income improved from $57 million in FY2022 to $502 million in FY2025 — a massive improvement that reflects both scale and cost discipline. The company generated material free cash flow throughout this period and has been using excess capital for aggressive share repurchases ($3.5 billion authorization, $349 million in Q1 2026 alone).

GTV grew from $24.0 billion in FY2022 to $42.5 billion TTM — roughly 13% CAGR over the three-year period. That is solid growth, but below the expectations set during the IPO when the market anticipated explosive post-pandemic acceleration. Revenue growth has been even lower at roughly 9% CAGR because the take rate has been roughly flat near 9%. Revenue growth of +3% TTM is particularly anemic and likely reflects one-time impacts or mix shifts.

### Why The Company Matters

Grocery delivery sits at the intersection of the largest consumer spend category (groceries represent roughly $1.5 trillion annually in the US) and the fastest-growing commerce channel (online grocery has grown from a niche to a structural part of consumer habits). Instacart's network effect is real: every additional retailer makes the platform more valuable to consumers (wider selection), every additional consumer makes the platform more valuable to retailers (demand density), and every additional shopper makes the platform more efficient (capacity utilization).

Instacart matters because it is not just a delivery app — it has become the default commerce infrastructure layer for mid-market and large retailers. Over 80 retailers, including Walmart, Kroger, Albertsons, Target, and Tesco (in Canada), use Instacart's platform for their online ordering and delivery. Many of these retailers do not have competitive in-house options — building an equivalent delivery network would cost billions and take years.

The company is also an emerging advertising platform. Instacart serves roughly 90 billion ad impressions annually on its platform and in-store receipt placements. Advertising revenue is already ~$286 million TTM and growing at 16%. If Instacart can monetize even 15% of its platform through ads over the long term, that's $600 million+ of high-margin revenue on top of the core marketplace.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Instacart earns revenue from multiple sources that together represent a diversified platform model:

The core revenue stream is marketplace commissions from retailers. When a consumer orders groceries from a retailer through Instacart, the retailer pays Instacart a percentage of the order value. This is the "take rate" revenue and it accounts for the majority of the $3.86 billion TTM revenue. The take rate is roughly 9% — Instacart keeps $9 of every $100 of GTV. This is modest by e-commerce standards (Amazon's effective marketplace take rate is closer to 15-20%) and represents a key growth lever.

The second revenue stream is advertising. Instacart serves roughly 90 billion ad impressions annually, reaching both digital users and in-store shoppers through receipt placements and shelf placement data. Advertising revenue of $286 million TTM is growing at 16% and has high incremental margins because it sits on top of existing infrastructure with minimal variable cost.

A third revenue stream is Instacart's white-label platform technology. Many retailers pay Instacart to operate their entire online ordering and delivery operation. This includes not just the consumer-facing app but also backend logistics, inventory management, and fulfillment. This is essentially a SaaS + operations business that generates recurring revenue and deep retailer lock-in.

Instacart also partners with non-grocery retailers including Ace Hardware, Lush, 1-800-Flowers, Michaels, and HomeGoods to expand beyond food. These partnerships use the same platform and shopper network, creating incremental GTV without proportional cost increases.

The revenue model is high-quality. ~73% gross margin is exceptional for a commerce company — it reflects the fact that Instacart is a platform that takes a percentage of transactions rather than bearing inventory risk or physical store costs. The variable cost per order is the shopper payout, which is structured as a fixed fee plus tips. Importantly, tips go to shoppers, not to Instacart — so Instacart's economics improve as tips rise because consumers are absorbing more of the cost while Instacart's percentage revenue stays the same.

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: grow Gross Transaction Volume by adding consumers, retailers, and categories -> take a percentage of each transaction as commission revenue -> expand the take rate gradually without triggering merchant defection -> monetize the same infrastructure through advertising (near-zero marginal cost, high gross margin) -> reinvest in technology, marketing, and shopper incentives to drive further network effects -> convert high-margin revenue to operating income and free cash flow -> return capital via aggressive buybacks at depressed valuations.

The weak link is GTV growth and consumer frequency. Post-pandemic, grocery delivery frequency declined across the industry as consumers returned to in-store shopping. If order frequency declines permanently and GTV growth settles at 5-6%, the engine loses its primary growth driver. The engine can compensate through take rate expansion and advertising monetization, but these alone cannot sustain double-digit revenue growth if GTV is stagnating. The second weak link is retailer concentration and dependency — Walmart alone accounts for a significant share of Instacart's GTV, and if Walmart's in-house grocery delivery grows faster (which it likely will), Instacart's economics on that partnership could deteriorate.

### Competitive Position

Instacart's main competitors are Amazon Fresh / Whole Foods delivery, Walmart's own grocery delivery network, DoorDash (which entered grocery delivery in 2024 by acquiring Wolt), Kroger's in-house platform, and regional grocery delivery startups. The landscape is fragmented and competitive.

Instacart wins on retailer breadth and integration depth. Over 80 retailers use Instacart's platform, including the three largest US chains (Kroger, Walmart, Albertsons). This means consumers don't need to download separate apps for different stores — Instacart is the aggregator. That's a real competitive advantage that is hard to replicate because building retailer integrations takes time and trust.

Instacart loses on price sensitivity. Consumers who are willing to wait 30 minutes longer might choose a retailer's own app for lower fees. Walmart in particular is investing heavily in its own delivery infrastructure, which competes directly with Instacart on Walmart's own customers.

The competitive dynamic is nuanced. Instacart's moat is not that consumers prefer Instacart over other delivery apps — it's that retailers depend on Instacart's technology platform to operate their online business. Many of these retailers do not have equivalent in-house platforms (and wouldn't want to build them at the cost involved). That dependency creates switching costs that work in Instacart's favor even as consumer-level competition intensifies.

## 5. Financial Snapshot

Instacart's financial trajectory is a remarkable turnaround story. The company went from losing over $1 billion cumulatively before the IPO to generating $427 million in net profit on $3.74 billion of revenue in FY2025. Operating income improved from $57 million in FY2022 to $502 million in FY2025 — roughly 24% CAGR improvement. Gross margins have been consistently strong at 73-75%, which is exceptional for commerce and reflects the platform economics of taking a percentage of transactions rather than bearing inventory risk.

Revenue has grown from $2.55 billion in FY2022 to $3.74 billion in FY2025 — roughly 14% CAGR. GTV has grown faster at 13% CAGR from $24.0 billion to $42.5 billion. Take rate has been roughly stable near 9%, meaning revenue growth has tracked GTV growth rather than outpacing it. For the stock to generate outsized returns, the take rate needs to expand.

One number to remember: $3.86 billion revenue, 73% gross margin, 15% operating margin, 12.4% FCF margin, $480 million FCF, 9.1% take rate on $42.5 billion GTV. This is not the profile of a dying delivery business — it is a profitable, cash-generative platform with room for multiple expansion.

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Reported earnings are clean and high-quality. Instacart generates real, cash-backed net income with minimal accounting adjustments. The ~73% gross margin is structural, not a result of cost-shifting or accounting manipulation. Free cash flow tracks net income closely because capex is modest relative to revenue — the company is a platform with software-like infrastructure needs, not a warehouse-and-logistics heavy asset owner.

SBC (stock-based compensation) is the main adjustment item, but Instacart's SBC is relatively manageable as a percentage of revenue compared to other tech companies. The company does not have the massive pre-IPO option pools that plague many IPOs, and the post-IPO grants appear reasonable. The FCF margin of 12.4% is meaningful because it reflects actual cash that belongs to shareholders after all operating expenses, capex, and SBC.

The revenue recognition is straightforward. Instacart recognizes commission revenue as transactions occur and advertising revenue as impressions are served. There are no major deferred revenue items or contract accounting complexities that obscure the quality of earnings. The operating cash flow of $600 million TTM vs net income of $423 million is a good sign — operating cash flow exceeds net income because of depreciation and timing differences, which means earnings quality is high.

One adjustment worth noting: Instacart reports its commission and advertising revenue on a gross basis (it keeps the entire take from merchants and advertising), which means the $3.86 billion revenue figure is meaningful and not just gross-to-net pass-through. This is different from some marketplaces that report revenue on a net basis (only the commission after paying suppliers), which would make revenue appear smaller but margins appear higher.

Another quality note: the company has successfully reduced debt from $384 million in FY2022 to $50 million in Q1 FY2026 — a clear sign of capital discipline and cash generation capability.

Multiple analysts cover CART with consensus estimates reflecting moderate growth expectations. ### Forecast Logic

The bear case assumes GTV growth stagnates at 4-6%, take rate fails to expand beyond 9-9.5%, and revenue CAGR settles at 5-6%. Operating margin holds at 14-15% (no further expansion possible because the company can't raise fees without losing merchants), and advertising growth decelerates as CPM compression or impression growth slows. In this scenario, the stock justifies roughly 15x earnings at most, implying about $27 per share. This is a downside — but the bear case is partially hedged by the $1.5B in cash and the active buyback program.

The base case assumes GTV stabilizes at 8-10% growth, take rate expands from 9% toward 10.5% over the next three years, and revenue CAGR reaches 12-14%. Operating margin expands from 14.7% to 18% as the platform scales advertising and non-grocery revenue (which are higher-margin). FCF reaches $700M+ on $5B+ revenue. At 18-20x earnings on 230 shares, value per share is $56-65.

The bull case requires advertising monetization to accelerate beyond 16%, non-grocery categories to meaningfully scale GTV, and take rate expansion to reach 11-12%. At 20-22x earnings on $1B+ net income, the per-share value exceeds $90.

Instacart checks the boxes on most fronts: GTV growth is stable at 6%, take rate is at 9% with room to expand, advertising is growing fast at 16%, operating leverage is clear (14.7% operating margin on $3.86B revenue), and share count is declining from 238M to 235M. The one metric that needs to change direction is revenue growth — it needs to accelerate above 8% for the base case to hold.

The board appears aligned with long-term shareholder value rather than short-term earnings management. The buyback authorization size is ambitious relative to market cap, suggesting management genuinely believes the stock is undervalued.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

The bull case is that Instacart is not a delivery company — it is an advertising and merchant-services platform with $42.5 billion of gross transaction volume flowing through it. At a 9% take rate, there is significant runway for expansion. If the take rate reaches 11-12% (still below Amazon's effective rate), revenue grows 25-33% even if GTV stays flat. Advertising revenue of $286 million growing at 16% represents a 90%+ gross margin business on top of the core marketplace.

Moreover, the operating margin is 14.7% and improving — but at this scale, every 1 point of operating margin expansion is a massive multiple of net income. Going from 14.7% to 18% operating margin is a 23% improvement in net earnings. The market hasn't priced in this leverage yet.

### What Bears Believe

The bear case is that grocery delivery is a structurally capped business. Consumer frequency declined post-pandemic and will not recover because the convenience premium of home delivery competed against in-store shopping that people ultimately prefer. Walmart is building its own delivery infrastructure and competes directly. DoorDash acquired Wolt and is entering grocery. Amazon has Whole Foods and Amazon Fresh. The competitive landscape means Instacart cannot meaningfully raise fees without losing transaction volume.

The +3% revenue growth TTM is the scariest number. If revenue is only growing 3% while GTV grows 6%, the take rate is actually declining. At 73% gross margin, you would think operating leverage could keep improving — but if revenue is flat, OpEx is essentially fixed, so margin improvement comes from headcount cuts rather than genuine scaling, which is finite.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The market appears to be focused on revenue growth deceleration and the grocery delivery narrative. The more important issue may be the advertising monetization trajectory. Instacart generated 90 billion ad impressions and $286 million in ad revenue TTM — that's roughly 3 cents per 1,000 impressions (CPM). If CPM normalizes to even $5 (which is still below the industry average), ad revenue could reach $450M+ on the same impression base. That's $164 million in incremental, 90%+ gross margin revenue. The market is not pricing this possibility.

Additionally, the non-grocery expansion into hardware, home goods, and specialty retail has potential to add significant GTV volume without proportional cost. If Instacart can become the go-to on-demand delivery platform for non-food categories, it effectively becomes a hybrid of DoorDash (non-food delivery) and Grubhub (food delivery) with a deeper retailer network than either.

## 10. Bull Case

If take rate expands from 9% to 11% while GTV maintains 8-10% growth, and operating margin reaches 20% as the platform scales, then revenue of $6-7 billion with $1.2-1.4 billion in operating income commands a 18-20x EV/Operating income multiple as a profitable advertising + commerce platform, implying $75-90 per share. The re-rate from "delivery company" to "advertising platform" is the inflection point — once advertising becomes a meaningful percentage of total revenue (30%+), the stock stops being compared to DoorDash and starts being compared to digital advertising exchanges. The first evidence should show up in quarterly revenue growth re-acceleration above 8% for two consecutive quarters.

## 11. Bear Case

If revenue growth stays at 3-5% and operating margin caps at 14-15% because the company cannot raise fees without losing merchants, then the stock is worth roughly 15-16x earnings on $550-600 million of net income, or roughly $35-40 per share — essentially where it is today. If competitive pressure from Walmart and DoorDash worsens the merchant economics further, the multiple compresses below 15x and the stock goes lower. The first evidence should show up in declining GTV or contracting take rate.

## 12. Base Case

The most likely scenario is that GTV growth stabilizes at 6-8%, take rate expands modestly toward 10-10.5% over the next two years, and operating margin reaches 16-18% as advertising scales and the company continues to benefit from operating leverage on a larger base. Revenue CAGR of 10% with $6+ billion in revenue and ~$650M in net income at 18x earnings implies roughly $55 per share, which is 38% upside from $39.80. The buyback program accelerates per-share value even if headline revenue stalls — the 2% reduction in shares from 240M to 235M compounds over time.

## 18. Conclusion

Instacart is fundamentally a different business today than it was at the IPO. The company has transformed from a cash-burning marketplace with $57M in operating income into a profitable platform generating $450M+ in operating income and $480M+ in free cash flow. The operating margin expanded from 2.2% to 14.7% — a remarkable improvement that proves the model works at scale.

At $39.80, the stock is not cheap by historical SaaS standards — it trades at roughly 2.4x revenue, 22x earnings, and 16x EV/FCF. However, those multiples are fair for a profitable platform with $42.5 billion in gross transaction volume, a 9% take rate with room to expand, and a 90%+ gross margin advertising business growing at 16%. The question is whether the market will eventually re-rate Instacart from a "grocery delivery service" multiple to an "advertising + commerce platform" multiple.

The stock works if take rate expands above 10%, revenue re-accelerates above 8%, and operating margin reaches 18%+ as the platform scales advertising and non-grocery categories. It fails if GTV growth and revenue stay in single digits, take rate stalls at 9%, and the market permanently views this as a mature, slow-growth delivery company. My base-case fair value is roughly $58 per share, implying 46% upside. I would revisit the view when Q2 FY2026 earnings reveal the direction of revenue growth, take rate trajectory, and ad monetization pace.
