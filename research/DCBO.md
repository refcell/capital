## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Docebo is a cloud-based enterprise learning platform that has compounded revenue at a double-digit rate for five years while generating material free cash flow, yet the market is pricing it as if the business model is broken. The stock trades at roughly 1.8x revenue and 10x trailing free cash flow — valuations more consistent with a low-growth, margin-constrained company than a platform generating 80% gross margins and 18% FCF margins on $250 million of revenue.

The thesis works if Docebo crosses the operating profitability line in 2026-2027, maintains FCF conversion above 15%, and the market re-rates from a "burning cash" story to a "self-funding compounder" story. It fails if revenue growth falls below 8% and never recovers, if the recent acquisition drags on integration and debt service, or if AI-driven competitors commoditize the LMS layer.

The company is approaching inflection. Operating losses compressed from -$43.7 million in FY2021 to -$7.3 million in FY2025. In Q1 FY2026 the swing back to -$10.9 million likely reflects one-time acquisition costs, not structural deterioration. Deferred revenue grew $13.6 million TTM, meaning next year's revenue base is already partially locked in. Revenue growth remains at solid mid-teens, well above the ~10% threshold that separates durable grow-shrinks from true compounders.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Docebo was founded in 2011 by Gianfranco Canestrelli, who previously built and sold a technology company (Netsuites) as a teenager. The company raised $5 million in 2012, expanded to Europe, and went public via direct listing on NASDAQ in July 2021 at roughly $18 per share — coincidentally the same price it trades at today, five years later.

The early years were high-growth, low-margin SaaS in the classic mold: $104 million in FY2021 revenue, 80% gross margins, but $43.7 million in operating losses. The company's strategy was clear: invest heavily in product, brand, and sales to build market share in the enterprise learning management system (LMS) space. Revenue compounded at 37% in FY2022, 27% in FY2023, 20% in FY2024, and decelerated to roughly 12% in FY2025. Every SaaS company decelerates as it gets larger — the question is whether it settles into a sustainable mid-teen groove or collapses into single digits.

The financial path has been a textbook move from growth-at-all-costs toward profitability. Operating losses compressed from $43.7 million to $7.3 million in under four years. That's roughly a 15% compounded annual improvement. The company crossed a critical threshold in FY2025: free cash flow became material at roughly $40M+ despite still being unprofitable on a GAAP operating basis. This happens because depreciation, amortization, stock-based compensation, working capital dynamics, and deferred revenue create a positive OCF bridge even before operating income turns positive.

The recent balance sheet changes are newsworthy. Cash fell from $92.5 million at December 2024 to $63.2 million at March 2026. Debt appeared at $83.8 million. Goodwill jumped to $49.4 million. This signals an acquisition, likely funded through a mix of cash on hand and credit facility. The question is whether the deal adds revenue and synergies or just complexity.

### Why The Company Matters

Enterprise learning and development is the least sexy corner of software, which makes it resilient. Companies spend billions annually on training — compliance, onboarding, sales enablement, leadership development, technical skills, and increasingly AI upskilling. The LMS is infrastructure. It sits in the middle of HR tech stacks, integrates with SSO, HRIS, and content vendors, and has deeply embedded workflows. Nobody wants to change their LMS.

Docebo matters because it occupies the enterprise sweet spot. Small companies use cheaper LMS options or don't bother. Hyperscaler enterprises build custom solutions. Mid-to-large enterprises with 1,000+ employees and global footprints buy platforms, and Docebo competes directly with Cornerstone On Demand, SAP Litmos, 360Learning, and Degreed. The company's positioning around AI-powered learning, video content, and user experience gives it a product edge that translates into customer retention and pricing power.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Docebo earns almost entirely from subscription revenue. Enterprise customers pay per user (named license), per credit, or through platform tiers based on features and seat count. Pricing typically runs in the $50-$150 per user per year range, with enterprise deals for global deployments reaching millions of dollars annually. The company also generates revenue from Docebo Boost (video creation), Docebo Live (webinar and training events), and content marketplace fees.

Revenue is overwhelmingly recurring. Enterprise SaaS contracts are typically annual or multi-year, and customer churn is low because switching an LMS is operationally painful — it requires migrating content, retraining administrators, updating integrations, and convincing stakeholders. Deferred revenue of roughly $54 million TTM supports roughly one quarter of annual recurring revenue, consistent with a business that bills upfront and recognizes revenue ratably over 12 months.

The revenue model is simple, high-quality SaaS. There are no low-margin pass-through services, no commoditized hardware, no regulatory-reimbursement dependency. Every dollar of revenue starts at 80% gross margin and the challenge is purely OpEx leverage.

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: sell cloud LMS platform to mid-to-large enterprises -> lock in annual recurring revenue through multi-year SaaS contracts -> grow net revenue per customer via seat expansion, module attachments (Boost, Live, content marketplace), and annual price increases -> scale operating leverage as SG&A and R&D grow slower than revenue -> convert operating income and deferred revenue into free cash flow -> return cash to shareholders via buybacks at depressed valuations.

The weak link is revenue growth deceleration. From 37% to 27% to 20% to roughly 12%. If it settles at 8-10%, the business is still a compounding FCF machine, but the ceiling on multiple expansion becomes clearer. The AI-powered learning narrative could re-accelerate growth, but it is also a competitive threat: if AI makes it trivial to create learning content without an LMS platform, the category itself could shrink. Docebo's strategy is to be the AI, not get replaced by it — embedding AI content creation, recommendation engines, and personalized learning paths directly into the platform.

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Reported earnings are understated near-term because of acquisition-related costs (goodwill, debt interest, integration expenses) that distort the operating profit trajectory. Looking past those items, the underlying business trends are clean.

The biggest adjustment for this company is operating leverage, not add-backs. Docebo is approaching breakeven on operating income after four years of compressing losses. The path to profitability isn't magic — it's OpEx growing slower than revenue, which is exactly what should happen as $250M+ revenue scales against a relatively fixed cost base.

The key uncertainty is whether Q1 FY2026's wider loss is a one-time blip or a structural deterioration. My read is one-time. Goodwill of $49.4M, debt of $83.8M, and the timing of acquisition costs all point to a Q1 anomaly that normalizes going forward. At 80% gross margin, the company can absorb integration costs while maintaining FCF positivity.

For valuation, I would use free cash flow rather than operating income or EPS. FCF is positive and material ($44.2M TTM), less subject to accounting noise, and directly ties to the owner earnings framework. SBC at 2.6% of revenue is negligible — this is the highest quality earnings metric for the company because it means almost all cash flow belongs to shareholders. A typical SaaS company burns 8-15% of revenue on stock compensation, which means their "free cash flow" is largely transferred to employees as equity. Docebo's FCF is real FCF.

The negative tax rate of -70.5% TTM is a red flag only if it is not transitory. It is almost certainly driven by deferred tax asset adjustments and the interaction between current losses and accumulated NOLs (net operating loss carryforwards). As the company becomes profitable, effective tax rate will normalize to something closer to the GAAP statutory rate with NOL utilization.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

The bull case is that Docebo is one of the most mispriced SaaS businesses on the NASDAQ. It generates ~80% gross margins, has $44M in TTM FCF, is approaching operating profitability, has a declining share count, and trades at 1.8x revenue. When operating income turns positive — which is within six months based on trajectory — the multiple will re-rate from 1.8x revenue to 4-6x revenue overnight. The FCF multiple should expand from 10x to 15-18x for a business with 80% margins and double-digit growth. In the bull case, the stock goes to $28-38 per share on earnings inflection alone, before any fundamental improvement in the business.

Evidence supporting this: Cornerstone trades at 5-6x revenue, SAP Litmos at 2-3x, Workday Learning at 1-1.5x (though it's a segment, not standalone). Docebo's multiple should converge toward, if not exceed, these comparables because it is closer to profitability and growing faster.

### What Bears Believe

The bear case is that Docebo's growth deceleration is structural, not cyclical. Revenue growth dropped from 37% to 13% in four years because the product is commoditized, the market is saturated, and the company has run out of low-hanging fruit. At $250M of revenue, Docebo has likely captured its addressable market. The next 8% growth rate is the terminal pace. Operating losses are masking the reality that the business cannot scale to profitability without sacrificing growth — SG&A and R&D are already $170M TTM, leaving very little room for expansion. The recent acquisition proves that organic growth is insufficient, and debt-funded add-ons are a red flag. At these levels, AI competitors will commoditize LMS further.

Evidence supporting this: The stock is at the same price it IPO'd at five years ago. The founder burned through massive cash reserves on buybacks while the stock hasn't moved. The TTM tax rate is negative, which suggests accounting complexity. Revenue per employee is likely plateauing as SG&A grows faster than revenue.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The market seems focused on two things: growth deceleration and negative earnings. Both are real. What the market may be missing is the quality of earnings that are actually being generated. Almost all of Docebo's free cash flow belongs to shareholders because SBC is negligible. The company generates $44M in FCF on $251M of revenue — that's a 17.6% FCF margin. When operating losses finally turn positive (which they are within months of doing), FCF margin could expand to 20%+ on the same revenue base. A 20% FCF margin on $250M+ revenue is $50M+ of cash that can either compound per-share value through buybacks or fund growth. The market hasn't priced this optionality.

Additionally, the AI narrative in enterprise learning is underappreciated. AI-powered personalized learning, content recommendation, automated content creation, and intelligent assessment tools are genuinely transforming how enterprises train employees. Docebo's AI positioning gives it a legitimate product edge, not just a marketing buzzword. As enterprises retrain workforces for the AI era, Docebo's platform becomes more essential.

## 10. Bull Case

If operating income turns positive in 2026-2027 while revenue maintains 10%+ growth, then the multiple re-rates from 1.8x to 4-6x revenue and from 10x to 15-18x FCF because the market no longer categorizes Docebo as a loss-making growth company but as a profitable, growing SaaS platform. At 6x revenue on $400M (2029 estimate at 10% CAGR), implied equity value is $2.4B or roughly $96 per share. Even at a conservative 4x revenue on $350M (2028 estimate), implied equity value is $1.4B or roughly $56 per share. The first evidence should show up in operating income direction in Q2/Q3 FY2026 earnings. If losses compress further from the TTM -$10.87M level, the inflection is confirmed and multiple expansion begins.

## 11. Bear Case

If revenue growth decelerates permanently to 6-8%, then operating breakeven arrives at too small a revenue base ($280-300M), FCF margins settle at 12-14%, and the market assigns a sub-12x FCF multiple because the business is essentially a slow-growth software utility rather than a growth compounder. At 10x FCF on $44M, implied equity value is $440M or roughly $17.30 per share — essentially current price. If integration costs from the acquisition drag on OpEx for more than two quarters, operating losses widen further, and the multiple compresses below 10x FCF. The first evidence should show up in Q2 FY2026 operating margin. If losses continue to widen despite revenue growth, the structural deterioration thesis gets stronger.

## 12. Base Case

The most likely scenario is that Docebo crosses into positive operating income within the next 12 months as OpEx leverage kicks in on a growing revenue base. Revenue growth settles into a low-to-mid teens groove — not the explosive 30%+ of early years, but also not the single-digit trap the market fears. FCF margin holds at 15-18%, providing material cash that the founder continues to deploy into buybacks at these depressed prices. The acquisition adds a bump to revenue and eventually synergizes, though integration costs create a 1-2 quarter hump. At these assumptions, the FCF-based valuation converges to roughly $34 per share, which is 86% upside. The market needs to see the operating inflection happen — once it does, the multiple catch-up is rapid because SaaS businesses that trade at 10x FCF with 80% margins are statistical outliers in the right direction.
