## Executive Summary

Hims & Hers is one of the more interesting and more difficult public consumer-health debates. The company has built a real platform: a direct-to-consumer healthcare brand, digital intake and provider network, recurring subscriptions, owned and partner pharmacy fulfillment, and growing category breadth across sexual health, hair loss, dermatology, mental health, weight loss, hormone health, labs, and international markets. FY2025 revenue grew 59% to $2.35 billion, subscribers grew to 2.5 million, adjusted EBITDA reached $318 million, and the company is guiding to $2.7 billion to $2.9 billion of FY2026 revenue.

The stock is controversial because the 2024-2025 breakout coincided with compounded GLP-1 demand. Revenue grew much faster than subscribers in 2025: subscribers rose 13%, while monthly revenue per average subscriber rose 28% to $83. That can be good mix, better personalization, and higher-value chronic care. It can also mean that normalized revenue and margin are more exposed to GLP-1 pricing, regulatory limits, and customer retention than the headline platform story suggests.

The bull case is that Hims & Hers used GLP-1s as an accelerant, not a one-off arbitrage. In this view, the company has proven it can acquire customers, launch new categories quickly, personalize treatment plans, and move from stigmatized low-ticket categories into larger chronic-care pools. The March 2026 Novo Nordisk pivot, where Hims & Hers said it would focus U.S. weight loss on FDA-approved GLP-1s and stop advertising compounded GLP-1 offerings, could reduce regulatory risk while preserving access to the category.

The bear case is sharper than "telehealth is competitive." HIMS may be a high-beta DTC pharmacy and marketing business whose best growth came from a shortage-era regulatory opening in compounded semaglutide. FDA warning letters, an FDA statement directly naming the company, HHS/DOJ referral language, an SEC investigation, Novo litigation, high insider selling, controlled-company governance, thin free cash flow, large SBC, and a $1.0 billion convertible note all matter. Adjusted EBITDA was $318 million in 2025, but free cash flow was only $57 million after heavy fulfillment, pharmacy, software, and intangible investment.

My read is that HIMS is a real company but not an obviously cheap stock at $28.27. The base case is roughly fair value: if revenue compounds at a low-to-mid teens rate for a long time and true owner free cash flow margins reach the mid-teens, the stock can be worth around the current price. The bull case can be much higher if the company approaches its $6.5 billion 2030 revenue roadmap and converts EBITDA into cash. The bear case is severe if GLP-1 economics normalize downward, regulatory costs rise, and adjusted EBITDA fails to become free cash flow.

## Company Overview

Hims & Hers is a consumer-first health and wellness platform. The company connects customers with licensed healthcare professionals, supports digital prescriptions, and fulfills many prescriptions through owned or partner pharmacies. The current platform covers sexual health, hair loss, hormone health, weight loss, dermatology, mental health, and lab testing.

The company began with relatively simple, stigmatized categories where customers value privacy and convenience: erectile dysfunction, hair loss, dermatology, and related wellness products. Over time it expanded into higher-ARPU and more chronic categories, especially weight loss and GLP-1 access, as well as hormone health, menopause, testosterone, labs, and international telehealth through acquisitions.

HIMS is mostly a cash-pay model. In the United States and most markets, customers pay the company directly rather than using third-party reimbursement. That reduces exposure to payer billing complexity and healthcare reimbursement regulation, but it also means the business is exposed to consumer affordability, paid marketing efficiency, subscription friction, and category-level price competition.

The company has also become more vertically integrated. It owns pharmacies, uses partner pharmacies, acquired a 503B outsourcing facility and other pharmaceutical infrastructure, and is investing heavily in fulfillment, pharmacy, software, and lab capabilities. That should improve speed, margin control, personalization, and category-launch capability, but it also increases regulatory, quality, capex, and operational risk.

## Business Model

The core formula is simple:

`Subscribers x monthly revenue per average subscriber x retention - marketing, fulfillment, clinical, pharmacy, and corporate costs`

Hims & Hers makes money by selling products and services through its websites and mobile applications, mostly on a recurring subscription basis. Revenue includes product access, consultation services, medication adjustments where applicable, post-consultation support, and fulfillment. The company also has wholesale revenue from non-prescription products sold through retailers and third-party platforms, but wholesale is now small and less strategically important.

The subscription model is central. Subscribers choose shipment cadences or treatment terms that can range from 30 days to much longer periods, depending on the offering. Longer cadences can improve unit economics because they reduce shipment frequency and fulfillment costs. Deferred revenue, cadence mix, and cancellation behavior are therefore important cash-flow and retention indicators.

HIMS historically reported Online Revenue and Wholesale Revenue. In the FY2025 10-K, management said that because of international expansion it is shifting disclosure toward United States Revenue and Rest of the World Revenue, and expects Online Revenue and Wholesale Revenue to become less relevant. That disclosure change is reasonable given acquisitions, but it also makes category-level visibility more important because investors still need to understand GLP-1, personalized treatments, and mature categories separately.

## Products and Technology

The product portfolio has three broad layers:

| Layer | Examples | Economic role |
|---|---|---|
| Mature recurring categories | ED, hair loss, dermatology, sexual health | Lower-ticket, recurring, brand-building, relatively resilient. |
| Personalized / higher-ARPU categories | Weight loss, GLP-1s, hormone health, menopause, testosterone, personalized dermatology | Larger revenue opportunity but more regulatory, supply, and margin risk. |
| Platform extensions | Labs, apps, education, community, international telehealth, retail/wholesale products | Cross-sell, retention, market expansion, and brand extension. |

The technology story is not that HIMS has a hard technical monopoly. The platform value is operational: condition-specific funnels, customer acquisition data, digital intake, provider workflows, pharmacy fulfillment, product personalization, subscription management, and customer service. These capabilities are difficult to build at scale, but they are not impossible for a well-funded competitor to replicate.

The company says personalized offerings represented more than 70% of U.S. revenue in 2025, up from roughly half in 2024. More than 1.6 million subscribers used a personalized solution at year-end 2025. That is a meaningful sign that the business is moving beyond generic DTC pharmacy into customized care plans. It is also a reason to watch regulatory and clinical quality carefully, because many personalized treatments can include compounded formulations.

## Industry and Competitive Position

HIMS sits at the intersection of telehealth, digital pharmacy, consumer wellness, cash-pay medicine, and direct-to-consumer healthcare marketing. The relevant competition differs by category.

| Competitor type | Examples | HIMS advantage | HIMS disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Category-specific DTC platforms | Ro, LifeMD, WeightWatchers/Sequence, specialty clinics | Brand scale, multiple categories, owned fulfillment. | Some rivals may be deeper in specific areas such as obesity or insurance navigation. |
| Large technology / retail health platforms | Amazon One Medical, Amazon Pharmacy, GoodRx | Condition-specific brand and subscription funnel. | Amazon has price transparency, logistics, consumer reach, and pharmacy scale. |
| Traditional healthcare | PCPs, specialists, local pharmacies, cash-pay clinics | Convenience, privacy, transparent pricing, home delivery. | Traditional providers have clinical trust, reimbursement relationships, and broader medical context. |
| Drug manufacturers / manufacturer DTC channels | Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly, other branded-drug programs | HIMS can aggregate demand and provide front-end care/navigation. | Manufacturers own the branded product economics and can commoditize distributors. |

The industry backdrop is attractive because many routine healthcare interactions are moving online. Customers are increasingly willing to use virtual care for repeat conditions, prescriptions, and wellness products. At the same time, trust, regulation, clinical quality, and price transparency matter more as the company moves into obesity, hormone health, and lab-enabled care.

The most direct threat is not a single competitor. It is the combination of high paid-media competition, Amazon-like price pressure, manufacturer direct access, regulatory limits on compounding, and lower-margin branded drug distribution. HIMS has to keep proving that its brand and care flow create enough value to retain customers after the initial acquisition event.

### Cash Flow and Owner Earnings

The cleanest caution in the financials is free cash flow. HIMS generated $300.0 million of operating cash flow in 2025, but free cash flow was only $57.4 million because purchases of property, equipment, intangible assets, website development, and internal-use software totaled $242.6 million.

Some of this investment may be growth capex: fulfillment facilities, pharmacy capacity, lab infrastructure, and software that should support future scale. But it is not free. If the business needs ongoing pharmacy, manufacturing, sterile compounding, lab, fulfillment, and international infrastructure investment to sustain growth, adjusted EBITDA will overstate owner earnings.

SBC also matters. Stock-based compensation was $135.2 million in 2025, more than GAAP net income of $128.4 million. Diluted weighted-average shares rose from 236.8 million in 2024 to 258.2 million in 2025. The company repurchased some stock, but dilution and converts still belong in the valuation.

### Balance Sheet

At 2025 year-end, HIMS had $228.6 million of cash and $700.1 million of available-for-sale securities, for $928.8 million of cash and securities. The company also had $972.6 million carrying value of convertible senior notes. The notes are 0% convertible senior notes due 2030, with an initial conversion price around $70.67 per share. The company also entered capped-call transactions, but those only limit dilution up to the capped-call economics.

The headline balance sheet is not distressed. The company is roughly net debt neutral if all AFS securities are credited. The more important question is forward capital allocation: 2025 acquisitions, the proposed Eucalyptus acquisition, earn-outs, deferred payments, capex, and potential share settlement can all consume cash or create dilution.

## Management, Governance, and Capital Allocation

Andrew Dudum is co-founder, CEO, and chair. HIMS is effectively founder-controlled. The 2026 proxy describes the company as a NYSE controlled company because Dudum controls more than 50% of voting power. The Class V stock carries 175 votes per share, and proxy disclosures indicate Dudum controls the overwhelming majority of total voting power.

Founder control can be a positive if it supports long-term category expansion and fast execution. It can be a negative if minority shareholders need governance discipline around regulatory risk, capital allocation, SBC, acquisition structure, or disclosure. In this case, governance should be treated as a real discount factor because the business is already regulatory-sensitive.

Capital allocation has become more aggressive:

| Area | Recent activity | Investor question |

| Acquisitions | Zava, Medici, YourBio, and proposed Eucalyptus. | Does M&A create durable international scale or obscure organic growth? |

| SBC | $135M in 2025. | Must decline as a percentage of revenue for owner earnings to improve. |

The Eucalyptus deal is especially important. HIMS announced an agreement to acquire Eucalyptus, an Australia-based digital health company operating in Australia, the United Kingdom, Germany, Canada, and Japan, for up to $1.15 billion total consideration. The structure includes about $240 million upfront cash, about $710 million deferred payments, and up to $200 million earn-out, with the company having the option to settle roughly 60% of deferred and earn-out payments in cash or stock. That can accelerate global scale, but it also creates integration, deferred-payment, earn-out, and dilution risk.

Institutional ownership is high, but reported figures vary by source and methodology. MarketBeat showed roughly 64% institutional ownership, while Fintel and 13F aggregators showed hundreds of holders. Q1 2026 13F data is incomplete until the May 15 filing deadline.

Insider activity is not a positive signal. Public Form 4 aggregators show no recent open-market insider buying and significant insider sales over the last twelve months. Insider selling does not prove the thesis is broken, but it weakens the signal quality when combined with regulatory investigations and a valuation that depends on long-duration execution.

Short interest is very high. MarketBeat showed about 69.8 million shares sold short as of 2026-04-15, around 35% of float and about three days to cover. Borrow cost appeared low, so this is not a classic forced-cover hard-to-borrow setup. It is still an important market fact: the stock is heavily debated, can move violently around earnings and regulatory news, and may have periodic squeeze dynamics.

Analyst sentiment is mixed. MarketBeat showed a Hold consensus with an average target around $32, while StockAnalysis showed a Buy consensus with an average target around $30 and a wide range from $18 to $68. That dispersion is the point. Investors are not debating a few cents of EPS; they are debating whether HIMS is a durable high-growth healthcare platform or a GLP-1-driven DTC pharmacy cycle.

## Bull Case

The bull case is that Hims & Hers is becoming the leading consumer front end for routine cash-pay healthcare. The company has brand permission in stigmatized categories, a large subscriber base, owned fulfillment capabilities, provider workflows, and a demonstrated ability to launch new product categories quickly.

In this case, GLP-1s are not the whole story. They accelerated awareness, raised ARPU, brought more women into the platform, and helped prove that HIMS can manage higher-value chronic-care categories. But the long-term value comes from cross-selling, retention, recurring prescriptions, labs, hormonal health, dermatology, sexual health, and international expansion.

The March 2026 weight-loss pivot could be a positive if it works. HIMS said it would shift U.S. weight loss toward FDA-approved GLP-1s, stop advertising compounded GLP-1s, and offer compounded GLP-1 only when a provider determines clinical necessity. If this lets the company keep weight-loss customers while reducing enforcement risk, the market may stop treating GLP-1 as a cliff.

The company also has visible long-term ambitions. Management has discussed a roadmap to at least $6.5 billion of 2030 revenue and $1.3 billion of adjusted EBITDA. If HIMS gets anywhere close to that while converting a reasonable portion of adjusted EBITDA into true free cash flow, the current $6.5 billion market cap will look low in hindsight.

## Bear Case

The bear case is that HIMS is priced like a durable healthcare platform while still behaving like a high-growth DTC marketing and pharmacy distributor with large regulatory and disclosure gaps.

The strongest version starts with GLP-1. HIMS benefited from extraordinary demand for weight-loss drugs, limited affordability of branded products, and the availability of compounded alternatives. FDA resolved the semaglutide shortage in February 2025, later clarified policies on essentially copies, issued warning letters over compounded GLP-1 promotional claims, and in 2026 directly named Hims & Hers in a statement about restricting GLP-1 APIs used in non-FDA-approved compounded drugs being mass-marketed as alternatives to FDA-approved drugs. The 10-K also discloses HHS/DOJ referral language, a Novo patent lawsuit, and an SEC investigation into public statements and disclosures concerning compounded semaglutide and related business relationships.

The bear case then turns to normalized economics. Branded GLP-1 distribution may preserve demand, but likely carries lower gross margin and less differentiation than compounded products. If customers came for cheap weight-loss drugs and do not retain or cross-sell into other categories, then the 2025 ARPU uplift may fade.

Finally, cash conversion is weak relative to the headline narrative. 2025 free cash flow was only $57 million, while SBC was $135 million and capex/software/intangible spending was $243 million. A company trading around 3x sales and roughly 50x net income needs strong future cash conversion. If capex, SBC, acquisitions, and regulatory costs remain elevated, the equity has little current free-cash-flow support.

## Base Case

The base case assumes HIMS is real but not yet proven as a high-quality compounder. Revenue grows from $2.35 billion in 2025 to the high-$2 billion range in 2026, then compounds at a double-digit but slowing rate as GLP-1 growth normalizes and non-GLP-1 categories continue to expand. Gross margin remains below the legacy 80% level because weight loss, international expansion, branded drugs, fulfillment, and clinical complexity carry higher costs.

Adjusted EBITDA remains positive, but true free cash flow takes time to catch up because the company keeps investing in fulfillment, pharmacy, labs, international operations, and software. SBC declines as a percentage of revenue but remains material. M&A adds revenue and optionality, but also complicates organic comparability.

In this scenario, the stock is roughly fairly valued at $28.27. The market is already giving HIMS credit for substantial growth and future margin improvement, but not full credit for the 2030 bull-case roadmap. The stock can work, but the margin of safety is not obvious until the company proves post-compounded-GLP-1 durability and better free cash flow conversion.

## Variant Perception

The variant long view is that the market is too focused on compounded GLP-1 controversy and is missing that HIMS is building the default consumer interface for routine cash-pay care. In that view, GLP-1 brought customers into the platform, but the durable asset is the brand, data, provider network, fulfillment stack, personalization, and category-launch machine.

The variant short view is that the market is too willing to capitalize adjusted EBITDA and management's 2030 roadmap while underweighting regulatory risk, free cash flow weakness, and GLP-1-driven ARPU. In that view, the company is not a healthcare compounder yet; it is a DTC marketing engine with pharmacy infrastructure and an unusually hot product cycle.

The most important unresolved question is not whether HIMS can grow. It can. The question is whether growth after the GLP-1 compounding reset can convert into durable owner earnings.

## Conclusion

HIMS is a real business with real platform characteristics, but the stock is not a simple cheap compounder. The company has scaled quickly, built a recognizable brand, expanded into large categories, and created a vertically integrated consumer-health infrastructure that could be valuable for years.

The problem is that the market price already assumes a lot of that future. At $28.27, HIMS needs sustained double-digit revenue growth and meaningful owner free cash flow margin expansion to justify the valuation. It also needs to prove that GLP-1 was an accelerant rather than the core growth engine.

My conclusion is neutral-to-cautiously constructive on the company and neutral on the stock at the current price. I would become more constructive if Q1/Q2 show durable non-GLP-1 growth, acceptable branded GLP-1 economics, regulatory de-risking, lower SBC intensity, and a clear free-cash-flow recovery. I would become more bearish if subscriber growth stays weak, ARPU rolls over, guidance depends on opaque GLP-1 economics, or regulatory matters create material operating restrictions.
