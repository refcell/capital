## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Hims & Hers looks like a broken GLP-1 trade on the surface. The stock has fallen from a 52-week high above $70 to $26.19, Q1 2026 revenue grew only 4%, gross margin collapsed to 65%, GAAP net income swung to a $92 million loss, and the company issued another convertible note less than a year after raising $1.0 billion of 2030 converts. That is the simple bear story.

The more interesting story is that the company is not obviously broken. Subscribers still grew to nearly 2.6 million, management raised FY2026 revenue guidance to $2.8 billion to $3.0 billion, international revenue almost 10x'd from a tiny base, free cash flow was positive in Q1, and the company is still targeting at least $6.5 billion of 2030 revenue and $1.3 billion of adjusted EBITDA. The market is trying to decide whether HIMS is a durable consumer-health platform using weight loss as an accelerant, or a high-beta direct-to-consumer pharmacy business whose best economics came from a temporary compounded-GLP-1 opening.

### Current View

My read is still cautiously constructive, but less so than it was in the low-$20s. At $26.19, HIMS is closer to the market's current average price target and no longer offers the same obvious controversy discount it had two weeks ago. The stock can still work if revenue compounds from $2.35 billion in 2025 to roughly $4.5 billion to $5.5 billion by 2030 with low-teens owner free-cash-flow margins. I would still call this an interesting but diligence-heavy watchlist idea rather than a sleep-well compounder.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Hims & Hers began as a direct-to-consumer brand for stigmatized and repeatable health categories: erectile dysfunction, hair loss, dermatology, sexual health, and related wellness products. The original wedge was privacy, convenience, transparent pricing, online intake, and subscription fulfillment. The customer did not need a broad primary-care relationship; the customer needed a low-friction way to access a recurring treatment.

The company then moved into higher-ARPU and more chronic categories: mental health, dermatology, weight loss, hormone health, menopause, testosterone, labs, and international digital health. The 2024-2025 acceleration came with GLP-1 weight loss demand and compounded semaglutide availability. That changed the story. HIMS stopped being just a stigmatized-care DTC platform and became part of a national debate over compounded GLP-1 access, branded drug affordability, direct-to-consumer advertising, and pharmacy regulation.

The 2026 story is another pivot. In March 2026, the company shifted its U.S. weight-loss offering toward branded GLP-1 medications and limited compounded GLP-1 access. In February 2026 it agreed to acquire Eucalyptus, an international digital-health company, for up to $1.15 billion. In May 2026 it announced access to generic semaglutide in Canada and issued new 2032 convertible notes to support international expansion and AI/platform investment. The company is trying to turn the GLP-1 controversy into a broader global consumer-health platform.

### Why The Company Matters

HIMS matters because ordinary healthcare is often too slow, embarrassing, fragmented, or expensive for recurring consumer conditions. The company gives customers a digital front door, asynchronous or online provider access, prescription fulfillment, recurring subscriptions, and condition-specific product flows. For the customer, the pitch is convenience and privacy. For the investor, the pitch is that enough recurring, cash-pay categories can create a scaled consumer-health platform with better data, retention, and economics than one-off DTC pharmacy.

The problem is also hard. Healthcare is regulated by state, federal, and international rules; pharmacy fulfillment must meet quality requirements; prescription marketing is constrained; compounding has special restrictions; branded drugs are controlled by manufacturers; and customer acquisition is expensive. HIMS has an edge if its brand, data, care flows, fulfillment network, and category-launch muscle lower acquisition cost and improve retention. It has no edge if it is mainly a paid-media arbitrage around whatever prescription category is hottest.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Hims & Hers earns revenue by selling health and wellness products and services through its websites and mobile apps. Revenue includes access to prescription and non-prescription products, consultation services, medication adjustments where applicable, post-consultation support, lab-result delivery where applicable, and fulfillment. Most revenue is subscription-based, but one-time purchases and wholesale/retail products also exist. Wholesale is not material.

The company now reports revenue by geography rather than the older Online / Wholesale framing.

### The Economic Engine

The economic engine is:

`condition-specific demand -> paid and organic acquisition -> digital intake -> provider review -> prescription or product access -> fulfillment -> subscription retention -> cross-sell into new categories`

That loop works if HIMS can acquire customers below lifetime value, retain them across treatment cycles, expand them into adjacent categories, and fulfill at attractive margins. The loop breaks if customers churn after one high-demand category, if marketing costs rise faster than retention, if branded products commoditize the front end, or if regulation forces the company to change the product flow.

### Unit Economics / Customer Economics

HIMS does not disclose enough category-level retention, CAC payback, GLP-1 gross profit, or cohort data to fully underwrite unit economics. The report therefore uses subscriber count, monthly revenue per average subscriber, marketing expense, gross margin, deferred revenue, and free cash flow as imperfect proxies.

### Competitive Position

HIMS' advantage is not a hard technical monopoly. The advantage is an integrated consumer funnel: brand, content, condition-specific landing pages, intake, provider network, pharmacy fulfillment, subscription management, and customer data. That is valuable, but it can be attacked from several directions.

| Competitor Type | Examples | Company Advantage | Company Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Category-specific DTC platforms | Ro, LifeMD, WeightWatchers/Sequence, specialty clinics. | Broader brand, category breadth, fulfillment scale. | Specialists may win in obesity, insurance, or disease-specific care. |
| Retail / tech healthcare | Amazon One Medical, Amazon Pharmacy, GoodRx. | Condition-specific funnel and brand voice. | Amazon and GoodRx can pressure price transparency and pharmacy margins. |
| Traditional healthcare | PCPs, specialists, local pharmacies, cash-pay clinics. | Convenience, privacy, subscription flow. | Traditional providers have clinical trust and broader medical context. |
| Manufacturers / manufacturer DTC | Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly, branded-drug programs. | Can aggregate consumer demand and provide care workflow. | Manufacturers own drug economics and can commoditize distributors. |
| International digital-health platforms | Eucalyptus, Zava, Livewell-like competitors. | HIMS can consolidate global consumer-health assets. | Local trust, regulation, and product-market fit vary. |

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

HIMS' reported and adjusted earnings need careful treatment. Q1 2026 GAAP net loss was ugly, but the adjusted EBITDA bridge was also heavily adjusted. The company added back stock comp, restructuring and other charges, legal settlement costs, acquisition costs, fair-value changes, payroll taxes on stock comp, D&A, and taxes. Some of those are non-recurring. Some are part of the cost of running an acquisition-heavy, equity-compensating, highly regulated healthcare platform.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

Andrew Dudum is co-founder, CEO, and chair. This matters because HIMS is not a normal one-share-one-vote public company. Shares of Class V common stock carry 175 votes per share, and Dudum holds all issued and outstanding Class V shares. The Q1 2026 filing says he controls about 90% of voting power. HIMS qualifies as a controlled company under NYSE rules.

Founder control can support bold, long-term execution. It can also reduce minority-shareholder leverage exactly when a company is making aggressive moves: weight-loss pivots, international M&A, convertible note issuance, capex buildout, and regulatory-risk management.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

Bulls believe HIMS is building the default consumer front end for cash-pay recurring healthcare. In this view, GLP-1s accelerated awareness but did not define the company. The company can use brand, data, provider workflows, pharmacy fulfillment, diagnostics, and AI-enabled personalization to expand across weight loss, sexual health, hair, dermatology, hormone health, mental health, labs, and international markets. If HIMS reaches something close to $6.5 billion of 2030 revenue with 20% adjusted EBITDA margins, the current stock price is too low.

### What Bears Believe

Bears believe HIMS is a promotional DTC pharmacy business that temporarily benefited from compounded semaglutide demand. In this view, the pivot to branded GLP-1s lowers margin and differentiation, regulators may further restrict compounding and advertising, manufacturer-controlled drug economics weaken the model, U.S. revenue is already declining, and adjusted EBITDA hides real costs. The controlled-company structure, insider selling, legal/regulatory issues, and converts make the setup worse.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The positive variant view is that HIMS does not need to hit the full $6.5 billion 2030 target for the stock to work from $26.19. A $4.5 billion to $5.0 billion revenue business with low-teens FCF margins can still support attractive upside if the market regains confidence in growth and cash conversion.

The negative variant view is that the 2026 guidance raise may be masking a lower-quality mix shift. If the growth comes from branded or generic GLP-1 access with lower gross margin, higher working capital, supplier concentration, regulatory risk, and less customer retention, revenue can grow while per-share value creation disappoints.

## 10. Bull Case

If HIMS is becoming a global consumer-health platform rather than a U.S. GLP-1 arbitrage, then Q1 2026 is a messy transition quarter, not a thesis break. In the bull case, the U.S. business reaccelerates in Q2/Q3, branded GLP-1 access retains customers, Canadian generic semaglutide and Eucalyptus prove the international model, and diagnostics/personalization lift retention. Revenue approaches $6.5 billion by 2030, adjusted EBITDA approaches $1.3 billion, and true FCF exceeds $1.0 billion. A 30x FCF multiple on a still-growing platform could support more than $100 per share even after dilution.

## 11. Bear Case

If the GLP-1 pivot exposes HIMS as a lower-margin distributor, then the stock is still too expensive. In that case, U.S. revenue remains under pressure, gross margin stays in the low-to-mid 60s, branded products commoditize the front end, regulatory and legal costs remain elevated, and acquisition-driven international growth fails to create clean organic growth. FCF margin stays around 5%, terminal FCF is less than $200 million, and the business deserves a market multiple. That supports a stock in the low-to-mid teens.

## 12. Base Case

The base case assumes HIMS is a real platform with messy but survivable GLP-1/regulatory transition risk. FY2026 revenue lands near the midpoint of company guidance and consensus, then compounds in the mid-teens through 2030. Gross margin recovers from Q1's 65% toward the low 70s as restructuring charges fade and scale helps, but it does not return to the clean 80% era because branded drugs, international mix, labs, and fulfillment complexity are structurally more expensive. Adjusted EBITDA margin reaches the mid-teens over time, and true FCF margin reaches about 11%. This supports a 2030 value around $50 per share, or a present value in the high $20s to mid-$30s depending on discount rate and dilution.

## 18. Conclusion

HIMS is still more interesting in the mid-$20s than it was above $50, but it is no longer a clean controversy bargain. The business has real assets: brand, 2.6 million subscribers, category breadth, international expansion, fulfillment investment, and a long-term roadmap. It also has real problems: Q1 U.S. revenue decline, 65% gross margin, a large GAAP loss, heavy adjustments, regulatory scrutiny, controlled-company governance, and two layers of convertible notes.

The stock works if Q1 2026 was the messy trough of a strategic pivot and revenue accelerates while gross margin recovers. The stock does not work if Q1 was the normalized model: lower-margin branded-drug distribution, expensive international expansion, and recurring legal/regulatory friction. My base-case fair value is still roughly $28-$35 per share, with a wide bear/bull range, which means the current price is closer to fair value than it was two weeks ago. I would keep HIMS on the watchlist and update after Q2 2026 results.
