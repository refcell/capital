## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

dLocal looks like a cheap, profitable emerging-market payments compounder after a post-Q1 selloff. The surface story is strong: Q1 2026 TPV grew 73% year over year, revenue grew 55%, gross profit reached a record $118.7 million, and management left full-year guidance unchanged. The real question is less flattering: can dLocal keep compounding gross profit and adjusted free cash flow while large merchants push down gross profit yield, working capital swings obscure cash conversion, and investors still remember the short-seller and litigation overhang?

The stock is worth studying because both sides have a coherent story. Bulls can point to scarce emerging-market payment coverage, high net revenue retention, 760+ enterprise merchants, 60+ markets, and a balance sheet that supports dividends and buybacks. Bears can point to gross profit / TPV falling to 0.84% in Q1 2026, top-merchant concentration, country risk, settlement-cash ambiguity, and the possibility that dLocal is winning volume by giving away economics.

### Current View

DLO is a watchlist long rather than a clean own. The current price does not require heroic assumptions if 2025 adjusted FCF is durable and FY2026 guidance is credible. My base-case value is roughly $26 to $28 per share using 2030 gross-profit and adjusted-FCF power, but that value depends on gross profit yield stabilizing and adjusted FCF catching up after Q1's working-capital drag. The next update trigger is Q2 2026: if gross profit stays on plan and adjusted FCF normalizes, the stock deserves a harder look; if gross profit / TPV keeps compressing below 0.80% and cash conversion remains weak, the cheap multiple is justified.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

dLocal was founded in Uruguay in 2016 to solve a specific problem: global companies could not easily accept local payment methods, make local payouts, handle FX, and comply with country-specific rules across emerging markets. The founding insight was that a merchant selling into Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Egypt, India, or Nigeria does not just need card acquiring. It needs local bank transfers, wallets, cash methods, local card routing, payouts, tax handling, reconciliation, risk controls, settlement, and local regulatory relationships.

The company listed on Nasdaq in 2021. That public-market period has been volatile. dLocal was once valued like a rare high-growth fintech infrastructure asset, then derated after take-rate compression, management transition, short-seller allegations, and class-action litigation. The current story is a rebuild: Pedro Arnt, formerly CFO of MercadoLibre, took over as CEO in 2023; the company expanded its country and license footprint; 2025 growth reaccelerated; and Q1 2026 showed very strong volume growth but still raised margin-quality questions.

### Why The Company Matters

dLocal matters because emerging-market payment localization is one of the places where "just use Stripe or Adyen everywhere" is not always enough. A global merchant selling digital goods, commerce, delivery, remittances, travel, SaaS, or advertising into emerging markets needs to maximize approval rates, accept local preferences, settle funds across currencies, pay local suppliers or users, and stay compliant with local rules. Doing that country by country is slow and risky.

dLocal's value proposition is "one API, one platform, one contract" across fragmented markets. The edge is not that the API itself cannot be copied. The edge, if real, is the accumulated operating system: licenses, local banking and processor connections, payment-method breadth, reconciliation, FX operations, fraud/routing data, local compliance, and enterprise integrations that are painful to replace.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

dLocal charges global merchants for processing pay-ins, pay-outs, FX, settlement, tax, fraud, reconciliation, and related payment services. Pricing can be a percentage of transaction value, a fixed fee, FX spread / service fee, product fee, chargeback / refund fee, setup fee, or minimum monthly fee. The company reports revenue under IFRS on a gross basis because it says it controls the processing service, contracts with merchants, selects and negotiates with processors, and bears margin risk.

That accounting point matters. Revenue is useful, but it is not the cleanest economic measure. dLocal can grow TPV and revenue while gross profit yield falls. For valuation, the core metrics are gross profit dollars, gross profit / TPV, adjusted EBITDA / gross profit, adjusted FCF, and the amount of cash that is actually free to distribute.

| Revenue Stream | Description | Share / Size | Economics | Risk |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Pay-ins | Collects local-currency payments from consumers for global merchants. | Q1 2026 TPV $10.1B, 72% of TPV. | Merchant fees, FX services, local routing economics. | Large merchants can demand lower pricing and multi-route. |
| Pay-outs | Sends local-currency payments to sellers, drivers, contractors, recipients, or users. | Q1 2026 TPV $3.9B, 28% of TPV. | Payout fees, FX, settlement and reconciliation value. | Trust, liquidity, AML, bank partner, and compliance risk. |
| Cross-border | Merchant and consumer / recipient sit across borders. | Q1 2026 TPV $6.3B, 45% of TPV. | Higher complexity can support better economics. | FX rules, trapped cash, tax, and settlement risk. |
| Local-to-local | Local entity processes local transactions in-country. | Q1 2026 TPV $7.7B, 55% of TPV. | Lower friction and better local acceptance. | Local competitors and lower take rates. |
| Platform / orchestration / add-ons | Marketplace onboarding, split payments, fraud, routing, reconciliation, tax, local methods. | Not separately disclosed. | Can deepen merchant integration. | May become bundled into lower headline pricing. |

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: add markets and payment methods -> win enterprise merchants -> expand share of wallet and countries -> grow TPV -> retain enough gross profit yield -> convert gross profit to operating profit and adjusted FCF -> return excess capital while reinvesting in licenses, compliance, and automation.

The weak link is "retain enough gross profit yield." dLocal's reported TPV growth can look spectacular while the equity economics are mediocre if each incremental dollar of TPV carries much lower gross profit. The business works if scale lowers processor costs, improves routing, deepens merchant reliance, and lets operating leverage offset pricing pressure. It breaks if dLocal becomes one interchangeable route in a merchant's payment stack.

## 4. Products, Segments, And Moat

### Product / Segment Breakdown

dLocal should not be analyzed as a single-country acquirer. The value is in the cross-market bundle. Pay-ins, payouts, FX, compliance, routing, reporting, reconciliation, tax handling, and local payment methods reinforce each other because the same merchant often wants many of them across many countries.

| Product / Segment | Role In Thesis | Economics | Evidence | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pay-ins | Core volume and merchant-entry product. | Percentage / fixed fees and FX economics. | $10.1B Q1 2026 TPV, +86% YoY. | Lower pricing for large merchants. |
| Pay-outs | Deepens marketplace, gig, remittance, and platform use cases. | Payout fees and settlement value. | $3.9B Q1 2026 TPV, +48% YoY. | Compliance, fraud, bank-partner, and liquidity risk. |

| Platform / orchestration products | Could increase switching costs. | Software-like attachment to payment flows. | Management highlights dLocal for Platforms, orchestration, fraud, routing, and reconciliation. | Could be treated as procurement checkbox rather than separate value. |

### Competitive Position

dLocal competes against global PSPs, regional specialists, banks, local acquirers, wallets, and merchant internalization. Its advantage is breadth and emerging-market specialization. Its disadvantage is that the largest merchants are sophisticated buyers. They can route around dLocal, compare vendors country by country, and use dLocal only where it performs best.

| Competitor Type | Examples | Company Advantage | Company Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Global PSPs / acquirers | Adyen, Stripe, PayPal / Hyperwallet, Nuvei, Worldpay. | Broader EM localization and pay-in / pay-out / FX bundle. | Global vendors have brand trust, procurement relationships, and balance-sheet scale. |
| EM specialists | EBANX, Rapyd, PPRO, local PSPs. | Public-company scale, multi-region footprint, enterprise references. | Specialists may be better or cheaper in specific corridors or countries. |
| Local banks and acquirers | Country-specific banks, acquirers, instant-payment rails. | One contract and API across markets. | Banks can own rails and may have local regulatory advantages. |
| Merchant internalization | Large global merchants building direct local integrations. | dLocal reduces complexity and time to market. | Largest merchants can multi-route and pressure pricing. |

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

dLocal is profitable, but reported operating cash flow is not the same thing as owner earnings. The company handles payment flows, settlement balances, merchant receivables, payables, local cash, FX, and working capital across many countries. In a growth quarter, operating cash flow can look artificially good or bad depending on settlement timing. dLocal's adjusted free cash flow, which strips merchant working-capital movements and subtracts capex, is the better starting point.

| Issue | Evidence | Why It Matters | Current Read |
|---|---|---|---|
| Merchant working capital | Q1 2026 changes in merchant working capital were negative $68.4M; FY2025 reported OCF benefited from payables growth. | Reported OCF can diverge sharply from owner earnings. | Use adjusted FCF, not raw OCF. |
| Gross revenue presentation | dLocal reports revenue gross and processor costs below revenue. | Revenue growth can overstate economic growth if gross margin falls. | Gross profit is the better metric. |

- Implied execution hurdle: DLO does not need to become Visa or Adyen; it needs to prove that gross profit and adjusted FCF can compound through falling take rates.

### Projection Takeaway

The stock works if Q1's volume strength turns into 15%+ multi-year gross profit growth and adjusted FCF conversion normalizes after the working-capital drag. The stock fails if TPV growth is bought with structurally lower gross profit yield and if settlement / merchant working-capital volatility makes reported cash less distributable than investors hoped. Consensus appears reasonable on revenue, but possibly too clean on EPS if gross profit yield and opex normalization disappoint.

### Business-Model Specific Projection Notes

For DLO, the main drivers are TPV, gross profit yield, adjusted EBITDA / gross profit, adjusted FCF, settlement working capital, merchant concentration, country mix, and local-currency / FX risk. Revenue is an expectations anchor, not the valuation anchor.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

Bulls believe dLocal is a rare public infrastructure asset tied to emerging-market digitization. Large merchants need a partner that can process local cards, wallets, bank transfers, cash methods, payouts, FX, tax, compliance, and reconciliation across dozens of countries. The more countries, methods, and licenses dLocal adds, the harder it becomes to replace. Lower take rate is the price of scaling with enterprise merchants, but gross profit dollars and adjusted FCF can still compound because TPV growth is so high and opex can scale more slowly after the 2025 investment cycle.

### What Bears Believe

Bears believe the "one API for emerging markets" story hides a lower-quality processor with declining yields, concentrated customers, opaque settlement balances, and difficult country risks. They do not need TPV growth to be fake for the short case to work. They only need incremental TPV to be low-margin, working capital to consume cash, top merchants to keep repricing, and public investors to keep applying a discount to litigation, governance, and cash-quality uncertainty.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The positive variant view is that the market is over-penalizing gross profit yield compression while underweighting gross profit dollars. A move from 1.05% to 0.84% gross profit / TPV looks bad, but if TPV compounds fast enough and opex growth slows, absolute gross profit and FCF can still grow at attractive rates. The negative variant view is that investors may still be too quick to credit adjusted FCF as distributable cash when settlement timing, local restrictions, and merchant terms can move cash around the balance sheet.

## 10. Bull Case

If dLocal's operating complexity is a real moat, then Q1 2026 is not a low-quality burst of volume; it is proof that global merchants are sending more countries, methods, and use cases through the platform. In that world, gross profit / TPV can settle near 0.80% to 0.90%, management can grow FY2026 gross profit into the $500M range, and opex discipline can restore adjusted FCF conversion.

The stock is attractive in this case because the current valuation is not pricing a premium compounder. A company producing $450M to $650M of adjusted FCF by 2030, with net cash and a buyback, should not trade at today's $3.5B market cap. The bull case does not require heroic revenue multiples. It requires visible adjusted FCF durability, better cash disclosure, and confidence that large-merchant repricing is a manageable cost of scale.

## 11. Bear Case

If incremental TPV is coming from very large merchants and lower-yield corridors, then dLocal can keep reporting impressive volume growth while the equity economics stagnate. Gross profit / TPV fell to 0.84% in Q1 2026. If that moves below 0.75% and opex does not come down, the company may miss the spirit of guidance even if TPV looks strong.

In that world, adjusted FCF does not compound from the 2025 base. Settlement timing, receivables, payables, local restrictions, and merchant working capital keep cash conversion volatile. Top merchants use dLocal as one route among many and continue demanding lower spreads. The right multiple becomes 10x to 12x FCF, not a fintech infrastructure multiple. Downside to the low teens or high single digits is plausible if the market loses faith in gross profit durability.

## 12. Base Case

The base case is that dLocal is a real but discounted payments infrastructure company. TPV growth remains high in 2026 and then decelerates. Gross profit grows much slower than TPV but still compounds in the mid-teens because the platform remains useful to global merchants. Gross profit / TPV stabilizes around the high-70 bps to high-80 bps range. Opex growth moderates after the investment cycle, and adjusted FCF conversion improves from the weak Q1 print.

The stock works in this base case because the market is too focused on the direction of take rate and not enough on absolute gross profit and cash generation. The market needs to see two or three more quarters of evidence: guidance tracking, gross profit yield not collapsing, adjusted FCF normalization, and actual buyback execution.

### Primary Valuation Framework

The primary framework is 2030 gross-profit and adjusted-FCF power. For DLO, gross profit is a better economic anchor than revenue because revenue is gross-reported and payment-method mix can move margins. Adjusted FCF is better than operating cash flow because merchant settlement working capital can dominate quarter-to-quarter cash generation.

I use 293.5 million shares and roughly $350 million of conservatively credited corporate net cash. I do not give full credit for all cash and financial assets because some cash may be operational, settlement-related, restricted, collateralized, locally trapped, or needed for faster payouts and regulatory requirements.

## 18. Conclusion

dLocal is not a clean compounder, but it is too interesting to ignore at $12.06. The company is growing quickly, profitable, and useful to global merchants operating across difficult payment markets. Q1 2026 strengthened the growth story and weakened neither the bull nor bear case: TPV, revenue, and gross profit were excellent, while gross profit yield and adjusted FCF kept the quality debate alive.

My current stance is watch / cautiously constructive. The base-case value is roughly $26 to $28 per share if gross profit compounds in the mid-teens and adjusted FCF conversion normalizes. The bear case lands near today's price if yield compression continues and cash conversion remains messy. The next report should focus less on headline TPV and more on gross profit / TPV, adjusted FCF, corporate cash, and buyback execution.
