# dLocal Limited (NASDAQ: DLO) Investment Research

Date: 2026-05-09

Price snapshot: DLO closed at $13.70 on 2026-05-08. Using 294.9 million Class A and Class B shares outstanding at 2025-12-31, market capitalization was approximately $4.04 billion. dLocal reported $719.9 million of total cash and cash equivalents, $424.5 million of corporate cash and cash equivalents, and roughly $86.7 million of financial liabilities / borrowings at 2025-12-31. A practical enterprise value range is therefore about $3.4 billion to $3.7 billion, depending on whether one gives full credit for total cash or only corporate cash. The latest reported financial period is Q4/FY2025, released 2026-03-18; Q1 2026 results were scheduled for 2026-05-14, so they are not included here.

This is investment research, not personalized financial advice.

## Executive Summary

dLocal is a high-growth, high-margin emerging-market payments infrastructure company with a real business, a real discount, and real reasons for that discount. The company helps global enterprise merchants accept payments, make payouts, manage FX, and settle funds across fragmented emerging-market payment rails through one API, one platform, and one contract. In FY2025, TPV grew 60% to $40.8 billion, revenue grew 47% to $1.09 billion, adjusted EBITDA grew 47% to $278 million, and adjusted free cash flow grew 110% to $191 million.

The bull case is that dLocal is becoming a scarce financial-infrastructure toll road for emerging-market commerce. Large merchants want access to Brazil, Mexico, Argentina, Africa, Asia, local cards, wallets, bank transfers, Pix, UPI, M-Pesa, payouts, FX, tax, compliance, and reconciliation without stitching together dozens of country-specific providers. If dLocal keeps growing gross profit 20%+ while converting most of that gross profit into EBITDA and cash, the current valuation is not demanding.

The bear case is not just "payments are competitive." The sharper version is that dLocal's headline TPV growth may be coming with lower-quality economics: lower take rates, large merchant pricing pressure, customer concentration, opaque settlement chains, Argentina/FX complexity, litigation and short-seller overhang, and working-capital swings that make reported cash flow hard to interpret. Gross profit / TPV fell from 1.57% in 2023 to 0.99% in 2025, and Q4 2025 gross profit / TPV was only 0.88%.

My read is cautiously constructive, but not clean enough to treat as a simple compounder. The key question is whether gross profit dollars and adjusted free cash flow can compound despite falling yield on TPV. On my base-case DCF, dLocal is worth about $19 per share versus $13.70 today. That is attractive, but the bear case can plausibly land near $10 if take-rate compression and working-capital needs overwhelm volume growth. I would frame DLO as an interesting but diligence-heavy long, not a sleep-well payments franchise.

## Company Overview

dLocal is a Cayman Islands company with operational headquarters in Montevideo, Uruguay. It was created to simplify online payments in emerging markets for global enterprise merchants. The company listed on Nasdaq in 2021 and reports under IFRS.

The core pitch is "One dLocal": one API, one platform, and one contract that gives global merchants access to local payment methods, cards, bank transfers, wallets, cash payment methods, payouts, FX, settlement, tax, compliance, fraud tools, and reconciliation across emerging markets. As of 2025, the platform operated in 44 countries, supported more than 160 local pay-in payment methods and 939 local pay-out payment methods, held 37 licenses or authorizations across 26 markets, and had 16 additional license applications in process.

The customer base is enterprise-heavy. dLocal disclosed over 760 global merchants, including examples such as Shein, Didi, Payoneer, Temu, Google, Facebook, Rappi, Deel, Uber, SpaceX, Worldpay, Spotify, and Shopify partnerships. Enterprise merchants represented 99% of TPV in 2025.

This is not a merchant-acquiring story in a single country. It is a cross-border and local-to-local emerging-market infrastructure story. The company is trying to own the hard part of payment localization for global merchants that want to sell into markets where card penetration, bank rails, FX rules, tax requirements, local wallets, and settlement procedures differ country by country.

## Business Model

dLocal makes money from fees charged to merchants on approved transactions. Those fees can be fixed per transaction, a percentage of transaction value, or product-specific fees. The company also earns FX service fees where transactions involve currency conversion and cross-border fund movement. Other fees include installment and advance fees, chargeback and refund fees, transfer fees, minimum monthly fees, setup fees, and transaction-tax related fees.

There are two core flows:

| Flow | Description | 2025 TPV |
|---|---:|---:|
| Pay-in | dLocal collects money from end users in local currency and makes funds available to merchants in the requested country/currency after settlement. | $28.2B / 69% |
| Pay-out | dLocal receives merchant funds and disburses local currency to registered recipients such as drivers, sellers, vendors, partners, contractors, renters, or refund recipients. | $12.6B / 31% |

The business also splits almost evenly between cross-border and local-to-local transactions. In 2025, cross-border TPV was $20.3 billion, or 50% of total TPV, and local-to-local TPV was $20.5 billion, also 50%.

dLocal reports revenue gross because it says it controls the payment-processing service, bears primary responsibility to merchants, contracts directly with merchants, negotiates independently with payment processors, and carries margin risk. This matters. Revenue can move faster or slower than economic value depending on payment method, processor costs, FX fees, and merchant pricing. For valuation, gross profit and adjusted FCF are more useful than headline revenue.

## Products and Technology

dLocal's product set includes pay-ins, payouts, platform/marketplace payments, FX management, reporting, reconciliation, tax and compliance support, fraud tooling, chargeback and refund workflows, local cards, bank transfers, e-wallets, cash methods, and alternative payment methods.

Recent product extensions include:

| Product / Extension | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| dLocal for Platforms | Helps marketplaces and platforms onboard, verify, split payments, withhold fees, and settle funds. |
| Payment orchestration | Lets merchants use dLocal's routing, fraud, and reporting layer while maintaining direct processor relationships in selected markets. |
| SmartAPMs / tokenized APMs | Attempts to make alternative local payment methods behave more like card-on-file, improving repeat checkout. |
| BNPL integrations | Connects third-party BNPL providers at checkout without dLocal taking direct credit risk. |
| Stablecoin rails | Supports fiat-to-stablecoin and stablecoin-to-fiat cross-border flows with regulated counterparties. |
| AI / routing / fraud tools | Used for approval optimization, fraud detection, anomaly detection, code productivity, and operational automation. |

The technology moat is not that the API itself is impossible to copy. The moat, if it exists, is operational: local licenses, local bank and processor relationships, country-specific compliance, payment-method coverage, settlement expertise, fraud data, routing performance, FX liquidity, and deep merchant integrations. This should improve with scale, but it can also be competed away by global PSPs, local PSPs, or merchants that multi-route volumes.

## Industry and Competitive Position

The payments industry is enormous, but dLocal's relevant market is narrower and more complex: global merchants selling into emerging markets where local payment methods, capital controls, tax rules, bank integrations, and FX settlement differ materially from developed markets.

The secular backdrop is attractive. Emerging markets continue to shift from cash to digital payments, e-commerce, instant payment rails, local wallets, bank transfers, and cross-border digital commerce. But the payment stack is fragmented. That fragmentation is dLocal's opportunity.

The competitive set includes:

| Competitor type | Examples | dLocal's advantage | dLocal's disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Global PSPs / acquirers | Adyen, Stripe, PayPal/Hyperwallet, Nuvei | Deeper EM specialization, local payment coverage, FX and payout expertise. | Global scale, brand trust, procurement comfort, broader enterprise relationships. |
| EM specialists | EBANX, Rapyd, PPRO, local PSPs | Public-company scale, broad country footprint, enterprise references, integrated pay-in/pay-out/FX. | Local specialists can be cheaper or better in individual countries. |
| Banks / local acquirers | Country-specific banks, acquirers, wallets | One contract and API across markets; better cross-border merchant experience. | Banks may own local rails, have lower cost of capital, and can win payout trust. |
| Merchant internalization | Large merchants building direct integrations | dLocal reduces operational complexity. | Large merchants can multi-route and use dLocal as one vendor among several. |

The biggest competitive risk is commoditization at scale. Large merchants have leverage. As they grow, they can demand tiered pricing, lower spreads, faster settlement, guarantees, and better terms. Management itself expects structural volume-based discounting in 2026. The question is whether scale offsets that through more volume, better processor pricing, lower fraud, more data, and operating leverage.

## Cash Flow and Accounting Quality

The cash-flow analysis is more complicated than the earnings growth suggests.

Reported IFRS operating cash flow was $415.5 million in 2025, a major swing from negative $32.8 million in 2024. But this was helped by settlement and merchant working capital: trade and other payables increased by $256.6 million, while trade and other receivables increased by $90.2 million. Payments companies can look extremely cash-generative when payables grow, but those balances may reverse or require liquidity if settlement timing changes.

dLocal's own adjusted free cash flow definition is the better valuation base. It starts with operating cash flow, strips out merchant working capital changes, and subtracts capex, including capitalized intangible additions. On that basis, adjusted FCF was $190.7 million in 2025 versus $90.6 million in 2024. That is still strong, but it is less spectacular than the headline IFRS operating cash flow.

| $m | 2024 | 2025 |
|---|---:|---:|
| IFRS operating cash flow | -32.8 | 415.5 |
| Merchant working-capital adjustment | 146.0 | -188.0 |
| Capex incl. intangibles | -22.6 | -36.8 |
| Adjusted free cash flow | 90.6 | 190.7 |

Key quality flags:

| Item | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Gross revenue presentation | Revenue includes merchant fees gross and processor costs below revenue, so take-rate comparisons need care. |
| Merchant working capital | Large receivable/payable balances can make operating cash flow volatile and not equivalent to owner earnings. |
| Capitalized intangibles | 2025 additions were $34.5 million, mostly internal software. These are real cash costs and should be included in maintenance/growth capex analysis. |
| SBC | 2025 SBC was $24.1 million, or about 12% of adjusted FCF. Not fatal, but not free. |
| Argentina accounting noise | Inflation adjustment, intra-group FX, treasury notes, hedging and expatriation costs complicate adjusted earnings. |
| Processor / acquirer credit risk | Top 10 processors/acquirers/collection entities processed 40% of TPV in 2025. |

The company is profitable and cash-generative, but owner earnings should be anchored to adjusted FCF rather than reported operating cash flow.

Customer concentration is high. The top 10 merchants represented 61% of 2025 revenue, and two merchants each represented more than 10% of revenue. This is not automatically bad: enterprise concentration is partly the business model. But it means retention, repricing, routing decisions, and share-of-wallet matter more than broad merchant-count metrics.

Existing merchants drove almost all 2025 revenue. Existing merchants contributed $1.08 billion of revenue, while new merchants contributed only $12.7 million in the annual filing table. Management reported NRR around 144% in 2025. That supports the land-and-expand thesis, but it also means the company is highly dependent on a small set of large global merchants growing and continuing to route volume through dLocal.

## Management, Governance, and Capital Allocation

Pedro Arnt, formerly CFO of MercadoLibre, became CEO in 2023. That is a meaningful credibility point for an emerging-market payments company, although management execution still needs to be judged by gross profit, cash conversion, and disclosure quality rather than pedigree.

The board changed materially in 2025. On 2025-12-23, dLocal announced a transition to a nine-person, majority-independent board with five independent members. Francisco Fernandez de Ybarra and Nelson Mattos joined as independent directors, while several earlier directors stepped down. The company also established Nominating & Corporate Governance, Compensation, and Product & Technology committees.

Governance remains imperfect. dLocal has dual-class shares: Class B shares carry five votes per share, while Class A shares carry one vote per share. Class B holders can effectively determine the outcome of most shareholder votes and elect a majority of directors. That may preserve founder control and long-term orientation, but it also limits public shareholder influence.

Capital allocation is now part of the thesis. dLocal returned $351 million to shareholders between 2022 and 2025 through dividends and buybacks. In 2025, it paid a $150 million dividend. In March 2026, the board declared a $57.2 million dividend, or about $0.1939 per share, payable around 2026-06-10 to holders of record on 2026-05-27. The board also authorized a $300 million share repurchase program expiring in March 2027.

The buyback can be attractive at current prices if adjusted FCF is durable. The caution is that payments infrastructure can require liquidity for settlement, collateral, faster merchant payouts, trapped funds, local regulatory requirements, and acquisitions. Excess cash is not necessarily all excess capital.

## Bull Case

The bull case starts with the business need. Emerging-market payments are not simple. Global merchants want local cards, local wallets, bank transfers, cash methods, payouts, FX, fraud tools, tax compliance, and settlement reliability in dozens of countries. dLocal provides an integrated way to access that without every merchant building local infrastructure.

The 2025 numbers show that the model can scale. TPV grew 60%, revenue grew 47%, gross profit grew 37%, adjusted EBITDA grew 47%, and adjusted FCF more than doubled. Existing merchants drove nearly all revenue, and NRR was around 144%. That suggests merchants are expanding usage rather than merely testing the platform.

The gross-profit-to-EBITDA conversion is attractive. In 2025, adjusted EBITDA was 69% of gross profit. If gross profit grows at the low-to-mid 20s and adjusted EBITDA / gross profit remains near 65% to 70%, dLocal can compound earnings and cash flow even if revenue / TPV keeps falling.

Capital return adds support. A $300 million buyback is roughly 7% of market cap at the current price, and the dividend policy returns 30% of adjusted FCF. If the business is real and growing, the current multiple is not high for a company with 20%+ gross profit growth and high returns on capital.

## Bear Case

The bear case is that dLocal is not a clean compounder, but a complex payment processor earning temporarily attractive spreads in difficult markets while those spreads get competed away.

The clearest evidence is yield compression. Revenue / TPV fell from 3.68% in 2023 to 2.68% in 2025. Gross profit / TPV fell from 1.57% to 0.99%. Management frames this as natural volume discounting and mix shift with large enterprise merchants. That may be true, but if gross profit yield keeps falling faster than TPV grows, the equity will not work.

The cash-flow picture can also mislead. IFRS operating cash flow was $415 million in 2025, but adjusted FCF was $191 million after stripping out merchant working-capital changes and capex. Trade and other payables rose by $257 million in 2025. That may reflect normal settlement timing in a growing payments business, but it is not the same as permanent owner earnings.

Customer concentration is high. Two merchants were individually above 10% of revenue in 2025. A large merchant can reroute volume, demand lower pricing, shift to a global PSP, or internalize country-specific rails. Even without churn, repricing can pressure economics.

The short-seller and litigation overhang is not gone. One New York State action was dismissed and the dismissal was affirmed, but the federal Laurenzi action remained pending in the FY2025 20-F. The court dismissal reduces one risk vector, but it does not answer every investor question about TPV, client funds, local-entity balances, or FX accounting.

## Base Case

The base case assumes dLocal remains a relevant emerging-market payments infrastructure provider but continues to share economics with large merchants and local partners. TPV grows quickly, but gross profit grows much slower than TPV. Gross profit / TPV continues to drift lower before stabilizing. Adjusted EBITDA remains around two-thirds of gross profit. Adjusted FCF conversion remains healthy but not equal to headline operating cash flow.

Under that case, FY2026 gross profit lands near management's guidance range at roughly $500 million. Operating profit grows faster than gross profit as 2025 investments scale. Adjusted FCF grows from $191 million in 2025 toward $250 million over the next couple of years. The stock can work, but not because TPV is huge. It works if gross profit dollars and adjusted FCF compound while the market becomes more comfortable with disclosure quality.

The thesis weakens if gross profit / TPV keeps falling below 0.80%, adjusted FCF fails to track net income, merchant concentration increases, or Q1/Q2 2026 show that the FY2026 guidance relies on lower-quality volume.

## Valuation

I use adjusted FCF as the primary valuation base because IFRS operating cash flow is distorted by merchant settlement working capital. I use 294.9 million shares, $424.5 million of corporate cash, and $86.7 million of financial liabilities. Giving credit for total cash would increase values, but some cash may be operational, settlement-related, restricted, collateralized, or locally trapped.

### DCF

Starting adjusted FCF is $190.7 million. The scenarios are intentionally wide:

| Scenario | Assumptions | Implied value/share |
|---|---|---:|
| Bear | FCF grows 8% for five years, then 3%; 11.5% discount rate; 2.0% terminal growth; corporate net cash. | ~$10 |
| Base | FCF grows 16% for five years, then 8%; 10.0% discount rate; 2.5% terminal growth; corporate net cash. | ~$19 |
| Bull | FCF grows 24% for five years, then 12%; 9.5% discount rate; 3.0% terminal growth; corporate net cash. | ~$34 |

The DCF is sensitive because the company is growing, margins are uncertain, and the appropriate discount rate is high given EM, FX, litigation, and disclosure risk. I would not anchor on a single target. The useful conclusion is that the current price requires a fairly modest long-term FCF growth path if the 2025 adjusted FCF base is durable.

### Reverse DCF

At an enterprise value around $3.7 billion using corporate cash, the current price implies roughly 7% annual adjusted FCF growth for 10 years, assuming a 10% discount rate and 2.5% terminal growth. That is not demanding for a company guiding to 22.5% to 27.5% gross profit growth in 2026.

The market is not saying dLocal cannot grow. It is saying some combination of reported FCF, margin durability, country risk, customer concentration, and governance deserves a discount. If dLocal proves gross profit and adjusted FCF can compound through take-rate compression, the multiple can expand.

### Multiples

At the current price, DLO trades near 13x FY2025 adjusted EBITDA and 19x FY2025 adjusted FCF on enterprise value. That is cheaper than premium global payments platforms on growth-adjusted quality, but it is not distressed. The market is already giving the company credit for being profitable and growing.

| Multiple lens | Bear | Base | Bull |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| FY2026 adjusted FCF estimate | $215M | $250M | $300M |
| Fair FCF multiple | 12x | 18x | 24x |
| Equity value/share after corporate net cash | ~$10 | ~$16 | ~$26 |

The multiple view is less generous than the DCF because it penalizes the governance and opacity discount. A fair range is probably $10 to $26 today, with the current price around the lower-middle of that range.

## Variant Perception

The variant long view is that the market is too focused on falling take rate and old short-seller allegations while missing that dLocal is compounding gross profit and cash flow through enterprise expansion. In this view, lower gross profit / TPV is the natural price of winning large merchant volume, and operating leverage plus buybacks will more than offset yield compression.

The variant bear view is that the market is still too generous because it is capitalizing adjusted FCF before fully understanding settlement-chain risk, customer concentration, country-level FX mechanics, and normalized working-capital needs. In this view, the 2025 growth reacceleration is real but lower quality than it looks.

My current view sits between those extremes. DLO is not obviously a fraud or a value trap based on the 2025 financials. It is also not clean enough to underwrite like Adyen or Visa. The right question is not "is TPV growing?" The right question is "how much gross profit and adjusted FCF does each incremental dollar of TPV create, and how durable is that cash?"

## Conclusion

dLocal is an interesting stock because the market's skepticism is not lazy. The business is growing quickly, profitable, cash-generative on an adjusted basis, and positioned in a structurally attractive corner of payments. But the discount exists for understandable reasons: falling gross profit yield, customer concentration, emerging-market FX/regulatory risk, opaque settlement working capital, dual-class governance, and the lingering short-seller/litigation shadow.

At $13.70, the stock does not require heroic assumptions. A base-case DCF around $19 per share and a reverse DCF implying only about 7% annual adjusted FCF growth suggest favorable asymmetry if the FY2025 adjusted FCF base is durable. But the downside case is also credible. If gross profit / TPV keeps compressing and cash conversion deteriorates, the stock can be cheap for a reason.

My conclusion: DLO is a high-upside but diligence-heavy payments idea. It is more attractive than the average fintech at this valuation, but it should be monitored through gross profit, adjusted FCF, and cash-quality disclosure rather than headline TPV.
