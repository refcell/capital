## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

e.l.f. Beauty has been one of the best public consumer stories of the last several years: a value-led beauty brand that repeatedly took share, moved quickly on social platforms, won retailer shelf space, and converted low absolute price points into high gross margins. FY2026 still showed that machine working. Net sales grew 25% to $1.636 billion, gross margin was 71%, operating cash flow was $212.5 million, and simple free cash flow was about $190 million.

The stock is interesting because the valuation reset while the business expanded. The shares are down sharply from the 2025 highs and now trade around 2.3x FY2027 guided sales, 11x FY2027 guided adjusted EBITDA, and 18.5x FY2027 guided adjusted EPS. That is not distressed, but it is no longer a hypergrowth multiple.

The problem is that the business is also less clean. rhode changes the company from a mostly organic mass-cosmetics compounding story into a multi-brand beauty platform with debt, goodwill, celebrity-founder dependence, Sephora exposure, a higher prestige/skincare mix, and acquisition-accounting noise. At the same time, management is responding to unit softness and price sensitivity after a $1 price increase by testing selective price reductions. The thesis now works if e.l.f. can keep the value gap wide, sustain unit growth, scale rhode/Naturium internationally, and keep FCF margins in the low-to-mid teens. It fails if the core brand slows, social commerce becomes more expensive, rhode normalizes lower, or tariff/promo pressure turns the 70% gross-margin story into an adjusted-EBITDA mirage.

### Current View

ELF is a watch-to-own idea, not a clean own. The setup is attractive because the stock now prices more like a solid consumer growth company than a flawless compounder, while the company still has rare brand, social, retail, and gross-margin assets. My base-case value is roughly $80 to $95 per share if revenue compounds high single to low double digits after FY2027 and FCF margin reaches the low-to-mid teens. The bear case is $30 to $40 if unit softness, tariffs, promo allowances, and rhode integration keep FCF power muted. The next proof point is not another sales beat by itself; it is unit velocity, gross margin after price cuts/tariffs, and FCF after debt, SBC, and acquisition costs.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

e.l.f. began as an accessible-price cosmetics brand built around the idea that consumers should not have to pay prestige prices for good color cosmetics. The company developed a playbook: spot prestige and social trends early, create fast value-priced products, launch through digital/community channels, and then scale through major retailers.

The platform broadened from e.l.f. Cosmetics into e.l.f. SKIN, Well People, Naturium, and now rhode. Naturium gave the company a proof point in skincare acquisition integration. rhode is the larger strategic move: a fast-growing prestige/skincare and hybrid makeup brand founded by Hailey Bieber, acquired in a deal announced at $1.0 billion. The transaction added Sephora and a more premium consumer relationship to a business historically known for mass value.

### Why The Company Matters

The company matters because beauty is a rare consumer category where low absolute price points, high emotional engagement, social discovery, retailer shelf competition, and gross margin can coexist. e.l.f. is not selling commodity makeup in the way a screen might imply. It is selling speed, cultural relevance, product value, and retailer productivity.

The hard question is whether those advantages are repeatable across brands. e.l.f. Cosmetics can win by being dramatically cheaper than prestige alternatives. rhode has a different job: it needs to sustain desirability, scarcity, Sephora productivity, and international growth without becoming another celebrity-brand fade.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

e.l.f. sells cosmetics and skincare through national and international retailers, e-commerce, Amazon, TikTok Shop, and brand-owned digital channels. FY2026 net sales were 76% retailer channel and 24% e-commerce. The top customers were Target at 18% of net sales, Walmart at 13%, Amazon at 11%, and Sephora at 10%.

| Revenue Stream | Description | Share / Size | Economics | Risk |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| e.l.f. Cosmetics | Core mass cosmetics brand. | Management cited about $1.8B global retail sales in FY2026. | Value-priced, high gross margin, retailer scale. | Unit softness, copyability, shelf competition. |
| e.l.f. SKIN | Skincare extension of core brand. | Management cited about $200M retail sales. | Builds category adjacency. | Less differentiated than core cosmetics. |
| Naturium | Acquired skincare brand. | Management cited nearly $250M retail sales. | Acquisition-scaling proof point. | Integration and category competition. |

| E-commerce | Brand sites, Amazon, TikTok Shop, digital platforms. | 24% of FY2026 net sales. | Data, community, product drops. | Algorithm/CAC volatility. |

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: identify beauty demand quickly -> launch value-priced products with strong social proof -> earn retailer shelf and high velocity -> keep gross margin near 70% through brand value and outsourced supply chain -> reinvest in marketing, product, and acquisitions -> convert scale into FCF per share.

The weak link is unit velocity. The model can absorb low absolute prices because gross margin is high, but it cannot absorb a broken value proposition. The Halo Glow price-reduction test, where management said a move from $18 to $14 drove strong sales lift, is important because it says the consumer still responds to value but may resist even small absolute price increases.

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Reported earnings are noisy because FY2026 GAAP income includes acquisition accounting and a large rhode contingent-consideration fair-value charge. The biggest positive is that cash conversion was better than GAAP net income: OCF was $212.5 million and FCF was $190.1 million. The biggest negative is that adjusted metrics add back real economic costs such as stock-based compensation and acquisition-related costs, and Q4 adjusted EBITDA still declined.

For valuation, I would use a blended owner-earnings approach: start with OCF minus capex, deduct a cost for SBC/dilution, and treat acquisition debt and contingent consideration as real claims. Adjusted EBITDA is useful for comparing to guidance, but it is not owner earnings.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

Bulls believe e.l.f. is still a rare consumer compounder. The company has brand heat, speed, value pricing, retail productivity, high gross margins, and a social-commerce operating model that incumbents struggle to copy. rhode gives e.l.f. a prestige brand with global demand, Sephora access, and a larger skincare/hybrid makeup opportunity. At the current valuation, the stock does not need 25% annual growth forever; it needs durable low double-digit growth and clean FCF.

### What Bears Believe

Bears believe the original e.l.f. story is slowing. The $1 price increase hurt units enough that management is now testing price reductions. Q4 adjusted EBITDA declined despite strong sales growth. rhode was expensive and young, and a celebrity-led brand can be culturally powerful until it is not. The balance sheet has more debt, the P&L has acquisition noise, securities-litigation allegations add headline risk, and the company's adjusted metrics exclude costs that common shareholders ultimately pay through dilution or cash.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The positive variant view is that the market is treating rhode as expensive M&A when it may be the bridge from a U.S. mass cosmetics story to a global multi-brand beauty platform. The negative variant view is that investors are focusing on the lower sales multiple while underweighting the change in business quality: more debt, more intangibles, more founder/celebrity dependence, more channel complexity, and a clearer sign of price sensitivity.

## 10. Bull Case

If rhode is a durable global prestige brand and e.l.f. Cosmetics keeps taking mass share, then FY2027 guidance is a reset year rather than the new long-run ceiling. Revenue can compound low-to-mid teens, gross margin can stay above 70%, and FCF margins can move into the mid-teens as acquisition costs normalize. In that world, a 22x to 28x FCF multiple is reasonable and the stock can be worth $150+ over five years.

The first evidence should show up in brand-level retail sales, Sephora productivity, international launches, e.l.f. unit growth, and FCF after price reductions.

## 11. Bear Case

If core e.l.f. unit growth is slowing and rhode's demand normalizes after the initial social/Sephora surge, then the company paid a high price for growth just as the core model got harder. Tariffs, price cuts, customer allowances, inventory, marketing spend, debt, and SBC can keep owner earnings below adjusted EBITDA. In that world, a 16x FCF multiple on about $200 million of terminal FCF supports only $30 to $40 per share.

The first evidence should show up in e.l.f. Cosmetics units, lower gross margin, rising promo allowances, inventory growth ahead of sales, and a gap between adjusted EBITDA and FCF.

## 12. Base Case

The base case assumes the e.l.f. operating system still works, but the old premium multiple was too high. FY2027 grows 12% to 14% as guided, then revenue compounds high single to low double digits as rhode, Naturium, skincare, and international offset slower core cosmetics growth. Gross margin holds around 70%, FCF margin reaches 13% to 14%, and diluted share count grows about 1% per year. That supports roughly $80 to $95 per share.

### Primary Valuation Framework

The primary framework is debt-adjusted FCF per share. Revenue multiples understate the importance of margin, tariffs, and customer allowances. GAAP P/E overstates the problem because FY2026 earnings were distorted by contingent-consideration accounting. Adjusted EBITDA is useful but incomplete because SBC, acquisition costs, debt, and dilution are real.

That looks too harsh if rhode and international are durable. It looks fair if the core brand needs price cuts and heavier marketing just to hold units.

## 15. Risks And Red Flags

The biggest risk is that the company confuses brand heat with durable economics. Beauty demand can look exceptional during a product or founder moment and then normalize quickly. The second-biggest risk is that the value proposition weakens: if consumers resist small price increases but tariffs and marketing costs rise, the P&L can lose leverage.

## 18. Conclusion

ELF is no longer the simple version of the old story. The company still has a rare combination of value positioning, cultural speed, retailer productivity, and high gross margin. But rhode, debt, tariffs, unit sensitivity, litigation headlines, insider-sale optics, and acquisition accounting make the underwriting more demanding.

My stance is watch / constructive. At $61.15, the valuation is reasonable enough that the upside can be meaningful if FY2027 guidance proves conservative and rhode scales well. I would want more evidence on unit growth, gross margin after price changes, and FCF conversion before treating it as a clean own.
