## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

UP Fintech is the Tiger Brokers parent: a Chinese-founder, Singapore-headquartered, Nasdaq-listed online brokerage serving global investors through mobile-first trading, margin, securities lending, IPO distribution, ESOP services, wealth products, virtual-asset capabilities in Hong Kong, and a growing self-clearing stack.

The stock is interesting because the reported numbers look extremely cheap while the new regulatory issue is not cosmetic. FY2025 total revenue increased 56.3% to $612.1 million, net revenue increased 62.9% to $538.7 million, GAAP net income attributable to ordinary shareholders increased 181.4% to $170.9 million, non-GAAP net income increased 164.7% to $186.5 million, customer accounts with deposits rose 14.8% to 1.254 million, and total account balance rose 45.7% to $60.8 billion. At roughly $5.01 per ADS, the stock screens at about 5.2x-5.6x trailing GAAP earnings, depending on share-count basis, and just above book value.

The problem is that on May 22, 2026, certain UP Fintech subsidiaries received notices from the CSRC Beijing Bureau finding unlicensed cross-border securities business and illegal fund/futures activity in mainland China. The bureau imposed administrative penalties of about RMB308.1 million and confiscation of about RMB103.1 million of illegal income. At the annual-report FX rate of RMB6.9931 per USD, that is roughly $58.8 million, before any secondary effects, and the company disclosed that mainland China retail client assets under consolidated accounts were about 10% of total client assets at year-end 2025.

The operating issue is bigger than the cash charge. Xinhua reported that the CSRC and seven other government departments issued a State Council-approved plan to eradicate illegal cross-border securities, futures, and fund operations over a two-year rectification period. During that period, the affected overseas institutions are reportedly prohibited from facilitating new buy orders or capital inflows for existing mainland China investors, with only sell orders and withdrawals permitted. That makes the key question customer and revenue attrition, not simply whether UP Fintech can pay the fine.

The clean underwriting question is not whether TIGR looks cheap on trailing earnings. It does. The question is whether FY2025 earnings are a sustainable international brokerage earnings base after the China penalty, or whether the market is correctly capitalizing a regulatory, ADR, VIE, and trading-cycle discount into every dollar of earnings.

### Current View

TIGR is a watchlist special situation, not a clean compounder. The operating business earned the right to be studied: FY2025 revenue, net income, funded accounts, account balances, and self-clearing progress were all strong. The price is also low enough that a finite penalty and stable non-mainland growth could create real upside.

I would not treat the trailing multiple as a standalone buy signal. The May 2026 CSRC action is the center of the report because it turns a broad China-ADR risk factor into a quantified, live impairment. My current range is deliberately wide: a bear case around $3 to $4, a regulatory-haircut base around $5 to $7, an operating/net-cash base around $8 to $10, and a bull case around $10 to $14. The stock becomes more interesting if management shows that the mainland-China issue is contained, funded-client growth remains positive outside mainland China, and normalized earnings after penalty and lower market activity remain above $120 million to $150 million.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

UP Fintech listed on Nasdaq in 2019. The company is legally a Cayman Islands holding company with subsidiaries and VIE arrangements, and it now lists principal executive offices in Singapore and Beijing. The operating brand is Tiger Brokers, and the main customer-facing platform is Tiger Trade.

The company began as an online brokerage focused on global market access for Chinese-speaking investors. It has since expanded into Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia, New Zealand, the United States, and other markets, while adding margin finance, securities lending, self-clearing, Hong Kong virtual-asset services, IPO distribution, ESOP administration, wealth products, and AI-assisted investor tools.

The story changed in 2024 and 2025 because scale finally reached the income statement. Revenue and earnings grew sharply, self-clearing reduced reliance on Interactive Brokers, client assets rose, and the company showed operating leverage. The May 2026 CSRC action changed the story again: UP Fintech is now a profitable international broker with a live China regulatory overhang.

### Why The Company Matters

UP Fintech matters because it sits at the intersection of three attractive but fragile pools: cross-border investing demand from Chinese-speaking investors, digital brokerage platform economics, and high-margin ancillary services like margin, securities lending, IPO distribution, and wealth products.

That positioning is valuable if it can be made compliant and geographically diversified. It is dangerous if the best customer-acquisition engine depends on regulators tolerating cross-border brokerage behavior that they later restrict.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

UP Fintech earns revenue from commissions, interest income, financing-service fees, and other revenue. Commissions and interest income are the two largest pools. In FY2025, commissions were 43.6% of total revenue, interest income was 42.0%, other revenue was 12.7%, and financing-service fees were 1.7%.

The revenue model is attractive in strong markets because higher account balances, trading volume, margin balances, securities lending, IPO activity, and wealth services can compound together. It is also cyclically exposed because commissions and margin/lending economics depend on trading activity, asset prices, interest rates, investor sentiment, and regulatory permissions.

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: acquire funded customers -> grow account balances -> increase trading, margin, securities lending, IPO, wealth, and corporate-services engagement -> self-clear more activity -> capture more gross economics per user -> reinvest into licenses, product, compliance, and market expansion.

The weak link is regulatory permission. The same cross-border investing demand that made Tiger valuable also makes the company sensitive to mainland China, Hong Kong, Singapore, U.S., Australia, New Zealand, and other regulators. The May 2026 CSRC action shows that this risk is not theoretical.

### Regulatory Footprint

The regulatory footprint is a source of both value and risk. Singapore is the headquarters and Tiger Brokers Singapore is licensed by MAS for capital-markets products, product financing, custodial services, and exempt financial-adviser activity. Hong Kong subsidiaries hold SFC Type 1, 2, 4, 5, and 9 licenses, with Type 1 upgraded to include virtual-asset dealing for professional investors and Type 9 covering asset management. U.S. subsidiaries include SEC-registered broker-dealers and FINRA members, and TradeUP Securities is part of the self-clearing infrastructure. Australia has an AFSL via Tiger Brokers AU, while New Zealand entities are FSPR-registered financial-service providers and AML/CFT reporting entities, but the 20-F notes they are not licensed by a New Zealand regulator to provide client money or property services.

This breadth helps the international-growth thesis. It also means the company must satisfy multiple regulators while dealing with a live mainland China enforcement action. The issue is not "is Tiger licensed anywhere?" It is whether the specific customer-acquisition, funding, product, and cross-border activity that drove growth is licensed in each relevant jurisdiction.

That distinction matters after the May 2026 CSRC action. The company filing quantified the penalties and mainland-client asset exposure. Xinhua's description of the broader plan added the operating mechanics: a two-year rectification period, no new buy orders or capital inflows for affected mainland investors, sell orders and withdrawals permitted, and eventual shutdown of mainland-targeted websites, trading apps, and supporting servers. The report therefore treats mainland-related earnings as impaired unless management proves otherwise.

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Headline operating cash flow is not a clean owner-earnings number for UP Fintech. The 20-F shows FY2025 operating cash flow of $1.317 billion, but that was heavily affected by customer receivables and payables. Receivables from customers increased by about $731 million while payables to customers increased by about $1.521 billion. A brokerage balance sheet can create very large operating-cash-flow swings that are not equivalent to distributable free cash flow.

Net income is a better starting point, but it still needs haircuts for trading-cycle sensitivity, regulatory costs, capital needs, and dilution. FY2025 share-based compensation was $15.6 million, only 2.6% of revenue, so SBC is not the main earnings-quality problem. The main issue is whether a $170.9 million GAAP earnings base survives lower trading activity and the China regulatory reset.

The broker-specific stress points are interest spread, collateral, and credit. FY2025 interest income rose to $257.0 million and interest expense rose to $73.4 million, leaving about $183.6 million of net interest contribution before operating expenses. That contribution depends on margin financing, securities lending, client balances, rates, and funding access. The allowance on customer receivables fell from $15.3 million at 2024-12-31 to $5.1 million at 2025-12-31, and full-year G&A declined partly because bad-debt expense decreased. That is positive if credit quality improved, but it also means a bad-debt normalization would hit earnings directly.

### Base Case Model

### Reverse Valuation

At about $5.01, TIGR does not need heroic growth if FY2025 earnings are close to sustainable. Using the 190 million ADS denominator, the market is valuing the company at roughly $952 million, or 5.6x FY2025 GAAP earnings and about 1.1x book. The reverse problem is that if normalized post-penalty earnings are only $70 million to $90 million, the stock is closer to 10.6x-13.6x earnings before giving credit for any net cash, which is less obviously cheap for a China-linked broker with live regulatory risk.

The current price appears to be saying: "The business is real, but the earnings base is not fully bankable." That is a defensible market view. A more optimistic reverse DCF says the price already embeds a severe revenue or margin reset: after haircutting cash/term deposits for convertibles, the CSRC charge, and regulatory-capital needs, the market is valuing the operating business at only a few hundred million dollars. The issue is not whether the stock screens cheap. It does. The issue is whether the cash can be treated as equity value and whether post-penalty earnings stay above $120 million to $150 million.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

Tianhua Wu is founder, chairman, CEO, and the controlling economic actor. The 20-F beneficial-ownership table shows him with 244.1 million Class A shares and all 97.6 million Class B shares, representing about 48.6% voting power as of March 31, 2026. Each Class B share carries 20 votes versus one vote for each Class A share.

The upside of this structure is founder continuity and product speed. The downside is minority-shareholder leverage. The May 2026 CSRC action included a warning and RMB1.25 million penalty for Mr. Wu as director, CEO, and controlling person, which makes governance and regulatory accountability more central to the thesis.

Capital allocation has been mixed. The company has raised equity, issued/maintained convertibles, invested in licenses and self-clearing, and repurchased modest shares under an older authorization. The $69.9 million due to related parties is notable but should be read precisely: the 20-F says it represents directors' and officers' cash account balances, while related-party transactions also include ordinary-course brokerage services and financing. That still belongs on the monitoring list because the number moved sharply, but it is not the same as an unexplained related-party funding liability. The best use of capital now is not aggressive buybacks. It is regulatory resolution, capital strength, and proving that non-mainland growth can fund the next stage without more dilution.

## 11. Bull Case

The bull case is that FY2025 was not a fluke. UP Fintech has become a scaled, profitable, international brokerage with improving unit economics and a valuation that is lower than the business quality deserves.

The evidence is strong enough to matter. Revenue grew 56%, net revenue grew 63%, net income grew 181%, account balances grew 46%, funded customers grew 15%, and the company beat its 2025 funded-client guidance. Self-clearing progress should structurally improve economics, and non-mainland markets like Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia, and New Zealand provide a path to reduce mainland China dependence over time.

If the CSRC action is finite, the math changes quickly. A one-time $58 million regulatory hit is painful but manageable against $865 million of equity and $170 million-plus of FY2025 net income. If normalized earnings stay in the $160 million to $200 million range and the stock receives even a 10x-12x multiple, the upside is large from $5.

## 12. Bear Case

The bear case is that TIGR is cheap for the right reasons. The company just disclosed a major regulatory penalty tied to unlicensed cross-border securities, fund, and futures activity in mainland China. Xinhua's description of the broader CSRC plan points to a two-year wind-down of affected illegal cross-border activity, with restrictions on new buy orders and capital inflows for existing mainland investors. That raises the question of how much customer growth, asset gathering, and revenue depended on practices that are now impaired.

The bear case does not require the business to fail. It only requires earnings to normalize. If mainland client acquisition is constrained, if customer assets leave, if trading volume cools, if margin/lending balances decline, or if compliance costs rise, the $170.9 million FY2025 earnings base can fall materially. A brokerage earning $70 million to $90 million through the cycle, with China/VIE/founder-control risk, is not obviously cheap at a roughly $950 million market cap.

There are also structural risks. The company uses a VIE structure. Regulators in multiple jurisdictions supervise its brokerage businesses. Self-clearing improves economics but adds capital, liquidity, and operational exposure. AI and virtual-asset products create new compliance surfaces. Founder control limits minority-shareholder leverage. The company has convertible debt, dilution, and related-party brokerage-account balances that require monitoring.

## 13. Base Case

The base case is that UP Fintech remains a profitable brokerage, but the market is right to haircut it. The company keeps growing outside mainland China, but the CSRC action increases compliance cost and reduces confidence in the durability of the China-linked growth engine. FY2025 earnings are directionally real but not a clean steady-state base.

In this case, normalized earnings fall to $120 million to $150 million, the stock deserves a mid/high-single-digit earnings multiple, and book value provides some floor. That produces a fair-value range around $5 to $7. The current price is not expensive, but I would want evidence before calling it deeply mispriced.

## 19. Conclusion

TIGR is worth watching because it combines a real business improvement with a real regulatory shock. FY2025 was strong enough that dismissing the company as a broken China ADR is too simplistic. Revenue, funded customers, client assets, margin/lending balances, self-clearing, and earnings all improved materially.

The stock is also not obviously mispriced just because the trailing P/E is low. The May 2026 CSRC filing is the center of the thesis. It is large enough to matter financially, and more important, it raises the question of what part of Tiger's historical growth engine was dependent on activity that regulators no longer tolerate.

My current stance is watch. The stock becomes interesting if post-penalty disclosures show stable client assets, continued non-mainland growth, and normalized earnings above $120 million to $150 million. It becomes a value trap if the penalty is only the first visible sign of a broader impairment to customer acquisition, trading activity, and regulatory permission.
