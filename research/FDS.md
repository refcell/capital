## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

FactSet looks like a former quality compounder that the market has started to treat as an AI-disrupted, low-growth financial-data utility. That is why it is worth studying. The stock has fallen roughly 50% from its 52-week high, yet the business still has high recurring subscription revenue, ASV retention above 95%, client retention around 91%, 9,101 clients, 241,352 users, strong free cash flow, and a long record of returning capital.

The surface issue is that FactSet is cheaper. The real issue is whether it deserves to be cheaper. The company has moved from a simple desktop-data and analytics story into a broader workflow, enterprise data, market infrastructure, wealth, private markets, AI, and OMS platform. That transition should increase embeddedness if it works. It also increases execution risk, capital intensity, cloud spend, content-cost exposure, acquisition integration needs, and competition from both incumbents and AI-native tools.

At $232, the stock trades around 14.0x clean EV / TTM reported free cash flow and roughly 15.6x clean EV / TTM owner free cash flow after stock-based compensation. That is not a demanding price for a business that can grow owner earnings mid-single digits with stable margins. It is too high if AI compresses seats, customers consolidate vendors, content and cloud costs keep rising, and FactSet becomes a 1%-3% grower with a lower terminal multiple.

The thesis works if organic ASV can grow at least mid-single digits, adjusted margins can stabilize without hiding too much economic cost, and AI/data products make FactSet more useful inside client workflows rather than less necessary. It fails if the old workstation economics erode faster than enterprise data, wealth, market infrastructure, and private-market workflow products can offset.

### Current View

FDS is a watch-to-own idea at $232. The risk/reward is better than it has been in years, but this is not a clean "own it and forget it" setup. The base case is roughly $300 to $360 per share if FactSet remains a 4%-5% revenue grower with mid/high-20s owner-FCF margins and an 18x terminal owner-FCF multiple. The bear case is roughly $190 to $220 if growth fades toward 2%-3%, owner-FCF margins settle lower, and the terminal multiple compresses to the low teens. The bull case can exceed $500 if AI and enterprise workflow products extend the moat, revenue grows mid-single digits or better, and owner-FCF margins expand.

The reason to care is that the market may be pricing a durable business as if its terminal value is already impaired. The reason to be careful is that the bear case is not imaginary: organic ASV growth has slowed from the earlier post-pandemic pace, Q2 FY2026 margin pressure was real, H1 FY2026 capital return exceeded free cash flow, buy-side exposure is high, and an unresolved internal-control material weakness remains in IT general controls supporting revenue, accounts receivable, and deferred revenue processes.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

FactSet was founded in 1978 by Howard Wille and Charles Snyder as a financial data and analytics company for investment professionals. It became public in the 1990s and built its reputation as a flexible, client-service-heavy alternative to Bloomberg, Reuters, and other institutional data platforms. The classic FactSet product was a workstation that combined company data, estimates, screening, analytics, charting, portfolio tools, Excel integration, and support for buy-side and sell-side workflows.

That original story is still important, but it is incomplete. FactSet now sells data feeds, APIs, managed services, analytics, portfolio and risk tools, wealth-management solutions, banking workflow tools, private-market products, AI-enabled search and productivity tools, and market-infrastructure identifiers through CUSIP Global Services. The company also bought LiquidityBook in February 2025 and Irwin in November 2024, pushing further into OMS/IBOR/FIX connectivity, investor relations, and capital-markets workflow.

The 2022 acquisition of CUSIP Global Services was the biggest strategic change in recent history. It added a market-infrastructure asset with identifier economics that are different from workstation seats. It also added leverage, amortization, and regulatory/legal sensitivity around identifier pricing and access. The current thesis therefore cannot be reduced to "will terminals grow?" It is about whether FactSet can combine desktop, enterprise data, market infrastructure, AI, and workflow into a durable bundle.

Management is also in transition. Sanoke Viswanathan became CEO on September 8, 2025. In March 2026, FactSet moved Kate Stepp from CTO to Chief AI Officer and named Bob Stolte CTO. In April 2026, CFO Helen Shan transitioned out and Joshua Warren became CFO. These changes are material because the next several years require both product reinvention and disciplined capital allocation.

### Why The Company Matters

FactSet matters because financial professionals do not only need raw data. They need clean, licensed, permissioned, auditable data inside research, portfolio management, risk, reporting, trading, wealth, banking, compliance, and client-service workflows. The problem is hard because the data is fragmented, rights are complex, calculations must be trusted, customers have entrenched workflows, and mistakes are costly.

The company is not as strategically dominant as Bloomberg, but it is deeply embedded in many institutional workflows. Its value proposition is breadth, flexibility, client support, open architecture, analytics, and workflow integration. That can be powerful in an AI world if AI increases the need for trusted and permissioned data. It can be weaker in an AI world if users ask natural-language interfaces for answers and no longer care which desktop or application sits in front of them.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

FactSet sells negotiated subscriptions to financial data, analytics, workflow software, feeds, APIs, identifiers, and managed services. Revenue is largely recurring and recognized over time. The core commercial metric is Annual Subscription Value, or ASV, which captures the annualized value of subscription services at a point in time. FactSet changed its disclosed growth metric in FY2025 from "Organic ASV plus professional services" to "Organic ASV," so historical comparisons need care.

The customer base is institutional. FactSet serves buy-side investment firms, sell-side banks, wealth managers, corporations, private-market investors, investor-relations teams, and market-infrastructure users. In FY2025, buy-side customers represented about 82% of organic ASV and sell-side customers represented about 18%. No single client represented more than 3.5% of revenue, which makes the revenue base diversified even though end-market exposure to asset-management budgets is high.

| Revenue Stream / Driver | Description | Economics | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Workstations and applications | Research, screening, estimates, portfolio analytics, risk, performance, reporting, banking, wealth, and productivity tools. | Recurring subscription seats and modules with high switching costs. | Seat pressure, AI interfaces, Bloomberg/S&P/LSEG competition. |
| Enterprise data, feeds, and APIs | Licensed data delivered into client systems, warehouses, workflows, and models. | Potentially sticky, scalable, and less tied to desktop UI. | Data-platform competition and content-rights costs. |
| Market infrastructure / CGS | CUSIP identifiers and related reference-data infrastructure. | Distinct market-infrastructure economics and pricing power. | Regulatory, litigation, customer pushback, and integration risk. |
| Workflow and trading | LiquidityBook OMS/IBOR/FIX connectivity and portfolio/trading workflow. | Deeper system-of-work embedding. | Integration risk and crowded trading-tech market. |
| Wealth and private markets | Advisor workstations, dashboards, proposals, Cobalt, Valutico partnership, Irwin, and private-market workflows. | Cross-sell and new user pools outside classic institutional research. | Product breadth can outrun sales focus. |
| Professional services / managed services | Implementation, outsourcing, data operations, and workflow support. | Helps adoption and retention, but lower multiple than software/data subscriptions. | Labor intensity and margin pressure. |

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: license high-value data and analytics to financial professionals -> embed FactSet into daily research, portfolio, risk, trading, wealth, banking, and reporting workflows -> renew at high ASV retention -> cross-sell additional data, APIs, analytics, AI tools, and workflow modules -> convert subscription revenue into free cash flow -> return cash through dividends and buybacks.

The weak link is whether the interface to financial data is changing faster than FactSet can adapt. A desktop seat model is vulnerable if AI makes data access easier and cheaper. A trusted data and workflow platform is more valuable if AI increases demand for clean, governed, licensed, permissioned data. FactSet is trying to move toward the second model; the stock is now priced as if the first model is under pressure.

FactSet does not disclose a full cohort retention bridge, CAC, payback, or net revenue retention the way a SaaS company might. ASV retention, client retention, client count, user count, ASV per client, and ASV per user are the best public proxies.

### Competitive Position

FactSet competes in an oligopoly plus a growing field of specialists. Bloomberg dominates premium terminals and trading/news workflows. LSEG/Refinitiv has enormous data, trading, index, and workflow assets. S&P Global Market Intelligence competes through Capital IQ, Visible Alpha, iLEVEL, Kensho, indices, ratings, and data. Morningstar owns manager research, PitchBook, public/private-company data, and wealth/research surfaces. MSCI and Moody's own strong analytics, ratings, index, ESG, and risk franchises. AlphaSense, Tegus, Canalyst, OpenBB, Snowflake, Databricks, and AI-native tools attack pieces of the workflow.

The competitive threat is not simply that a cheaper terminal exists. The deeper threat is that financial data access may become less tied to a specific front-end application. If a portfolio manager, banker, analyst, or advisor gets a trusted answer from an AI layer connected to multiple data sources, the value captured by the old workstation can fall. FactSet's answer is to become one of the trusted data and workflow layers behind that AI experience.

| Competitor Type | Examples | FactSet Advantage | FactSet Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Premium terminal / trading ecosystem | Bloomberg | Flexible analytics, service, open architecture, pricing, portfolio workflows. | Bloomberg has unmatched network, news, messaging, and trading gravity. |
| Data and index incumbents | LSEG, S&P Global, MSCI, Moody's | Broad client relationships and workflow flexibility. | Larger peers own critical datasets, indices, ratings, and benchmarks. |
| Research / private-market platforms | Morningstar, PitchBook, AlphaSense, Tegus, Canalyst | Multi-asset data and workflow breadth. | Point tools can be better in specific workflows. |
| Data clouds and AI platforms | Snowflake, Databricks, hyperscalers | Licensed content, calculations, and financial workflow context. | Data platforms may own the AI work surface. |
| Open-source / lower-cost tools | OpenBB, direct APIs, in-house data teams | Enterprise support, permissioning, and compliance. | Good-enough tools pressure price and UI value. |

### Moat Assessment

The moat is strongest in switching costs, workflow embedding, data breadth, enterprise support, and market-infrastructure identifiers. It is weaker in the desktop user interface and generic AI features. FactSet's data may be valuable, but data moats are only durable when the company controls unique content, licenses it effectively, integrates it into workflows, and can raise price without causing churn.

|---|---|---|---|
| Switching costs | Daily workflows, Excel models, portfolio analytics, reporting, data permissions, implementation work. | Workstations can be rationalized when budgets tighten. | Real, but not absolute. |

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

FactSet's earnings quality is good, but not pristine. The good part is straightforward: the company has recurring revenue, high retention, diversified customers, strong free cash flow, and modest stock-based compensation compared with many software companies. Reported FCF conversion is strong, and TTM reported FCF of about $683 million is close enough to net income that the business is not obviously accounting-driven.

The caution is that "adjusted" is doing real work. FY2025 GAAP operating income of $748 million bridged to adjusted operating income of $843 million through intangible amortization, acquisition/divestiture costs, a sales-tax dispute, executive-search costs, and restructuring/severance. Q2 FY2026 adjusted results added back intangible amortization, CEO compensation costs, India labor-code reform, acquisition/divestiture costs, asset impairment, and restructuring/severance. Some of those are genuinely unusual. Others are part of the ongoing cost of an acquisitive, software-and-data platform.

Capitalized internal-use software also matters. FactSet defines FCF as operating cash flow less purchases of property, equipment, leasehold improvements, and capitalized internal-use software. That is the right direction, because internal-use software is part of the productive asset base. Rising software capitalization and amortization can make GAAP margins and owner earnings diverge from the adjusted narrative.

The other quality issue is internal control. FactSet disclosed a material weakness in IT general controls supporting revenue, accounts receivable, and deferred revenue processes, and it had not been fully remediated as of Q2 FY2026. I do not treat that as proof that revenue is misstated. I do treat it as a reason not to overpay for adjusted precision.

Capital return also needs discipline. H1 FY2026 free cash flow was $276 million, while buybacks and dividends were about $385 million combined. That is not dangerous for a company with FactSet's cash generation, but repurchasing large amounts above the current share price while debt maturities approach is not obviously value-maximizing. Buybacks are attractive at $232 if the base case is right; they were less compelling at the Q2 average repurchase price of $250.

The model is sensitive to terminal assumptions because FactSet is a high-margin recurring business. That is unavoidable. The practical question is whether the company deserves a low-teens terminal owner-FCF multiple or a high-teens multiple. The answer depends on ASV durability, AI disruption, and margin trajectory.

### What The Current Price Implies

At $232, the market is not underwriting much growth. Using clean financial EV of about $9.54 billion and TTM owner free cash flow after SBC of about $613 million, the stock trades at roughly 15.6x EV / owner FCF. In the reverse DCF, using an 8% discount rate and a 16x terminal owner-FCF multiple, the current EV implies approximately 0% long-term owner-FCF CAGR. At a 14x terminal multiple, it implies about 1% long-term owner-FCF CAGR.

That is the source of the opportunity. The market does not need FactSet to become a hypergrowth AI company for the stock to work. It needs the company to avoid terminal impairment. If FactSet can grow owner FCF at roughly 4%-5% and retain a mid/high-teens terminal multiple, $232 is too low. If owner FCF growth falls toward zero and the terminal multiple falls toward 10x-12x, $232 is fair or high.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### The Debate

The bull case is that the market has overreacted to AI. FactSet has sticky workflows, high retention, trusted licensed data, market-infrastructure assets, and an increasingly open enterprise-data platform. AI may make FactSet more valuable if customers need governed data, auditable calculations, permissioning, and workflow integration. Public bull commentary from Ocular Capital, visible through a Yahoo summary, frames FactSet as a sticky workflow business that has been mispriced by AI fears. Ariel also initiated or added to FactSet exposure in public fund commentary, citing embedded workflows and switching costs.

The bear case is that the old moat is weakening. Conestoga sold its position and cited slower organic growth, asset-manager budget pressure, Bloomberg/S&P/new fintech competition, rising technology and content costs, and valuation. Morningstar downgraded its moat rating to none and cut fair value because generative AI increases uncertainty about the competitive future. Portage published an AI disruption paper arguing that LLMs are the first credible chance to simplify financial-data access at scale. Reuters-syndicated coverage also noted that AI fears have weighed on financial-data and information-service multiples.

Both sides are partly right. FactSet is not a melting ice cube today; organic ASV was +6.7% in Q2 and retention remains high. But the historical "data compounder" multiple required investors to believe the interface, workflow, and data bundle were durable. AI has made that assumption less automatic.

## 10. Bull Case

The bull case is not that FactSet suddenly becomes a high-growth software stock. It is that the market has priced the company as if terminal impairment is already likely, while the actual evidence still shows a sticky, cash-generative, mid-single-digit recurring-revenue platform.

Organic ASV grew 6.7% in Q2 FY2026, ASV retention remained above 95%, client retention was 91%, client count grew to 9,101, and users grew to 241,352. Those are not broken-business metrics. The company also has multiple growth levers beyond desktop seats: enterprise data, APIs, managed services, wealth, private markets, CGS/CUSIP, LiquidityBook workflow, Irwin, AI-enabled document search, and data-cloud distribution.

The AI bull case is credible if AI makes trusted data more important. An LLM does not magically solve licensing, permissioning, lineage, auditability, entitlements, identifiers, estimates, portfolio calculations, compliance, or integration into client systems. FactSet can be a supplier of governed financial data and workflow context to AI products, whether those products sit inside FactSet applications or client environments.

At the current price, the valuation hurdle is low. A business with roughly $613 million of TTM owner FCF, net debt near $1.08 billion, and mid-single-digit owner-earnings growth can be worth much more than $232 per share if it deserves an 18x terminal owner-FCF multiple. The base-case DCF output is about $361 per share; I would frame the practical base-case range as $300 to $360 to avoid false precision.

## 11. Bear Case

The strongest bear case is that FactSet is changing from a durable compounder into a slower-growth, higher-cost, more contested workflow platform. The revenue base is still sticky, but the incremental economics are less certain.

There are several pieces of evidence. Organic ASV growth is mid-single digit, not high single digit. Q2 FY2026 GAAP and adjusted operating margins both declined materially. Buy-side clients represented 82% of FY2025 organic ASV, leaving the company exposed to asset-manager budgets, passive pressure, fee compression, hedge-fund launches/closures, and vendor consolidation. Content suppliers and cloud suppliers create input-cost risk; FY2025 included two third-party content suppliers above 10% of data/content costs, and one cloud supplier provided the majority of cloud support.

AI is a real risk because it may reduce the value of the front-end interface. If users can ask a natural-language agent for analysis, and that agent can pull data from multiple sources, FactSet may lose pricing power on desktop seats. The company can still sell data, but data-only economics may be more competitive and less differentiated than workflow economics.

The quality-of-earnings bear case is that adjusted EPS flatters the business. Intangible amortization, acquisition costs, CEO compensation costs, India labor-code costs, impairment, capitalized software, and SBC may not all be one-time. H1 capital return exceeded FCF. The internal-control material weakness remains unresolved. Debt maturities in 2027 and 2028 require planning. Low insider ownership also reduces the owner-operator comfort level.

## 12. Base Case

The base case is that FactSet remains relevant, but not immune. Organic ASV grows around 5%-6%, revenue grows 4%-5%, owner-FCF margins settle in the mid/high 20s, and buybacks reduce shares modestly. AI becomes both a cost and a product feature. It does not destroy the business, but it also does not produce a dramatic growth reacceleration quickly.

In that case, FDS is worth more than the current price but deserves a wider uncertainty band than the old compounder narrative. I would underwrite $300 to $360 as a reasonable base-case fair-value range, anchored by owner FCF and a mid/high-teens terminal multiple. ## 18. Conclusion

FactSet is not a broken company. It is a high-retention, cash-generative financial-data and workflow platform trading at a multiple that already discounts a meaningful amount of AI and growth anxiety. That makes the stock interesting at $232.

The conclusion is constructive but conditional. The current price appears to imply very low long-term owner-earnings growth. If FactSet can maintain mid-single-digit organic ASV growth, stabilize GAAP margins, grow owner FCF, and show that AI/data products defend or expand workflows, the stock should be worth materially more than the current price. My base-case range is $300 to $360, with a bear range around $190 to $220 and a bull case above $500.

I would not ignore the bear case. AI can change how financial professionals access data. Margins are under pressure. Adjusted earnings require scrutiny. Capital return exceeded H1 FCF. The internal-control material weakness needs remediation. Management is new. The next few quarters matter.

The cleanest decision rule is simple: keep watching organic ASV, users, GAAP margin, and owner FCF. If those hold, FDS looks mispriced. If they weaken together, the market's lower multiple is probably deserved.
