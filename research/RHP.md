## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Ryman is not a normal hotel REIT. The core business is a concentrated portfolio of giant convention-oriented destination resorts, mostly under the Gaylord flag and managed by Marriott, with an attached entertainment business that includes the Grand Ole Opry, the Ryman Auditorium, Ole Red, Block 21 in Austin, and related country-music and events assets. That combination gives RHP a stranger mix than the average lodging name: less transient room demand, more contracted group business, more food-and-beverage and ancillary spend, more embedded capex, and more strategic optionality around experiences and branded entertainment (company website, accessed 2026-06-14; RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

That structure explains both why the company has looked so resilient since the post-COVID recovery and why the stock is not obviously cheap. The market already understands that Gaylord-scale convention assets are scarce, that group bookings create better visibility than ordinary room-night businesses, and that Marriott management plus destination-quality meeting space can support premium rates. The real debate is whether that quality deserves more upside from here once leverage, ongoing capital spending, acquisition-driven growth, and the mixed economics of the entertainment segment are fully considered.

The stock is worth studying now because RHP sits at a junction of quality and valuation. The business continues to post healthy revenue growth, consensus still expects another year of top-line expansion and higher FFO in 2026, and analysts remain broadly constructive. But the average one-year price target is only about $123, essentially flat to the current stock price, which suggests the Street likes the company more than it likes the stock at this level (StockAnalysis forecast, accessed 2026-06-14).

### The Core Debate

The debate is not whether Ryman owns good assets. It does. The debate is whether those good assets are already fully capitalized in the current multiple. If the company is primarily a scarce convention-resort platform with durable pricing, room-night visibility, and long-run FFO growth, then a mid-teens forward FFO multiple can still work. If it is instead a high-quality but capital-hungry lodging REIT whose growth increasingly comes from acquisitions and reinvestment rather than pure same-store operating leverage, then the current price may already reflect most of the good news.

### Current View

RHP looks like a good business at a mostly fair price. My base case is watch rather than own. The company has a differentiated asset base, visible group demand drivers, and a rare combination of resort-scale convention real estate plus branded entertainment. But the stock already trades like a good company. With consensus FY2026 FFO/share at $8.96, the market is not offering a clear dislocation; it is offering a respectable yield, moderate growth, and limited room for mistakes (StockAnalysis statistics and forecast, accessed 2026-06-14).

The stock becomes more compelling if either the price weakens materially or the company demonstrates that the recent acquisition cycle, especially JW Marriott Desert Ridge, can raise per-share FFO and not just absolute EBITDA. Until then, RHP looks better as a quality watchlist compounder than a high-conviction bargain.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Ryman’s current identity is the product of a long evolution from a media and entertainment heritage into a specialized lodging REIT. Today’s core lodging platform is built around very large convention-focused destination resorts, especially the Gaylord portfolio, while the entertainment side preserves the company’s legacy connection to country music and destination experiences. That mix still matters because the company is not just renting hotel rooms; it is selling meeting destinations, event ecosystems, and branded entertainment experiences tied to place (company website, accessed 2026-06-14).

The lodging portfolio is unusually concentrated but unusually strategic. As of the company’s public description, the hotel portfolio includes five Gaylord convention center resorts, two large JW Marriott resorts, and two ancillary hotels adjacent to Gaylord assets, for a combined 12,364 rooms and more than 3 million square feet of indoor and outdoor meeting space. The 2023 10-K described the core owned network as five Gaylord resorts totaling 9,917 rooms, plus ancillary and later-added JW Marriott assets; by 2026 the public company presentation reflects a broader portfolio that includes the Phoenix Desert Ridge and San Antonio Hill Country properties (company website, accessed 2026-06-14; RHP 2023 10-K filed 2024-02-23; RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

The major recent strategic change was expansion beyond the original Gaylord-heavy footprint. The company acquired JW Marriott Hill Country in 2023 and added JW Marriott Desert Ridge in June 2025. The Q1 2026 10-Q shows Desert Ridge contributing $73.9 million of revenue in its first comparable quarter inside the portfolio, which means the acquisition is already large enough to matter to the consolidated growth story (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

### Why The Company Matters

Ryman matters because very large group-oriented convention hotels are not easily replicated. Many lodging REITs compete in fragmented transient, leisure, or select-service categories where rooms are more commodity-like and competition is local. RHP instead competes in destination-scale meetings and conventions, where the asset itself is part of the moat. A customer does not casually replace a Gaylord-scale convention booking with a random full-service hotel.

The entertainment assets matter for a different reason. Opry Entertainment Group, or OEG, gives RHP a second economic identity that is not purely lodging-linked. That does not make the stock a sum-of-the-parts gold mine by itself, but it does create a differentiated brand halo around Nashville and country-music entertainment and gives management optionality around live events, venues, restaurants, and destination traffic (company website, accessed 2026-06-14; RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Ryman earns most of its money from its Hospitality segment, which includes room revenue, food and beverage, and other ancillary hotel revenue at large destination resorts. The important point is that group business drives the system. This is not mainly a transient room-rate trade. It is a meetings-and-conventions trade, where room blocks, banquet and catering spend, meeting space economics, and on-property ancillary spending all matter together (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

The other revenue engine is Entertainment. In Q1 2026, Hospitality generated $585.4 million of segment revenue while Entertainment generated $79.2 million, so Hospitality represented about 88% of total segment revenue and Entertainment about 12%. That already tells the underwriting story: OEG is strategically differentiating, but the stock is still primarily a lodging REIT with an attached entertainment business, not the other way around (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Hospitality rooms | Convention and destination room revenue across Gaylord and JW Marriott assets. | Largest profit pool. | High operating leverage when occupancy and ADR hold. | Group travel and corporate-event cyclicality. |
| Food and beverage / ancillary | Catering, banquet, outlet, and meeting-related spend tied to group volume. | Material inside hospitality. | Good complement to room blocks and convention density. | Mix-sensitive and labor-intensive. |
| Entertainment | Venues, attractions, media, restaurants, and events in OEG. | $79.2M Q1 2026 revenue. | Can deepen destination appeal and diversify revenue. | Lower margins, volatility, and execution complexity. |
| Acquired resort contribution | Hill Country and Desert Ridge expansion. | Desert Ridge alone was $73.9M of Q1 2026 revenue. | Adds scale and new geographies. | Acquisition underwriting and financing risk. |

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: own scarce convention-oriented resort assets in attractive leisure and group destinations -> book large group events well in advance -> monetize not only rooms but catering, meeting space, and ancillary spend -> convert that revenue into FFO and dividends -> reinvest in expansions, renovations, and selective acquisitions that deepen the platform.

The weak link is capital intensity. The basic resort model is attractive, but it is not effortless. These are expensive assets to maintain, expand, renovate, and occasionally acquire. The engine works best when pricing, occupancy, and outside-the-room spend all cooperate. It works less well if the company must lean too hard on acquisition financing or persistent capex to keep the multiple justified.

### Unit Economics / Customer Economics

Customer economics here are not about CAC and churn, but about booking quality, rate, spend per attendee, and asset utilization. Management highlights ADR, RevPAR, Total RevPAR, and net definite group room nights booked because those are the real unit-level signals for the resort platform (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

The public Q1 filing gives useful operating markers. Same-store ADR in Hospitality, excluding the Desert Ridge acquisition, rose 5.1% year over year. Same-store group rooms traveled fell 4.1%, but outside-the-room spend rose 3.2%, suggesting the mix of group activity remained favorable. Gaylord Opryland delivered 16.5% growth in total revenue and Total RevPAR, while Gaylord Palms grew 10.5% on the same basis. Desert Ridge posted $357.42 of RevPAR and $863.95 of Total RevPAR in the quarter, showing why management wanted the asset (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

### Product / Segment Breakdown

RHP’s value is not spread evenly across everything it owns. The core value driver is still the Hospitality segment, and within that, the largest convention resorts matter most. Entertainment is real, but at current scale it is best viewed as a supporting strategic differentiator and a smaller earnings contributor rather than the primary valuation anchor.

### Competitive Position

Ryman’s competitive advantage is partly brand and partly geometry. The geometry matters more. There are not many assets in the United States that can replicate the meeting-space scale, room count, and destination feel of the Gaylord resorts. That gives the company bargaining power with large group customers and supports higher-value bookings that pull through into catering and other spend.

Marriott management is another quiet edge. Ryman gets a strong brand and operating partner without being a traditional asset-light operator itself. That does not remove execution risk, but it does mean the operating system around these hotels is more institutional and repeatable than it would be under a weaker manager (company website and 2023 10-K, accessed 2026-06-14).

The main competitive risk is not that another operator builds five giant lookalike assets overnight. It is that corporate event demand softens, large-group budgets compress, or new supply in destination group markets chips away at pricing power. In that sense the competition is as much macro and event-budget competition as it is direct hotel competition.

### Moat Assessment

The moat is strongest in asset scarcity and customer use-case fit, weaker in capital efficiency. The market may underrate just how hard it is to recreate large, destination-oriented, non-gaming convention resorts at scale. But it may overrate how cleanly that scarcity translates into per-share compounding once leverage, capex, and M&A are accounted for.

### Balance Sheet And Capital Structure

The balance sheet is manageable, but it is not light. Cash at FY2025 year-end was $471.4 million against total debt of $4.14 billion. StockAnalysis also shows lease liabilities of $158.8 million and a debt/EBITDA ratio of 5.08x. That is acceptable for a real-estate-heavy platform with premium assets, but it is high enough that equity investors should care about refinancing cost, covenant headroom, and the quality of incremental growth (StockAnalysis balance sheet and statistics, accessed 2026-06-14).

One quirk of the published balance sheet is that most debt appears in current liabilities as “current portion of long-term debt,” which makes liquidity screens look harsher than the business reality. The better way to underwrite the balance sheet is by total debt, interest coverage, and covenant headroom. The Q1 2026 10-Q states that the revolving credit agreement requires a consolidated net leverage ratio not greater than 7.25x and fixed charge coverage of at least 1.50x, so the company has room but not infinite room (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q filed 2026-05-01).

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Reported earnings are noisy, but not for the usual “avoid at all costs” reasons. As a REIT, RHP’s GAAP earnings are structurally burdened by depreciation, which makes simple P/E less useful than FFO or EBITDA-based lenses. On that dimension, the quality of earnings is better than a normal industrial company with the same P/E might suggest. But that does not mean all non-GAAP earnings are clean owner earnings.

The biggest adjustment is capital intensity. FFO tells you more than GAAP EPS, but not everything. Capex was $358.2 million in FY2025 after $407.9 million in FY2024, which means the business still requires substantial reinvestment even in a healthy operating environment. A lodging REIT with large destination assets cannot be valued as if depreciation were purely fictional. Some of it is economically real (StockAnalysis cash flow statement, accessed 2026-06-14).

The second quality issue is mix. The Entertainment segment is consolidated, but its economics are not identical to the core Hospitality segment, and there is a noncontrolling interest attached to OEG. That makes the consolidated income statement a little less straightforward than a pure-play hotel REIT. The third issue is acquisition accounting and financing. Desert Ridge boosted revenue, but it also came with new equity and debt financing, so investors should focus on per-share FFO accretion rather than absolute revenue growth (RHP Q1 2026 10-Q and 2025 10-K, filed 2026-05-01 and 2026-02-24).

My quality-of-earnings conclusion is: cleaner than GAAP EPS suggests, noisier than headline FFO suggests. For valuation, forward FFO/share and dividend-covered cash generation are better anchors than P/E, but they must be adjusted mentally for real reinvestment needs.

### Estimate Revision Trend

Public revision history is incomplete without premium data, but the analyst-rating pages show a consistent pattern: lots of Buys, price targets clustered tightly around the current price, and very little evidence of a truly aggressive rerating call. ### Forecast Logic

The bear case is not a collapse in convention travel. It is a lower-quality version of success: same-store pricing cools, acquired assets do fine but not brilliantly, entertainment remains uneven, and leverage plus capex keep the market from paying more than roughly 12x forward FFO.

The base case assumes group demand remains healthy, Desert Ridge integrates well, Hill Country remains productive, and management converts moderate revenue growth into modest FFO/share growth. In that case, the stock can work, but mostly through dividend collection and slow appreciation rather than a dramatic rerating.

The bull case requires more than just stable macro conditions. It requires Ryman to prove that it can convert its unique asset base into sustainably rising per-share FFO, likely above consensus, while keeping leverage controlled and demonstrating that the entertainment and acquisition strategies add to, rather than dilute, the core convention-hotel multiple.

### What The Current Price Implies

At roughly $123, the market appears to be underwriting a business that remains good but not explosive. The implied assumptions look like mid-single-digit to high-single-digit revenue growth, continued dividend support, and forward FFO/share growth that is solid enough to justify a premium to many lodging names, but not strong enough to command a major rerating. In plain language, the market seems to believe RHP deserves credit for quality, but not a scarcity premium much above where it already trades.

### Projection Takeaway

The stock works if RHP can keep compounding forward FFO/share through group pricing, disciplined capital allocation, and accretive integration of the expanded resort base. It fails, or at least disappoints, if the company keeps growing revenue while per-share economics remain merely adequate. Consensus looks reasonable rather than obviously wrong. The next metric that should change conviction is forward booking / group pacing translated into per-share FFO, not just absolute room revenue.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

Ryman’s management structure looks experienced and orderly. The 2025 10-K lists Colin V. Reed as executive chairman and Mark Fioravanti as director, president, and chief executive officer, with Jennifer Hutcheson as chief financial officer. That is a helpful combination for this kind of asset-heavy business: continuity at the board level and an operating CEO who has to prove that the portfolio can keep compounding through a more acquisitive period (RHP 2025 10-K filed 2026-02-24).

Capital allocation is the more important question than biography. Management has pursued acquisitions, maintained a meaningful dividend, and used the balance sheet assertively. That can be value-creating when assets are scarce and the financing math works. But it also means investors cannot simply extrapolate the historical resort-quality narrative. They need to ask whether each new dollar of capital produces durable per-share FFO.

The acquisition of Desert Ridge is the best near-term test. It clearly added revenue. The harder question is whether it added enough per-share value after the equity issuance, note financing, and higher interest burden that supported the deal. If the answer is yes, management deserves more credit. If the answer is only “the company is bigger,” the stock should not rerate much from here.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

### What Bulls Believe

Bulls believe RHP is one of the best ways to own U.S. group lodging because its assets are unusually hard to replicate, Marriott management improves execution, and the portfolio can support strong pricing even in a choppier macro backdrop. They also believe the entertainment portfolio is a real intangible asset that supports destination demand and gives the company more strategic optionality than a plain-vanilla hotel REIT.

### What Bears Believe

Bears believe the market is already paying up for those strengths. They see a levered, capital-intensive lodging REIT with acquisition exposure, a mixed entertainment segment, and a dividend that looks safer on FFO than on simple earnings. In their view, the stock is fine, but the upside is capped unless investors are willing to pay an even richer multiple for essentially the same business quality they already recognize.

### What The Market May Be Missing

The mild positive variant is that Desert Ridge and the broader portfolio refresh give RHP a longer runway for per-share FFO growth than the near-flat price target implies. The mild negative variant is that the company’s uniqueness obscures how much new capital it needs to keep growing. My current read is that the market is not missing much on either side; it has roughly the right debate.

## 10. Bull Case

If Ryman’s convention-resort portfolio continues to function like a scarce experiential infrastructure asset, then the current valuation is too conservative. In that outcome, same-store pricing stays healthy, outside-the-room spend remains resilient, Desert Ridge and Hill Country broaden the earnings base, and management converts revenue growth into FFO/share growth above today’s modest consensus. The stock would then be worth something closer to the mid-$160s on a higher FFO/share base and a still-reasonable premium multiple. The first evidence should show up in group room-night pacing, ADR, and sustained growth in hospitality operating income.

## 11. Bear Case

If the business quality remains high but the economics stay capital-hungry, then the stock is not cheap. Revenue can grow, dividends can keep getting paid, and the assets can remain excellent while the equity still underperforms because capex, leverage, and acquisition financing absorb too much of the operating success. In that case, forward FFO/share stalls around the high-$8 range and the market values the stock around 12x FFO, putting downside near $100. The first evidence should show up in sluggish per-share FFO progress despite respectable hotel-level operating metrics.

## 12. Base Case

The base case is that RHP continues to execute well operationally and only adequately at the stock level. Group business remains healthy, Marriott-managed properties preserve pricing, and Desert Ridge proves useful without radically changing the company’s earnings trajectory. Entertainment remains strategically nice but financially secondary. That should produce moderate FFO/share growth and a decent dividend-supported return profile, but not a dramatic valuation gap.

This is why the stance is watch. The business does not need to be criticized much. It only needs to be recognized for what it is: a quality REIT whose current stock price already reflects a lot of that quality.

The primary framework is forward FFO/share. RHP’s assets are real, scarce, and cash-generative, but they are also capital-intensive. That makes simple asset-value stories less useful than per-share recurring earnings power. EV/EBITDA is a good secondary check because it captures the enterprise burden better than P/FFO alone.

The valuation math is mostly driven by two variables: how much FFO/share the business can produce after the current acquisition cycle settles, and what multiple the market assigns to that FFO in light of leverage and quality. ## 15. Risks And Red Flags

The biggest risk is not operational collapse. It is valuation complacency around capital intensity. RHP can remain a good company and still deliver only mediocre stock returns if reinvestment, leverage, and acquisition financing consume too much of the operating upside.

A second risk is group demand normalization. The company’s advantage is its convention focus, but that is also the concentration. If large corporate and association event demand softens, the operating leverage works in reverse. A third risk is that entertainment expansion adds complexity without adding enough earnings. The country-music and venue assets are interesting, but they should not distract from the fact that hospitality still has to do the heavy lifting.

## 18. Conclusion

RHP is a differentiated and respectable company. The convention-resort platform is real, the assets are hard to replicate, Marriott management is an advantage, and the entertainment portfolio gives the story more character than most lodging REITs have. Operationally, that is a strong setup.

The problem is not the business. The problem is that the stock already knows it is a good business. At roughly $123, with forward FFO/share consensus near $8.96 and a price target essentially flat to the market price, the risk/reward looks balanced rather than asymmetric. My stance is watch. The stock works if Ryman turns asset quality into stronger per-share FFO compounding than the market expects. It disappoints if the company remains solid but merely adequate at converting growth into equity upside.

I would revisit the view if either valuation becomes more generous in the investor’s favor or management produces clearer evidence that the enlarged portfolio can raise per-share earnings power without asking the balance sheet to do too much.
