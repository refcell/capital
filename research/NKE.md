## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Nike is no longer being priced as an untouchable consumer compounder. The stock is down hard, earnings are depressed, DTC is shrinking, China is weak, Converse is in outright decline, and management is cleaning up an over-distributed lifestyle-footwear portfolio. The bull case is simple: Nike is still one of the world's best sports brands, current earnings are trough-like, Elliott Hill is reversing the DTC overreach, and normalized margins can recover as inventory, markdowns, wholesale relationships, and product innovation improve.

The bear case is not lazy. Nike may have over-earned during the DTC / digital boom, overharvested classics like Dunk, Air Force 1, and Air Jordan 1, ceded performance-running credibility to On, Hoka, Adidas, New Balance, Brooks, and Asics, and structurally weakened its China profit pool. The current stock price already assumes meaningful margin recovery. If Nike normalizes at low-double-digit EBIT margins rather than the historical low-to-mid-teens range, the equity is not obviously cheap.

### Current View

NKE is a watchlist-quality turnaround, not a high-conviction bargain. My base-case value is roughly $38 to $45 per share, with a bear case around $17 to $25 and a bull case around $65 to $75. The stock becomes more interesting if Q4 / FY2026 and early FY2027 show cleaner inventory, improving sell-through, lower markdowns, recovering digital, and a credible path back to 12%-14% EBIT margins. The stock becomes a value trap if wholesale growth is mostly sell-in, China keeps deteriorating, and margins require repeated resets.

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Nike is the global leader in athletic footwear and apparel. Its economic engine has always been bigger than shoes: sport credibility, athlete relationships, product design, brand storytelling, sourcing scale, distribution, and culture. The company does not primarily make money as a manufacturer. It designs, markets, sources, and distributes branded products while independent contract manufacturers make substantially all of its footwear and apparel.

The investor story changed over the last decade. Nike pushed harder into Nike Direct, digital membership, owned stores, and a tighter marketplace. That strategy promised higher gross margins, more consumer data, and less dependence on wholesale partners. It also created a problem: Nike under-served wholesale shelves, over-distributed classic lifestyle franchises, and left room for competitors to win performance-running and specialty-retail mindshare.

Elliott Hill's "Win Now" reset is trying to rebuild Nike around sport, product newness, marketplace balance, and healthier inventory. That is strategically sensible. The question is whether the reset can restore full-price demand quickly enough to justify the current valuation.

### Why The Company Matters

Nike still matters because it sits at the intersection of sport, fashion, culture, and global consumer spending. The company has a brand archive that few competitors can match, from Air Max to Jordan to running innovation. It also has massive demand-creation capacity, elite athlete relationships, global sourcing scale, and a retailer network that reaches far beyond owned digital traffic.

The problem is that brand scale is not the same as brand heat. Nike can remain a huge brand while producing poor shareholder returns if full-price demand weakens, product cycles miss, China structurally resets lower, and DTC economics prove less attractive than advertised.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Nike sells athletic footwear, apparel, equipment, accessories, and related services under the Nike Brand, Jordan Brand, and Converse. Footwear is the core profit pool. Apparel is large but more competitive. Equipment is small. Converse is strategically useful but currently a financial drag.

Nike sells through two primary channel families:

- Wholesale: retail partners, distributors, licensees, and sales representatives.
- Nike Direct: Nike-owned stores and Nike digital platforms.

Wholesale has lower gross margin per unit but broader reach, faster inventory turns, physical discovery, specialist-retail credibility, and partner-driven demand generation. Direct should provide higher gross margin and consumer data, but it also brings store costs, digital traffic acquisition, fulfillment, inventory ownership, returns, and markdown risk. The current turnaround implicitly admits that the company pushed too far toward Direct and underweighted wholesale.

FY2025 channel and product data need clean denominators. Nike Brand channel revenue, Converse revenue, company revenue, and product revenue are different cuts of the same business, not additive rows.

### The Economic Engine

The engine is: create sport-led product demand -> use athlete and cultural marketing to create desire -> allocate product through wholesale and Direct channels -> drive full-price sell-through -> replenish new product cycles -> convert gross profit into FCF -> return capital through dividends and buybacks.

The weak link today is marketplace health. Nike can report decent wholesale revenue if partners take inventory, but the equity works only if end-consumer sell-through improves and gross margin recovers. The next few quarters need to show fewer markdowns, healthier digital, and better category share, not only revenue stabilization.

## 6. Quality Of Earnings

Nike's earnings quality issue is not fake revenue. It is whether reported revenue and GAAP earnings reflect durable full-price consumer demand. During a channel reset, wholesale sell-in, returns reserves, inventory reserves, markdowns, receivables, and supplier finance all matter.

| Issue | Evidence | Why It Matters | Current Read |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sell-in vs sell-through | Q3 wholesale +5% reported while Direct -4% and digital -9%. | Wholesale can improve before consumer demand does. | Watch carefully. |
| Returns / discounts | FY2025 sales-related reserves rose to $1.834B from $1.282B; sales returns reserve rose to $1.277B from $799M. | Gross-to-net assumptions matter during cleanup. | Bearish / monitor. |
| Inventory reserves | FY2025 inventory reserve rose to $233M from $155M. | Obsolescence and markdowns directly hit margin. | Bearish. |
| Cash conversion | 9M FY2026 FCF $685M vs $2.905B prior year. | Owner earnings are much weaker than normalized narratives imply. | Bearish. |
| Receivables | 9M FY2026 receivables were a $623M cash drag; company tied AR increase to wholesale revenue and timing. | Wholesale shipment timing affects cash quality. | Monitor. |
| Severance / restructuring | Q3 FY2026 severance $230M; 9M $304M. | Some cost is temporary, but repeated resets reduce confidence. | Mixed. |
| Tariffs | Q3 gross margin pressure primarily from North America tariffs; IEEPA refund recovery not probable. | Gross margin bridge may remain noisy. | Bearish / uncertain. |
| Supplier finance | $0.7B outstanding at Q3 FY2026. | Payables quality affects OCF comparability. | Monitor. |
| Capitalized software | Internal-use software capitalized and amortized over 2-12 years. | Normal, but capex should be deducted in owner earnings. | Manageable. |

The main owner-earnings adjustment is caution. It is tempting to normalize FY2025 / FY2026 as a temporary trough and jump straight to historical margins. That may be right, but current cash conversion, reserves, and channel comments do not yet prove it.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

### Management

Elliott Hill is the right kind of CEO for this problem in the sense that Nike needs product, sport, wholesale, and culture repair rather than a generic consumer internet strategy. The reset is called "Win Now" and centers on cleaning up unhealthy marketplace inventory, rebuilding sport-led teams, restoring wholesale partnerships, and improving product newness.

The main execution question is timing. The company has already acknowledged that cleanup actions will weigh on results over the balance of calendar 2026 and, in China, may extend into FY2027. A good strategy can still be a bad near-term stock if earnings estimates keep moving down.

### Governance

Nike remains a dual-class company. Class A holders elect nine of twelve directors and Class B holders elect three. Class A is primarily associated with Swoosh, LLC and the Knight family influence. This is not a near-term operating issue, but it limits public-holder ability to force strategic or capital-allocation change.

MarketBeat showed 53.9 million shares short as of 2026-05-15, up about 7% from the prior report, equal to 3.67% of float and 2.58 days to cover. Yahoo / Morningstar used the same share base but showed a higher percent-of-float figure because of denominator differences.

Short interest matters for sentiment, but this is not a classic squeeze setup. The more relevant signal is that skeptics increasingly frame Nike as structural share loss rather than temporary channel cleanup.

## 10. Prior Research And Market Debate

### Public Debate

The directly accessible debate is split but skewed cautious. Seeking Alpha visible titles include bearish or cautious pieces such as "Nike: Still Not Interested," "NIKE: Just Sell It," and "Nike: No Margin Of Safety At Current Valuation," alongside contrarian comeback pieces focused on "Win Now" and the historical durability of Nike's brand. I treated those as visible public titles / snippets unless the full article was accessible.

Value Investors Club has an NKE page, but the full report is login-gated. A Smartkarma-syndicated summary of an August 2024 VIC idea framed Nike as a high-quality company where the selloff created a long-term opportunity. I did not rely on inaccessible VIC text.

Public investor-letter commentary is also mixed. Yahoo / Insider Monkey summaries cite bullish Pershing Square commentary from 2025 around Elliott Hill's turnaround, but later public commentary said Pershing exited Nike after disappointing performance. Madison Large Cap Fund disclosed selling Nike in Q4 2025. These are secondary summaries, not a substitute for full fund letters.

### What Bulls Say

Bulls say Nike's issue is execution, not brand death. The company over-indexed on DTC, underinvested in wholesale relationships, let classics become too available, and temporarily lost product cadence. Elliott Hill is reversing those decisions. If Nike restores sport-led product innovation, wholesale shelves, Jordan scarcity, running credibility, and China execution, earnings can recover sharply from depressed FY2025 / FY2026 levels.

### What Bears Say

Bears say this is now structural. Nike lost performance credibility, gave up shelf space, overharvested lifestyle franchises, trained consumers to wait for discounts, and is fighting local champions in China. Even if revenue stabilizes, the normalized business may deserve a lower margin and lower multiple than old Nike.

### Variant Perception

The variant question is not whether Nike remains a large company. It will. The question is whether normalized Nike is still a premium-growth, high-margin sports platform, or a mature incumbent with lower growth, higher promotion, and lower returns on capital.

My read: the market has recognized the turnaround, but still may be too willing to underwrite recovery before seeing sell-through proof. I would rather miss the first part of the rebound than pay for margins that have not yet returned.

## 11. Bull Case

### The Bull Argument

Nike is one of the few consumer brands with global sport permission, Jordan, athlete access, product archives, and distribution scale. The company made correctable mistakes: it leaned too far into DTC, over-distributed classics, damaged wholesale relationships, and let competitors occupy performance-running mindshare. Those are serious, but not necessarily permanent.

Elliott Hill's reset is aimed at the right problems. If the company clears aged inventory, restores scarcity, rebuilds wholesale, and launches stronger running / basketball / women's product, reported growth could lag for a few quarters while business quality improves. Once the cleanup is done, gross margin and EBIT margin can recover meaningfully.

### Bull Case Evidence

| Evidence | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Nike Brand scale remains enormous, with $44.7B FY2025 revenue. | The platform is not small or niche. |
| Q3 wholesale grew 5% reported. | Wholesale relationships are not dead. |
| Insider buying in April 2026. | Management / directors are signaling confidence near current prices. |
| Performance running category is growing. | Nike has a large category to attack if product improves. |
| Jordan and Nike archive remain culturally valuable. | Scarcity and product discipline can restore value. |
| Balance sheet is not distressed. | Nike has time to execute. |

### Bull Case Valuation

If Nike grows revenue mid-single digits, recovers to a 14%-15% EBIT margin, converts earnings into FCF, and deserves a premium global-brand multiple, the stock can be worth roughly $65-$75 per share. More aggressive bull cases can get higher, but they require confidence that Nike's old margin structure returns. I do not think the current evidence supports that yet.

## 12. Bear Case

### The Bear Argument

Nike may have a brand problem hiding inside a strategy problem. The company did not merely mistime inventory; it ceded performance credibility to sharper brands, let lifestyle franchises become too common, damaged consumer urgency, and lost some wholesale shelf influence. China may be structurally harder because local brands are better, more national, and more attuned to local consumers.

The accounting and cash-flow signals are not clean. Sales-related reserves and returns reserves rose materially in FY2025. Inventory reserves rose. 9M FY2026 FCF collapsed. Accounts receivable was a cash drag tied partly to wholesale revenues and shipment timing. Management says sell-through is not yet where it wants it to be and digital remains too promotional.

### Bear Case Evidence

| Evidence | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Q3 Nike Direct -4% reported / -7% currency-neutral. | The consumer-owned channel is still weak. |
| Q3 Nike Brand Digital -9%. | Digital is not yet fixed. |
| Q3 gross margin 40.2%, down 130 bps. | Margin recovery is not visible yet. |
| 9M FY2026 FCF $685M vs $2.905B. | Owner earnings are impaired. |
| 9M Greater China revenue -11%. | Key profit pool is under pressure. |
| Q3 Converse revenue -35%, EBIT loss. | Brand portfolio has weak spots. |
| Historical buybacks averaged $97.57 under current authorization. | Capital allocation destroyed value with hindsight. |

### Bear Case Valuation

If revenue is flat to low-growth, EBIT margin only recovers to 7%-9%, China and Direct remain weak, and Nike deserves a lower multiple, the stock can be worth $17-$25 per share. This is not my base case, but it is plausible enough that the current price needs a larger margin of safety.

## 13. Base Case

### The Base Argument

Nike is not broken, but the reset is real. I expect the company to stabilize revenue over FY2026-FY2027, rebuild some wholesale momentum, improve inventory quality, and recover part of the margin structure. I do not underwrite a full return to old Nike margins without evidence.

In the base case, revenue grows about 3% annually over five years and EBIT margin recovers to roughly 11.5%. That is a meaningful improvement from FY2025 / TTM levels, but below the best historical years. The stock is worth around $38-$45 per share, roughly near the current price.

The primary framework is normalized owner earnings, not sales. At roughly $45, the market seems to be underwriting a return to low-teens EBIT margins. ## 19. Conclusion

Nike is a better business than the recent financials suggest, but that is already the market's argument. At $45-$46, the stock is not priced for permanent collapse. It is priced for a meaningful recovery from depressed margins and cash flow.

My current stance is watch / not enough margin of safety. The brand remains powerful, the balance sheet is fine, and the new CEO is attacking the right problems. But the evidence is not yet strong enough: Direct and digital are weak, China is deteriorating, Converse is collapsing, gross margin is below normal, FCF conversion is poor, and wholesale growth needs sell-through proof.

The most useful next checkpoint is not whether Q4 revenue beats consensus. It is whether Nike can show healthier inventory, less promotion, a credible FY2027 margin bridge, improving digital, and a China plan that does not require repeated resets. If those appear together, the stock can work. If they do not, normalized earnings may be lower than the market still wants to believe.
