## Executive Summary

TransMedics is not a normal medtech company. It is trying to rebuild the organ-transplant workflow around its Organ Care System, or OCS, and National OCS Program, or NOP. OCS keeps donor organs in warm, functioning condition outside the body. NOP adds procurement surgeons, perfusion management, aircraft, ground transportation, and logistics coordination. If this works, TransMedics becomes a core infrastructure layer for more complex organ transplantation.

The business is real. Revenue grew from $93.5 million in 2022 to $241.6 million in 2023, $441.5 million in 2024, and $605.5 million in 2025. The company completed more than 5,100 U.S. OCS transplants in 2025, and liver has become a scaled commercial franchise. U.S. liver revenue was $459.4 million in 2025, compared with $111.8 million for U.S. heart and $13.4 million for U.S. lung.

The recent selloff is also understandable. Q1 2026 revenue grew 21% to $173.9 million, but operating income fell 52% to $13.3 million, gross margin declined to 58% from 61%, adjusted operating margin fell to 10.4% from 20.7%, and simple free cash flow was negative after $36.7 million of capex. Management reiterated FY2026 revenue guidance of $727 million to $757 million, but the market is now asking a sharper question: how much normalized margin and owner earnings does this hybrid device-logistics model actually generate?

My read is balanced. At $67.84, TMDX is no longer priced like the 2024 momentum stock. On the midpoint of FY2026 revenue guidance, it trades around 3.2x market cap / sales, 3.7x screen EV / sales, and about 4.1x my more conservative obligation-adjusted EV / sales. That is not extreme for a category leader, but FY2025 headline earnings were helped by a large tax benefit, and Q1 2026 reminded investors that aircraft, clinical teams, R&D, and international expansion are real cash costs.

The bull case is that Q1 margin pressure is a front-loaded investment cycle. If OCS/NOP continues to expand liver, reaccelerates heart and lung, adds kidney, and launches Europe with improving logistics density, the current price can be attractive. The bear case is that the market still wants to value TMDX as high-margin medtech while the business is becoming a lower-margin aviation-enabled transplant-services operator.

I would frame TMDX as a high-upside, high-diligence long candidate after the drawdown, not a clean compounder. The thesis works if revenue growth comes with margin recovery, positive free cash flow after normalized aircraft and facilities capex, and broader adoption beyond liver. It weakens if liver growth slows, heart/lung remain small, service margin stays low, the inventory-control weakness persists, or reimbursement/regulatory scrutiny rises.

## Company Overview

TransMedics is a commercial-stage medical technology company founded in 1998. The company developed the OCS platform to replace or supplement static cold storage in heart, lung, and liver transplantation. Instead of placing organs on ice, OCS perfuses organs with warm, oxygenated, nutrient-enriched blood and allows functional monitoring outside the body.

The company says OCS Heart, OCS Lung, and OCS Liver have FDA PMA approvals for both donation after brain death, or DBD, and donation after circulatory death, or DCD, indications. That approval footprint is important because the bull case is not just taking share from cold storage in ordinary cases. It is expanding usable organ supply and improving logistics for harder cases.

NOP is the second major asset. It is a turnkey U.S. service model that can provide organ procurement, OCS perfusion management, transplant logistics, aviation, ground transportation, and coordination. As of 2026-03-31, TransMedics owned 22 aircraft. The company also announced a planned investment in PAD Aviation in Germany to build a European transplant logistics network.

Economically, TransMedics has two revenue streams:

| Revenue stream | Description | Economic quality |
|---|---|---|
| Product revenue | OCS disposable sets, perfusion solutions, consoles sold or implied-rented through loaned consoles. | High gross margin and recurring per case. |
| Service revenue | Procurement, perfusion management, aviation, ground transport, and logistics through NOP. | Strategically valuable but lower margin and capital/labor intensive. |

The company is still overwhelmingly U.S.-driven. In 2025, OCS transplant revenue was $601.4 million, of which $584.7 million came from U.S. customers.

## Business Model

The cleanest unit of analysis is a transplant case. Each OCS case usually requires an organ-specific disposable set and solutions. If the center uses NOP, the case can also include procurement, perfusion management, transport, and coordination services.

This creates an attractive product pull-through model. Consoles matter, but disposable sets matter more. Each new transplant procedure requires another disposable set. That product revenue has recently carried high-70s gross margin.

NOP makes adoption easier for transplant centers and can increase OCS utilization, but it also changes the margin profile. Cost of service revenue includes organ procurement and perfusion labor, pilot labor, aircraft depreciation, aircraft costs, fuel, crew travel, maintenance, third-party flight costs, and ground transportation. This is why service revenue is strategically useful but economically lower quality than pure product revenue.

Bulls can call this "good dilution" if NOP drives high-margin disposable pull-through and better clinical execution. Bears can call it a sign that TMDX is less of a pure medtech platform and more of a capital-intensive transplant logistics company.

## Products, Technology, and Assets

### OCS Liver

Liver is the company today. U.S. liver revenue grew from $46.2 million in 2022 to $151.7 million in 2023, $309.5 million in 2024, and $459.4 million in 2025. In Q1 2026, U.S. liver revenue was $139.0 million, up from $108.7 million in Q1 2025.

That success is the strongest proof that OCS/NOP can scale. It is also a concentration risk. If liver penetration matures or hospitals push back on economics, TMDX needs heart, lung, kidney, and international growth to carry the next phase.

### OCS Heart

Heart is meaningful but not yet large enough to diversify the business. U.S. heart revenue was $111.8 million in 2025 and $25.9 million in Q1 2026, roughly flat versus $26.3 million in Q1 2025.

The next-generation heart opportunity matters. The ENHANCE Heart trial is intended to evaluate a next-generation OCS Heart platform under extended perfusion conditions and to assess outcomes relative to static cold storage in certain donor populations. The 2025 10-K says ENHANCE Heart is expected to enroll more than 650 heart transplant recipients over multiple years.

### OCS Lung

Lung is currently small. U.S. lung revenue was $13.4 million in 2025 and only $2.2 million in Q1 2026. This is a weak spot in the current multi-organ narrative. The platform is multi-organ technologically, but commercial economics are liver-heavy.

The DENOVO Lung trial is intended to evaluate a modified OCS Lung system and may support superiority claims versus static cold storage. Until lung revenue inflects, it should be valued as optionality.

### CHOPS, Kidney, and Next-Generation Systems

At ISHLT 2026, TransMedics unveiled CHOPS, a Controlled Hypothermic Organ Preservation System aimed at facilitating control-arm enrollment in OCS ENHANCE Heart Part B and OCS DENOVO Lung. The company is also advancing OCS Kidney and next-generation OCS systems.

These programs are necessary for the long-term TAM story, but they raise near-term spending and execution risk. Q1 2026 R&D expense increased to $24.9 million from $17.2 million a year earlier.

### Aircraft and Logistics

The aviation network is a moat if it increases reliability, route density, and transplant-center adoption. It is a liability if it stays underutilized or introduces operational failures. A typical medtech investor does not have to underwrite pilot labor, aircraft maintenance, fuel, FAA compliance, or dispatch complexity. A TMDX investor does.

The Q1 2026 balance sheet also added a $334.5 million finance lease right-of-use asset and a $343.8 million finance lease liability, with base rent beginning in 2028 for the new Somerville headquarters lease. This changes leverage optics and should be considered in owner-earnings valuation.

## Industry and Competitive Position

The U.S. transplant market is growing but still heavily supply-constrained. UNOS reported 49,064 U.S. organ transplants in 2025, a fifth consecutive annual record, while more than 100,000 people remained on the waiting list. Liver transplants reached 12,344 in 2025.

The addressable market is not simply today's transplant volume. The bull case depends on increasing usable donor organs, especially DCD organs, longer-distance organs, marginal organs needing assessment, and cases where transplant-center procurement capacity is constrained.

Cold storage remains the incumbent standard. It is simple, cheap, and familiar. Its limitations are the opening for TransMedics: ischemic injury, short preservation windows, limited functional assessment, and poor utilization of certain donor pools.

Competition includes OrganOx in liver warm perfusion, XVIVO in lung, and cold preservation or transport systems such as Paragonix. Terumo acquired OrganOx in 2025, and Getinge acquired Paragonix in 2024. The competitive question is not whether TransMedics is differentiated. It is whether differentiation sustains pricing, reimbursement, and share as larger strategic competitors and transplant centers adapt.

| Moat layer | Why it matters | What could weaken it |
|---|---|---|
| FDA approvals | PMA approvals across heart, lung, and liver create regulatory credibility. | Competitors win organ-specific approvals or better labels. |
| Clinical evidence | Transplant centers rely on outcomes and peer usage. | Adverse data, registry concerns, or superior competitor data. |
| Workflow integration | NOP embeds TransMedics in procurement and logistics. | Centers, OPOs, or competitors replicate service capability. |
| Disposable pull-through | Each OCS case drives recurring product revenue. | Lower utilization per console or pricing pressure. |
| Aviation network | Dedicated assets may improve availability and quality control. | Low utilization, safety/FAA issues, maintenance, fuel, or pilot constraints. |
| Reimbursement expertise | Billing and organ acquisition cost recovery are complex. | CMS, private payor, hospital, or OPO scrutiny. |
