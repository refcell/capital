## How To Use This Template

This is a prose-first research template. The goal is not to fill every box. The goal is
to write a clear investment memo that a reader can follow from setup, to evidence, to
valuation, to decision.

The postmortem style is the model: natural paragraphs first, compact tables second. A
good report should read like a coherent argument, not a checklist. The reader should
understand what matters before seeing the table, and should understand what the table
changes after seeing it.

Each major section should open with plain paragraphs that explain the point in English.
Use tables only when comparison, history, or scenario math becomes clearer in table
form. Delete tables that do not add clarity; a completed report should not contain
blank or ceremonial tables.

Make the core investment debate obvious on the first page. Separate what the market
appears to believe from what this report believes. Keep the writing company-specific
and explain the mechanism behind growth, margins, cash flow, and multiples instead of
only naming the direction.

Forward projections should be anchored in analyst expectations, management guidance,
and historical evidence. Say when a number is fragile, stale, pro forma, adjusted,
non-GAAP, consensus-derived, or data-provider-specific. Prefer primary sources for
filings, guidance, share count, debt, segment data, risks, and management commentary.
Use public analyst-consensus sources as expectations anchors, not as proof of intrinsic
value.

Most sections should start with two to five natural paragraphs, add a table only if it
compresses evidence the prose has already framed, and end with a short interpretation
that ties the section back to the thesis or valuation.

Delete this "How To Use" section in completed reports.

## 1. Thesis In One Page

### The Setup

Write the opening like a clean investment memo, not a company profile. Explain why the
stock is worth studying now, what the surface narrative is, and what the real question
is underneath it.

Good shape:

"[Company] looks like [surface narrative], but the real question is [core debate]. The
stock is interesting because [valuation / controversy / transition / inflection /
quality / neglect]. The thesis works if [business outcome]. It fails if [business
failure]."

The first page should tell the reader what has to be true for the stock to work. If the
answer is still unclear, say that directly and explain what evidence would settle it.

### The Core Debate

Write this in prose first. The useful version is not "bulls like growth and bears worry
about competition." The useful version is the actual underwriting conflict.

Example:

"The debate is not whether [Company] is growing. It is whether the growth is coming from
[high-quality source] or [lower-quality source]. If the former is true, today's multiple
is too low. If the latter is true, the apparent cheapness is a trap."

## 2. Company Story

### Origin And Evolution

Tell the company story as a short history of how the economic engine got here. Cover
founding, original product, major pivots, IPO / SPAC / listing history, M&A, management
transitions, regulatory events, product inflections, and strategic changes only when
they matter to today's thesis.

The point is not chronology for its own sake. The point is to show which parts of the
current business are inherited strengths, which are recent bets, and which old
assumptions may no longer be true.

### Why The Company Matters

Explain the company's role in its ecosystem in normal language. Who is the customer?
What pain does the company solve? Why is the problem hard? Why might this company have
an edge? Why might that edge be fading?

The reader should come away understanding why the company exists and why the market
assigns value to it.

## 3. Business Model

### How The Company Makes Money

Explain the revenue model in prose before showing line items. Cover revenue streams,
pricing model, gross vs net reporting, recurring vs transactional revenue, customer
concentration, cyclicality, and accounting quirks.

The key question is which revenue is strategically valuable. A dollar of revenue from
the core profit pool may not deserve the same multiple as a dollar from a low-margin,
cyclical, promotional, or commoditized source.

### The Economic Engine

Describe the causal loop that creates value. This should usually be one concise
paragraph that starts with "The engine is:" and follows the money from customer need to
revenue, margin, cash flow, and per-share value.

Examples: a SaaS company might acquire customers, expand seats, attach modules, retain,
price, and convert recurring revenue to FCF. A lender might acquire deposits,
underwrite credit, manage losses, and compound book value. A marketplace might grow
supply and demand, increase frequency, and monetize ads, payments, logistics, or
fintech. A payments company might grow TPV, retain gross profit yield, and convert
gross profit to cash. A consumer brand might earn shelf space, drive velocity, reinvest
in brand and distribution, and protect margin. A medtech company might grow installed
base or procedure volume, improve utilization, and scale manufacturing and service.

Then name the weak link. Every engine has one. It might be retention, take rate, credit
quality, distribution, regulation, input cost, reimbursement, utilization, or terminal
multiple.

### Unit Economics / Customer Economics

Use this section when customer-level economics matter. If they do not, explain what
replaces them for this business.

Write the interpretation first. Are unit economics improving, deteriorating, hidden, or
not disclosed? Are the reported metrics enough to prove the business is attractive, or
are they missing the actual constraint?

### What The Current Price Implies

Reverse-engineer the stock price in prose. At today's price, what growth, margin,
cash-flow durability, credit quality, terminal multiple, or ROE is the market
underwriting?

If a quick scaffold helps, state the implied revenue CAGR, long-term margin, EPS or FCF
power, exit multiple, and execution hurdle in one short paragraph.

### Projection Takeaway

End with one readable paragraph:

"The stock works if [specific business outcome]. It fails if [specific business
failure]. Consensus appears [too high / too low / reasonable] because [evidence]. The

### Business-Model Specific Projection Notes

Delete notes that are not relevant in completed reports. For banks and lenders, the
drivers are usually loans, deposits, NIM, cost of deposits, NPLs, charge-offs,
provisions, ROE / ROTCE, tangible book value per share, and capital ratios. For SaaS,
look at ARR or revenue growth, net retention, customer growth, ARPU, gross margin,
operating margin, SBC, FCF margin, and diluted share count. For payments, focus on TPV,
take rate, gross profit yield, adjusted EBITDA, adjusted FCF, settlement working
capital, and merchant concentration. For consumer brands, use volume, price / mix,
distribution, category growth, market share, gross margin, promo intensity, inventory,
and working capital. For marketplaces, use GMV, take rate, frequency, ads, logistics,
fintech, credit losses, segment margin, and SOTP. For medtech and healthcare, use
procedure volume, installed base, reimbursement, gross margin, R&D, sales force
productivity, capex, and regulatory milestones.

## 8. Management, Governance, And Capital Allocation

The goal is not biography. The goal is whether these people can compound capital per
share.

Write the capital-allocation read in prose. Are incentives aligned? Has management
created or destroyed per-share value? Are buybacks intelligent or cosmetic? Has M&A
improved the economic engine or hidden organic weakness? Is dilution acceptable relative
to value creation?

Cover founder or operator quality, incentives and ownership, insider buying and
selling, dilution, M&A record, buybacks and dividends, capital structure, and governance
red flags when they affect per-share value.

## 9. Market Debate And Variant Perception

Write this section like a fair fight. The bull case should be strong enough that a bull
would recognize it. The bear case should be strong enough that a bear would recognize
it.

### What Bulls Believe

Explain the strongest version of the long case in paragraphs. Include the business
mechanism, valuation logic, and evidence that would make the case more credible.

### What Bears Believe

Explain the strongest version of the short / pass case in paragraphs. Include the
business mechanism, valuation risk, and evidence that would make the case more credible.

### What The Market May Be Missing

This is the variant view. Be explicit about whether it is positive or negative. The best
version is usually:

"The market appears to be focused on [visible issue]. The more important issue may be
[less obvious mechanism]."

## 10. Bull Case

Make the bull case causal, not a list of positives.

Required shape:

"If [business outcome] is true, then [financial outcome] happens, and the stock is worth
[valuation outcome]. The first evidence should show up in [metric / event]."

## 11. Bear Case

Use the same standard as the bull case. Explain the mechanism, not just the downside.

Required shape:

"If [business outcome] breaks, then [financial outcome] happens, and downside is
[valuation outcome]. The first evidence should show up in [metric / event]."

## 12. Base Case

State what is most likely today. Include growth assumptions, margin assumptions, capital
intensity, multiple / discount rate / ROE framework, and what the market needs to see
next.

This should be a paragraph-led section. A reader should understand the base case before
seeing any valuation table.

### Primary Valuation Framework

Explain why this framework is the right tool for this business.

Examples: banks and lenders usually need P/B, ROTCE, residual income, and credit-cycle
scenarios. SaaS usually needs FCF, revenue multiples, growth durability, and
SBC-adjusted owner earnings. Payments companies usually need gross profit, adjusted FCF,
and take-rate durability. Consumer companies usually need normalized EBIT or FCF, brand
durability, inventory, and promo-cycle context. Marketplaces often need SOTP, GMV
monetization, ads, fintech, and logistics optionality. Medtech companies usually need a
revenue ramp, gross margin, normalized operating margin, and capex intensity.

## 18. Conclusion

Write the final judgment in one to three paragraphs. State the stance, fair-value range,
