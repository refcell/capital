# Celsius Holdings (NASDAQ: CELH) Investment Research

Date: 2026-05-13

Price snapshot: CELH closed at $29.61 on 2026-05-12, the latest completed regular-market session before this report. Using 255.640 million common shares outstanding at 2026-04-30, common equity market capitalization was approximately $7.57 billion. At 2026-03-31, Celsius had $549.2 million of cash and equivalents and $675.9 million of debt carrying value, implying enterprise value of about $7.70 billion before treating Pepsi's convertible preferred stock as debt-like capital. Including Pepsi's Series A and Series B preferred at $1.135 billion aggregate liquidation preference implies enterprise value of about $8.84 billion; including the $1.760 billion carrying value of mezzanine preferred implies roughly $9.46 billion.

This is investment research, not personalized financial advice.

## Executive Summary

Celsius is no longer a simple single-brand energy-drink insurgent. In 2025 it bought Alani Nu, acquired the U.S. and Canada rights to Rockstar from PepsiCo, moved Alani Nu into the Pepsi system, and became PepsiCo's U.S. strategic energy-category lead across CELSIUS, Alani Nu, and Rockstar. That gives the company a much larger shelf-space, portfolio, and route-to-market story than it had two years ago.

The stock is interesting because the market has punished CELH while the reported numbers look strong. Q1 2026 revenue grew 138% year over year to $782.6 million, adjusted EBITDA was $195.5 million, and the portfolio reached roughly 20.9% U.S. energy-drink dollar share. But the quality of that growth is the debate: Alani Nu contributed $368.1 million, Rockstar contributed $66.6 million, and the legacy CELSIUS brand grew only about 6%. Q1 also benefited from increased Alani Nu orders as the brand moved from its prior distribution system into PepsiCo's system.

The bull case is that Celsius has become the best scaled energy platform inside Pepsi's distribution network. CELSIUS, Alani Nu, and Rockstar have different consumer roles. Alani Nu adds a younger and more female-skewing lifestyle brand. Rockstar gives Celsius another brand to manage inside Pepsi's system. The portfolio has enough share to matter to retailers, and international is still tiny.

The bear case is that headline growth is masking a more complicated business. Celsius paid heavily for Alani Nu, issued stock, took on debt, and layered in Pepsi preferred stock that sits ahead of common equity. Pepsi was 59.0% of Q1 2026 revenue and 45.5% of accounts receivable. Gross margin fell to 48.3% from 52.3% because Alani and Rockstar started with lower margin profiles, and promotional allowances are large.

My read is cautiously constructive but not clean. At $29.61, CELH is not priced like the old momentum stock, and 2026 sell-side estimates imply a much more reasonable forward earnings multiple. But the common equity is not obviously cheap if one treats Pepsi's preferred as a real senior or convertible claim. My base-case DCF, using a conservative preferred-stock bridge, lands around $18 to $20 per share. A multiples-based base case lands closer to the low-to-high $30s. The disagreement comes from whether Q1 adjusted EBITDA is a clean run-rate. I would call CELH a watchlist-quality compounder with a live mispricing setup, not a clean bargain yet.

## Company Overview

Celsius Holdings develops, markets, sells, and distributes functional energy drinks and related wellness products. The current portfolio is built around three energy brands:

| Brand | Role in portfolio |
|---|---|
| CELSIUS | Flagship fitness and functional-energy brand. |
| Alani Nu | Lifestyle energy brand with stronger appeal to younger and female consumers. |
| Rockstar Energy | Legacy mainstream energy brand acquired in the U.S. and Canada from PepsiCo. |

The company reports as one operating segment. That simplifies disclosure but makes brand-level economics harder to underwrite. The important business split is not a formal segment split; it is the difference between legacy CELSIUS, acquired Alani Nu, acquired Rockstar, North America, international, and Pepsi-driven distribution economics.

2025 was a reset year. Celsius acquired Alani Nu on April 1, 2025. On August 28, 2025, it expanded its PepsiCo relationship, acquired the Rockstar brand in the U.S. and Canada, gave PepsiCo Series B convertible preferred stock, and moved Alani Nu into Pepsi distribution in the U.S. and Canada. Pepsi remains the primary distributor for CELSIUS products in the U.S. and Canada and also became the primary distributor for Alani Nu and Rockstar in those markets.

The company is still overwhelmingly North American. In 2025, North America generated $2.422 billion of revenue, Europe $72.5 million, Asia-Pacific $13.0 million, and other markets $7.3 million. In Q1 2026, North America was $747.3 million and international revenue was only $35.3 million, despite 55% year-over-year growth.

## Business Model

The business model is straightforward at the consumer level:

`brand demand x retail distribution x shelf space x velocity x gross margin - marketing, trade, distribution, and corporate costs`

Celsius sells branded energy products to distributors, club stores, mass retailers, e-commerce platforms, and other customers. In the U.S. and Canada, Pepsi is the critical route-to-market partner. Pepsi's direct-store-delivery system can improve reach, cooler placement, merchandising, and retail execution. It also creates dependency. In Q1 2026, Pepsi was not just a distributor but the largest customer, a preferred-stock holder, a board-rights holder, and a related party.

The most important accounting issue is gross-to-net revenue. Promotional allowances, distributor economics, transition reimbursements, deferred revenue, and Pepsi-related deferred other costs matter. In Q1 2026, accrued promotional allowance was $401.1 million, including $197.0 million related-party. That does not mean the revenue is bad, but it means reported sales need to be evaluated alongside promo support and receivables.

## Products and Portfolio

CELSIUS is the original functional-energy brand. It is positioned around fitness, zero sugar, and functional benefits. The brand benefited from a multi-year distribution expansion and from the consumer shift toward zero-sugar energy. In Q1 2026, CELSIUS brand revenue increased about 6% year over year. That is still growth, but it is a very different story from the historical hyper-growth narrative.

Alani Nu is the strategic swing factor. Celsius bought it for roughly $2.056 billion of total consideration, including $1.322 billion of cash, $722.0 million of share consideration, and contingent consideration. Alani contributed roughly $1.002 billion of revenue in the second, third, and fourth quarters of 2025 and $368.1 million in Q1 2026. Management said Q1 benefited from both strong demand and increased orders from the largest distributor as Alani moved into PepsiCo's system.

Rockstar is the portfolio-control asset. Celsius acquired the U.S. and Canada rights from PepsiCo, while Pepsi retained international Rockstar rights. Rockstar contributed $55.6 million of revenue in 2025 after the August transaction and $66.6 million in Q1 2026. Rockstar retail sales were down in Q1, so the near-term bull case is less about explosive Rockstar growth and more about giving Celsius another brand to rationalize inside Pepsi's energy portfolio.

The portfolio claim is credible. Celsius said its brands together reached approximately 20.9% U.S. energy-drink dollar share in Q1 2026, with CELSIUS at 9.9%, Alani Nu at 9.0%, and Rockstar at 2.0%. The risk is that portfolio share does not automatically translate into owner earnings if it requires heavy promotional allowances, lower gross margin, or constant SKU churn.

## Industry and Competitive Position

Energy drinks remain one of the more attractive categories in non-alcoholic beverages. The category benefits from frequent consumption, premium pricing, zero-sugar adoption, convenience-channel strength, and broadening use cases beyond traditional gaming or extreme-sports energy. Celsius also argues that lower alcohol consumption and more social energy-drink usage create incremental occasions.

Competition is brutal. Red Bull and Monster remain the category anchors. Celsius now competes with Monster, Red Bull, C4, Ghost, Prime, Bloom, 5-hour Energy, private label, Coca-Cola and Pepsi-affiliated brands, and many smaller functional beverage brands. Keurig Dr Pepper's GHOST acquisition and C4 relationship show that other scaled beverage systems are also trying to buy or build energy exposure.

The moat is not formula IP. It is brand, distribution, shelf presence, velocity, retail trust, and portfolio architecture. The Pepsi system is the biggest advantage and the biggest dependency. If Pepsi and Celsius are aligned, Celsius gets national DSD execution and a better claim to category captaincy. If execution weakens or incentives diverge, Celsius has much less control than a fully self-distributed company.

International remains an option rather than the central thesis. Q1 2026 international revenue was only $35.3 million, or about 4.5% of total revenue. The Nordics, the U.K., Ireland, France, Australia, New Zealand, Benelux, Canada, and other expansion markets matter, but it is too early to underwrite CELH as a global energy platform on reported international results alone.

## Management, Governance, and Capital Allocation

John Fieldly is Chairman and CEO. Eric Hanson is President and COO. Jarrod Langhans is CFO. The company has become more complex quickly, so the management question has changed from "can this team scale a hot brand?" to "can this team integrate a multi-brand portfolio, manage Pepsi, avoid bad M&A, and keep brand velocity high?"

Capital allocation became aggressive in 2025:

| Area | 2025-2026 fact | Investor question |
|---|---|---|
| Alani Nu acquisition | $2.056B total consideration, including cash and stock. | Did Celsius buy a durable brand or pay peak multiple for category momentum? |
| Rockstar / Pepsi transactions | Rockstar U.S./Canada acquisition plus captaincy and Pepsi preferred issuance. | Does portfolio control offset Pepsi dependency and preferred-stock complexity? |
| Debt | Debt moved from zero to about $676M. | Can the company delever while funding working capital and marketing? |
| Preferred stock | Pepsi holds Series A and Series B convertible preferred. | How much upside belongs to common after senior/convertible claims? |
| Buybacks | Q1 2026 included about $24M of share repurchases. | Buybacks may be rational at lower prices, but integration should remain the priority. |

Pepsi's governance role matters. The 2025 10-K says Pepsi has two designated directors serving on the board. Pepsi also has ownership rights tied to its preferred stock and beneficial ownership on an as-converted basis. This alignment can be positive because Pepsi benefits from Celsius' growth. It can also create conflict because Pepsi's system economics, distribution priorities, and board influence may not always match common shareholders' preferred outcome.

The preferred stock deserves explicit treatment. At 2025 year-end, Series A preferred had $550.0 million aggregate liquidation preference and Series B had $585.0 million. The 10-K said approximately 22.0 million common shares were issuable on Series A conversion and 11.3 million on Series B conversion. The shares are not currently freely converted in the simple way a plain vanilla common holder would treat them, but economically they are a real senior or convertible claim.

## Ownership, Insider Activity, Short Interest, and Analyst Sentiment

Institutional ownership is high, though providers differ. Nasdaq's institutional data showed 182.3 million institutional shares, or roughly 71.3% ownership, across 737 holders. Fintel showed 163.1 million shares, or 63.5%, with fewer holders. The exact number is less important than the conclusion: CELH is widely owned and actively followed, not undiscovered.

The 2026 proxy listed several large common holders: the Alani Holdings / Max Clemons / Trey Steiger group at 22.451 million shares, Chau Hoi Shuen Solina Holly-related ownership at 23.041 million shares, Deborah and Dean DeSantis-related ownership above 19 million shares each, AllianceBernstein above 16 million shares, and BlackRock above 14 million shares. Officers and directors as a group owned about 6.030 million shares, or 2.33%.

Dataroma showed zero superinvestor ownership count for CELH. That is not a decisive negative, but it means there is no obvious cluster of value-investor sponsorship in that database.

Insider activity is mixed but not a clean bear signal. Recent Form 4s included tax-withholding transactions rather than open-market sales. OpenInsider showed two notable open-market purchases in November 2025: director Hal Kravitz bought 10,000 shares at $45.24 and President/COO Eric Hanson bought 4,558 shares at $43.93. It also showed director Joyce Russell selling 2,880 shares at $51.31 in March 2026.

Short interest is meaningful but not extreme. Nasdaq showed 22.879 million shares short as of 2026-04-30, about 9.0% of shares outstanding and roughly 11% to 12% of float depending on float source. Days to cover were about 3.2. This is debated, but not a crowded-short forced-cover setup.

Sell-side sentiment remains very bullish. StockAnalysis showed 17 analysts with an average target of $62.47, low $44 and high $75. MarketBeat, Yahoo, Nasdaq, and ChartMill all showed targets in the low-to-mid $60s. Recent revisions after Q1 were mixed: JPMorgan raised its target, Morgan Stanley cut its target, and Rothschild initiated at Hold. The sell side is giving CELH credit for the portfolio transformation; the stock price is more skeptical.

## Prior Research and Market Debate

The public debate has moved through three phases. The old bull case was simple: CELSIUS was a fast-growing energy brand with superior positioning, distribution white space, and high incremental margins. The 2024-2025 debate became about whether U.S. scanner sales and Pepsi inventory dynamics showed a real slowdown. The current debate is about the portfolio.

Bulls argue that Alani Nu and Rockstar changed the company. Celsius is now a three-brand platform with Pepsi category captaincy, stronger retailer relevance, and a broader consumer map. Bears argue that the company had to buy growth, accept dilution and preferred-stock complexity, and become dependent on Pepsi to offset slowing legacy CELSIUS growth.

Accessible public research reflects both sides:

The market may be missing how valuable portfolio control can be if Alani Nu is truly underdistributed and Pepsi execution is strong. The market may also be missing how much Q1 reported revenue benefited from transition orders, and how much common-equity value is absorbed by the preferred-stock structure.

## Bull Case

The bull case starts with category quality. Energy drinks are still growing, zero-sugar energy is a strong subcategory, and Celsius has three scaled brands rather than one. A 20.9% dollar share gives the company relevance to retailers, distributors, and category planners.

Alani Nu may be a better asset than the market credits. It adds a different consumer cohort, had strong retail momentum, and generated $368.1 million of Q1 2026 sales. If the brand can sustain high sell-through after the Pepsi transition laps, Celsius may have acquired a second core brand rather than simply buying one year of revenue.

Pepsi can be a force multiplier. The DSD system, retailer relationships, planogram influence, and national execution can turn shelf opportunity into realized revenue. The captaincy structure gives Celsius more influence over energy execution inside Pepsi's U.S. system.

Margins can recover. Q1 gross margin was 48.3%, but management points to purchasing integration, freight optimization, raw-material alignment, an orbit model, and price-pack architecture. If consolidated gross margin returns to the low 50s while SG&A leverage holds, the company could generate substantially more cash than 2025 GAAP results suggest.

International is still early. International revenue was only $35.3 million in Q1 2026. If CELSIUS travels internationally and Alani eventually follows, the company has a real runway beyond the U.S. energy shelf.

## Bear Case

The bear case is that CELH has become more complicated because the original high-quality story slowed.

Legacy CELSIUS grew only about 6% in Q1 2026. That may be acceptable for a mature brand, but not for a stock that still needs growth-company execution. The reported 138% revenue growth came mostly from acquired brands and transition dynamics.

Alani Nu Q1 revenue may not be a clean run-rate. Management said Alani benefited from ongoing customer demand and increased orders as the brand moved out of its prior distribution system and into PepsiCo's system. The key bear question is whether Q2 and Q3 shipments normalize lower as channel inventory settles.

Gross margin is lower. Q1 gross margin fell 400 basis points year over year to 48.3%. Management says some transition and cost issues are temporary, but Alani and Rockstar entered with lower margin profiles. If consolidated gross margin remains below 50%, the acquired portfolio is less valuable than the old CELSIUS model.

Pepsi concentration is high. Pepsi was 43.2% of 2025 revenue and 59.0% of Q1 2026 revenue. It was also 45.5% of Q1 accounts receivable. Bulls call that aligned distribution scale; bears call it customer, distributor, governance, and working-capital concentration.

The balance sheet is less clean. At Q1 2026, Celsius had about $676 million of debt, $1.760 billion of mezzanine preferred carrying value, $920 million of goodwill, and about $1.386 billion of brand/customer intangible assets. The common equity is now more exposed to acquisition accounting, integration risk, impairment risk, and preferred-stock economics.

## Base Case

The base case assumes Celsius has a real multi-brand platform, but Q1 2026 overstates normalized momentum. Revenue grows from a 2026 base near $3.15 billion at high single digits for several years, then tapers as the portfolio matures. CELSIUS grows mid-single digits, Alani grows faster but normalizes after the Pepsi transition, Rockstar stabilizes but does not become a major growth engine, and international grows quickly off a small base.

Gross margin gradually improves from the high 40s toward 50% to 52% as purchasing and freight synergies come through, but it does not immediately return to a pristine pre-acquisition structure. SG&A as a percentage of revenue improves as distributor termination and integration expenses roll off, but marketing and trade support remain meaningful because the energy shelf is competitive.

Owner earnings improve, but not as cleanly as adjusted EBITDA suggests. I would normalize free cash flow around 8% to 12% of revenue over time, not simply annualize Q1 adjusted EBITDA. Pepsi-related deferred revenue, deferred other costs, promotional accruals, and receivables should remain part of the cash-flow diligence.

The DCF is conservative because it penalizes the common equity for Pepsi's preferred claim. That is appropriate for a margin-of-safety analysis. If the preferred ultimately converts and the business compounds strongly, the economic result will be closer to a diluted equity model. Either way, common holders are not underwriting a clean net-cash beverage brand anymore.

### Reverse DCF

At $29.61, the market is implicitly underwriting enterprise value around $8.84 billion if the preferred is treated at liquidation preference, or $9.46 billion if treated at carrying value.

If 2026 revenue is $3.15 billion, revenue grows 8% annually for a decade, WACC is 9%, and terminal growth is 2.5%, CELH needs terminal free-cash-flow margin around 14% to justify the current price using the liquidation-preference bridge. If free-cash-flow margin reaches only 12%, the company needs roughly 10% decade-long revenue growth.

Monster trades at a much richer multiple, while mature beverage companies trade lower. My blended read is that fair value is wide, roughly $20 to $40 in the current evidence set. Below $25 the stock would start to look clearly interesting on risk/reward. Above $40 it would require more confidence in Alani sell-through and margin recovery than the filings currently prove.

## Variant Perception

The common perception after the selloff is that CELH is either a broken growth stock or a misunderstood bargain. I think both framings are too simple.

The more precise variant view is that CELH has become a portfolio allocator and Pepsi-system energy captain. That can be a better business than a one-brand story if Alani Nu keeps working and Pepsi execution improves. It can be a worse business if acquired growth masks CELSIUS maturity and the preferred-stock structure absorbs common-equity upside.

The evidence that would change my mind is practical:

| Evidence | Thesis impact |
|---|---|
| Alani sell-through remains strong after transition inventory normalizes | Bull case becomes much stronger. |
| CELSIUS brand returns to double-digit growth | Supports premium multiple. |
| Gross margin moves back above 50% with lower addbacks | Confirms integration benefits. |
| Pepsi receivables and promotional allowances stay elevated | Weakens revenue quality. |
| International becomes 8-10% of revenue with strong growth | Adds a second runway. |
| Preferred-stock treatment becomes clearer or less punitive | Improves common-equity valuation. |

11. International revenue by geography.
12. Any caffeine, labeling, youth-marketing, or Alani Nu product-safety developments.

## Conclusion

CELH is not the same company investors owned during the original CELSIUS breakout. It is now a more scaled but more complicated energy portfolio. That makes the opportunity more nuanced.

At $29.61, the stock is no longer obviously expensive on revenue or forward earnings. The market is giving investors a chance to underwrite the portfolio at a reasonable headline multiple. But the right valuation is not the clean vendor EV. Pepsi's preferred stock, distribution control, customer concentration, and board rights are part of the common-equity analysis.

I would not chase the stock solely because Q1 beat estimates. The better setup is to wait for evidence that Alani Nu's Pepsi transition is translating into sustained consumer takeaway, that core CELSIUS is not rolling over, and that gross margin recovers. If those data points appear, the stock has meaningful upside. If Q1 was mostly transition load-in and acquired growth, the common can still be a value trap despite the lower share price.
