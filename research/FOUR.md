# Shift4 Payments (FOUR) Investment Research

Date: 2026-05-06
Ticker: NYSE: FOUR
Price snapshot: $40.97 as of the 2026-05-05 close / last regular-market quote from Yahoo chart data. StockAnalysis and related market-data sources showed market capitalization around $3.3B and enterprise value around $7.0B, with differences depending on whether fully diluted common shares and mandatory convertible preferred stock are treated as equity or debt-like capital.

This is investment research, not personalized financial advice.

## Executive Summary

Shift4 is a more interesting company than the headline label "payments processor" suggests. It is an integrated payments and commerce platform with strong positions in restaurants, hospitality, sports and entertainment venues, and, after Global Blue, luxury retail and tax-free shopping. The company is trying to do something more valuable than generic acquiring: it wants to own embedded payments in verticals where the payment flow is tied tightly to software, operations, point-of-sale, commerce, and customer experience.

The stock, however, tells a very different story from the operating narrative. At roughly $41 per share, FOUR is trading near its 52-week low and at valuation levels that look optically cheap: about 1.7x EV/Sales, 8.9x EV/EBITDA, 14.0x EV/FCF, and 7.4x forward earnings on StockAnalysis data. A business expected to generate roughly $1.17B to $1.22B of adjusted EBITDA in 2026 is valued by the market at an enterprise value around $7B before making preferred-stock adjustments. That is not an exuberant multiple.

The reason the stock is cheap is also clear. Shift4 is now a more complex story. It has layered a large Global Blue acquisition, Smartpay, prior international assets, meaningful debt, mandatory convertible preferred stock, governance transition, and a founder departure into what was already a competitive payments business. Adjusted EBITDA is strong, but GAAP net income is much lower. Free cash flow is real, but the bridge from adjusted metrics to owner earnings needs scrutiny. Short interest is high. Analysts still see upside, but sentiment has cooled materially.

The bull case is that the market is over-penalizing complexity. Shift4 has valuable vertical positions, a large cross-sell funnel, a founder who has been buying stock aggressively in the open market, and 2026 guidance that still implies strong EBITDA and free cash flow. If Global Blue works, SkyTab continues gaining share, and leverage comes down, the current price could prove far too low.

The bear case is that Shift4 is becoming a levered payments roll-up whose adjusted growth masks integration risk, take-rate pressure, cyclicality, dilution, and capital-allocation complexity. In that version of the story, the low multiple is not a mistake; it is the appropriate discount for a business whose quality is harder to underwrite than it first appears.

My read: Shift4 is a high-upside, higher-risk payments compounder. It is not the cleanest business in the coverage universe, but it may be mispriced if management can prove organic growth, integration execution, and deleveraging. The investment debate is unusually sharp: the base-case DCF points toward roughly $100+ per share, while a credible bear case can land below the current price. That makes the stock potentially compelling, but only for an investor willing to underwrite complexity.

## Company Overview

Shift4 provides integrated payment processing and commerce technology to merchants. The company is strongest in verticals where payments are operationally embedded rather than purely transactional: restaurants, hotels, stadiums, arenas, resorts, specialty retail, e-commerce, airlines, and other enterprise or SMB environments that need more than a generic card terminal.

The core pitch is simple. Shift4 wants to be the single payments and commerce layer for a merchant. It provides end-to-end processing, merchant acquiring, a proprietary omni-channel gateway, POS and mobile payment tools, SkyTab, e-commerce capabilities, risk and security tools, reporting, analytics, and software integrations. In practice, that means Shift4 sits between the merchant, the consumer, the card networks, the sponsor banks, software partners, and sometimes the point-of-sale system itself.

This positioning matters because payments can be a commodity when the provider is just moving money. They become more attractive when the provider is embedded into the software and operating workflow of the merchant. A restaurant that runs front-of-house operations, tableside ordering, loyalty, reporting, and payments through a system is not just buying basis points of processing. A hotel or stadium that integrates payments across booking, check-in, concessions, retail, and online ordering is not simply swapping one acquirer for another. That is where Shift4 is trying to create stickier economics.

## Business Model

Shift4 reports one operating segment, but the business has several economic layers. The largest is payments-based revenue, which was $3.471B in FY2025. Global Blue added tax-free shopping revenue, which contributed $255M in FY2025 after the acquisition. Subscription and other revenue was $454M. Total gross revenue was $4.180B.

Gross revenue is not the best way to understand the business, because a large portion is passed through to card networks and related parties. Management emphasizes gross revenue less network fees, or GRLNF, as the cleaner economic revenue base. In FY2025, gross revenue was $4.180B, network fees were $2.199B, and GRLNF was $1.981B. After other costs of sales and equipment depreciation, gross profit was $1.354B.

The company processed $209B of volume in FY2025, up from $165B in FY2024. That volume produced a blended spread of about 61 basis points for the year, with Q4 at about 57 basis points. This spread is important. If volume grows but spread compresses, headline payment volume can look better than economics. If volume grows while GRLNF and gross profit grow faster, the platform is becoming more valuable. In 2025, GRLNF grew 46% and gross profit grew 47%, both faster than gross revenue and volume, though acquisitions materially influenced the year.

Shift4's vertical mix is central to the thesis. The company claims the #2 position in U.S. restaurants, #1 in U.S. hospitality, #1 in U.S. sports and entertainment, and, after Global Blue, #1 globally in luxury retail tax-free shopping. Those claims are hard to normalize across all competitors, but they frame the company's ambition: dominate attractive payment verticals, then cross-sell broader commerce and software functionality.

The international mix is also changing. In FY2025, U.S. revenue was $3.312B and international revenue was $868M. Global Blue and Smartpay make the company more global, more diversified, and potentially more valuable, but also more exposed to regulatory complexity, travel cycles, FX, integration execution, and luxury retail spending.

## Financial Snapshot

The headline financial growth is strong. FY2025 volume rose to $209B, gross revenue grew 25% to $4.180B, GRLNF grew 46% to $1.981B, and gross profit grew 47% to $1.354B. Adjusted EBITDA increased from $678M in FY2024 to $970M in FY2025. That is a substantial step-up in scale.

The quality of that growth needs a more careful read. GAAP net income was $147M in FY2025, down from $295M in FY2024, even though adjusted EBITDA increased sharply. The gap comes from depreciation and amortization, interest expense, acquisition and integration costs, stock-based compensation, FX and other items, impairments, and debt-extinguishment costs. None of that means the adjusted EBITDA number is useless, but it does mean that adjusted EBITDA is carrying a lot of the bull narrative.

Cash generation is meaningful. Operating cash flow was $634M in FY2025, and company-defined adjusted free cash flow summed to roughly $500M across the four quarters. Q4 was especially strong, with $59B of volume, $1.189B of gross revenue, $610M of GRLNF, $429M of gross profit, $304M of adjusted EBITDA, $224M of operating cash flow, and $171M of adjusted free cash flow.

The 2026 guide is important because it shows management still expects growth despite the stock decline. Shift4 guided to $240B to $260B of payment volume, $2.50B to $2.60B of GRLNF, $1.165B to $1.215B of adjusted EBITDA, $490M to $510M of adjusted free cash flow, and non-GAAP EPS of $5.50 to $5.70. If achieved, those numbers make the current equity valuation look undemanding.

The balance sheet is the biggest change in the story. At year-end 2025, Shift4 had $964M of cash and cash equivalents against $4.589B of principal debt. That debt included $633M of 2027 convertible notes, $1.650B of 2032 senior notes at 6.75%, $1.309B of 2033 euro notes at 5.50%, and a $997M term loan facility. The company also issued $1B of 6.00% mandatory convertible preferred stock in May 2025. Management projected annualized interest expense of about $250M after financing activity.

This makes leverage a central underwriting issue. On 2025 adjusted EBITDA, net debt was roughly 3.7x and gross debt was roughly 4.7x. That is manageable if EBITDA grows, integration goes well, and cash flow is directed toward deleveraging. It becomes much more problematic if payment volumes slow, take rates compress, or Global Blue underperforms.

## Management and Governance

Shift4 is also going through a governance transition. Founder Jared Isaacman stepped down from the CEO role in June 2025 amid his NASA nomination, later became NASA Administrator in December 2025, and resigned from executive chairman and board roles. Taylor Lauber became CEO in June 2025 and chairman in December 2025. Nancy Disman stepped down as CFO in September 2025 and returned to the board; Christopher Cruz became CFO.

The February 2026 simplification transaction collapsed the Up-C structure, converted founder/Rook Class B and Class C interests into Class A, eliminated majority voting control, and relieved Shift4 of material future TRA payments to Rook. The founder/Rook received approximately $192M of value, including $139M of cash and 423,296 mandatory convertible preferred shares. The transaction simplified the story, but it did not remove founder influence entirely. After simplification, Isaacman still controlled approximately 27% of voting power.

There is an obvious bull and bear interpretation. The bull view is that simplification removes a messy controlled-company structure, broadens potential investor appeal, and reduces future tax receivable agreement complexity. The bear view is that the company paid meaningful value to unwind founder-related arrangements while the founder remains influential and no longer runs the business day to day. For a company already digesting large acquisitions, governance clarity matters.

Insider behavior is one of the more positive signals. Jared Isaacman has made several large open-market purchases. OpenInsider and SEC Form 4 filings show purchases of about $2.0M on 2026-03-10, another $2.0M on 2026-03-02, about $13.7M across 2026-02-26 and 2026-02-27, and about $16.3M across 2025-08-08 and 2025-08-11. These were open-market purchases, not grants or option exercises. That does not solve the governance question, but it does show that the founder has been willing to deploy meaningful personal capital at prices above or around recent levels.

There has also been insider selling, but the character is different. Nancy Disman sold 5,000 shares in December 2025 under a 10b5-1 plan. Taylor Lauber sold shares around the same period and had plan-related context disclosed in SEC footnotes. Several Form 4 transactions were tax-withholding shares on RSU vesting rather than discretionary sales. Overall, the insider signal skews positive because the largest discretionary transactions are founder purchases.

Henry Ellenbogen's Durable Capital Partners held about 6.61M shares, roughly 3.9% of the portfolio, and had recently added. Institutional ownership is high, but data providers differ on the exact figure. StockAnalysis showed 81.24M shares outstanding, 28.1% insider ownership, 80.4% institutional ownership, and a 46.16M share float. HoldingsChannel listed 427 institutional holders, including Durable Capital, BlackRock, Vanguard, Darlington Partners, and Wasatch. MarketBeat showed a higher institutional ownership percentage, likely due to methodology and share-class differences. The practical takeaway is that Shift4 is well owned institutionally, but the float is smaller than the market cap might suggest because founder/insider ownership is substantial.

Short interest is high. MarketBeat showed 15.04M shares sold short as of 2026-04-15, equal to 24.7% of float and 9.75 days to cover. StockAnalysis showed the same absolute short interest but calculated it as 32.6% of float and 6.8 days to cover. The exact percentage depends on float methodology, but either way the short base is meaningful. This is one reason the stock can move sharply on changes in sentiment, and it also tells us the bear case has real sponsorship.

Analyst sentiment is mixed but still implies upside. StockAnalysis showed 20 analysts with a Buy consensus and an average price target of $79.05, with a range from $50 to $127. MarketBeat showed a more cautious Hold consensus across 23 analysts, with 11 buys, 10 holds, and 2 sells. Its average price target was $72.76, with a range from $50 to $107. The important nuance is that recent sentiment has cooled. Several firms downgraded the stock or cut targets in March and April 2026, with new or revised targets clustered near $50.

Public investor debate is split in the same way. Bullish write-ups have emphasized Shift4's hospitality position, software integrations, Global Blue cross-sell opportunity, and low forward valuation. Bob Hammel's May 2025 Substack framed Shift4 as well positioned in hospitality and restaurants, while explicitly flagging concerns around Global Blue, leverage, founder transition, acquisition dependence, and expansion outside core verticals. Other public bull-case summaries highlighted Global Blue as potentially transformative because it expands Shift4's merchant base and international opportunity. No clearly accessible public Value Investors Club write-up was found during this review.

## Competitive Position

Shift4 competes in a crowded and aggressive market. Depending on the product and vertical, relevant competitors include Fiserv/Clover, Global Payments, FIS/Worldpay, Adyen, Stripe, PayPal/Braintree, Block/Square, Toast, Lightspeed, Shopify, Oracle MICROS-style systems, NCR-type offerings, FreedomPay, and tax-free shopping alternatives.

The company's differentiation is strongest where it can bundle payments, software, hardware, gateway, acquiring, and vertical-specific workflow into one relationship. In restaurants, SkyTab competes against Toast, Square, Lightspeed, and other POS/payment combinations. In hospitality and venues, Shift4 can be attractive because payments need to work across complex operational environments. In luxury retail and tax-free shopping, Global Blue brings a different kind of network and international merchant base.

The risk is that many of these competitors are larger, better capitalized, or more specialized in certain niches. Toast is focused deeply on restaurants. Adyen is a high-quality global enterprise payments platform. Fiserv and Global Payments have scale and distribution. Stripe has developer mindshare. Shopify owns commerce workflows for many merchants. If Shift4's value proposition becomes primarily price, margins and take rates can compress. The company needs to win on integration and vertical depth, not just processing cost.

## Bull Case

The bull case is that Shift4 is being valued like a levered, messy payment acquirer just as it is becoming a larger vertical commerce platform. The company has strong positions in attractive verticals, a growing international footprint, and a large cross-sell opportunity across Global Blue, Smartpay, SkyTab, hospitality, restaurants, venues, and specialty retail. If the platform works, Shift4 can grow volume, monetize more of that volume through GRLNF and gross profit, and expand EBITDA faster than revenue.

In this version of the future, Global Blue is not just an acquisition; it is a distribution wedge. Shift4 gains access to luxury retail and tax-free shopping relationships, then cross-sells payments, dynamic currency conversion, and broader commerce tools. Smartpay expands Australia and New Zealand. SkyTab continues to gain share in restaurants. Enterprise wins in hotels, venues, resorts, and airlines deepen the company's vertical moat.

The bull-case valuation assumes the company grows free cash flow at a high-teens rate for several years, deleverages, and earns a terminal FCF multiple closer to a high-quality vertical software/payments compounder. The valuation agent's bull case produced an estimated value around $202 per share. That is not a base-rate expectation, but it shows the asymmetry if the market's current skepticism proves too severe.

What would confirm the bull case is straightforward: sustained volume and GRLNF growth, stable or rising spreads, visible organic growth separate from acquisitions, successful Global Blue integration, improving GAAP profitability, falling leverage, and share repurchases that are clearly accretive after accounting for dilution and preferred conversion.

## Bear Case

The bear case is a quality-and-complexity thesis. Shift4's headline growth and adjusted EBITDA increasingly depend on acquisitions, add-backs, leverage, and cross-sell execution. The company is not just selling a simple payments story anymore; it is integrating Global Blue, Smartpay, prior international assets, a tax-free shopping network, a restaurant POS platform, enterprise payment relationships, and a more complicated capital structure.

The gap between GAAP net income and adjusted EBITDA is the first warning sign. In FY2025, net income was $147M while adjusted EBITDA was $970M. The bridge included $432M of depreciation and amortization, $190M of interest expense, $84M of acquisition/restructuring/integration costs, $85M of equity-based compensation and related payroll tax, FX and other non-recurring items, impairments, and debt-extinguishment losses. Some of these are legitimate adjustments, but the cumulative gap is large. If integration costs, SBC, acquisition-related amortization, and interest are recurring features of the strategy, adjusted EBITDA may overstate owner earnings.

Leverage is the second risk. Gross debt was $4.589B at year-end 2025, with about $3.625B of net debt before considering preferred stock. Annualized interest expense of about $250M is a meaningful fixed claim relative to GAAP net income. The mandatory convertible preferred stock adds dilution and a 6% dividend. In a downturn, the combination of leverage, cyclicality, and integration risk could pressure the equity.

The third risk is acquisition execution. Goodwill increased from $1.456B to $2.704B in 2025, and other intangible assets rose from $758M to $2.829B. Goodwill plus intangibles represented roughly two-thirds of total assets. That is not automatically bad, but it means the balance sheet now embeds a large amount of expected future value from acquisitions. If Global Blue or other deals disappoint, impairments and lower returns on capital become real possibilities.

The bear-case valuation assumes slower growth, margin pressure, and a lower terminal multiple. The valuation agent's bear case produced an estimated value around $29 per share. This case becomes more credible if organic growth weakens, SkyTab requires heavy subsidies, Global Blue proves cyclical or hard to integrate, spreads compress, leverage remains elevated, or free cash flow fails to converge with adjusted EBITDA.

## Base Case

The base case is that Shift4 is messy but undervalued. The company continues to grow, but not cleanly. Global Blue contributes, SkyTab remains competitive, and hospitality/restaurant/venue verticals keep expanding. At the same time, leverage, preferred dilution, and integration costs prevent the market from awarding a premium multiple.

The base-case DCF produced an estimated value around $107 per share. A more conservative multiple-based synthesis is lower, roughly $50 to $80 per share if FOUR deserves 8x to 10x FY2026E EBITDA or 12x to 14x FY2026E FCF. I would put more weight on that conservative range until the company proves cleaner organic growth and deleveraging. Even so, the current price around $41 appears to discount a lot of bad news.

The base case works if management hits 2026 guidance, converts adjusted EBITDA into cash, starts reducing leverage, and shows that Global Blue is additive rather than distracting. It fails if the company needs continual acquisitions and adjustments to sustain the growth narrative.

## Valuation

At the current quote, the valuation is not demanding on headline metrics. The valuation agent found market cap around $3.33B and enterprise value around $6.98B. StockAnalysis data showed EV/Sales of 1.67x, EV/EBITDA of 8.90x, EV/FCF of 13.99x, and forward P/E of 7.35x. Those multiples sit much closer to mature acquirer territory than high-growth vertical software territory.

The central valuation problem is deciding which peer group Shift4 deserves. If it is mostly a levered merchant acquirer, the current multiple may be reasonable. If it is a vertical commerce platform with strong organic growth, high incremental margins, and a durable software/payments wedge, the multiple is too low. The stock's upside depends on migrating investor perception from the first bucket toward the second.

The simplified DCF uses free cash flow growth and terminal multiples rather than pretending to precisely model every acquisition and working-capital item. The output is wide, which is appropriate for a company with this much operating and capital-structure uncertainty.

| Scenario | Core assumption | Estimated value/share |
|---|---:|---:|
| Bull | Strong growth, successful integration, premium terminal multiple | ~$202 |
| Base | Continued growth, some complexity discount, moderate terminal multiple | ~$107 |
| Bear | Slower growth, margin/take-rate pressure, lower terminal multiple | ~$29 |

The reverse DCF is the more useful lens. At the current enterprise value, the market appears to be embedding only low-to-mid-single-digit FCF growth if terminal FCF multiples settle around 12x to 14x. That suggests investors are not paying for much growth. Instead, the market is discounting the possibility that reported FCF quality, leverage, integration, and competitive dynamics are worse than the adjusted story implies.

A multiple-based cross-check is more conservative than the DCF. If Shift4 earns roughly the midpoint of 2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance and trades at 8x to 10x EBITDA, the equity can support a value above the current price, but the exact number depends heavily on debt, cash, preferred stock treatment, and share count. On FCF, 12x to 14x FY2026E adjusted free cash flow supports a rough $50 to $80 range. That is still attractive versus the current quote, but much less heroic than the DCF's base case.

## Catalysts

The most important catalyst is evidence that Global Blue is working. Investors need to see cross-sell, synergy realization, resilient tax-free shopping economics, and no major control, integration, or impairment surprises. Because Global Blue changed the company's scale and risk profile, successful integration could materially improve the narrative.

The second catalyst is deleveraging. If Shift4 uses cash flow to reduce net debt and the market sees interest expense becoming less burdensome, the equity risk premium should fall. A levered payments company gets a lower multiple than a self-funding compounder. Deleveraging is the bridge between those two perceptions.

The third catalyst is organic growth disclosure. The market needs confidence that growth is not merely acquired. Better disclosure around organic volume, GRLNF, gross profit, cohort economics, SkyTab retention, and take-rate stability would help investors separate durable business performance from acquisition noise.

Share repurchases could also be powerful if the stock remains depressed and the company is right about intrinsic value. But buybacks should be judged against leverage, preferred dilution, and acquisition needs. Repurchasing stock while carrying elevated debt can be either highly accretive or imprudent depending on execution.

## Risks and Red Flags

Shift4's biggest risk is that adjusted EBITDA overstates the economic earnings power of the business. In 2025, the gap between GAAP net income and adjusted EBITDA was large. Investors should watch whether acquisition costs, integration costs, SBC, amortization, and other add-backs decline as a percentage of EBITDA. If they do not, the market will keep applying a discount.

The second major risk is leverage. Debt and preferred stock increase the equity's sensitivity to execution. A cyclical slowdown in restaurants, hospitality, venues, travel, or luxury retail could hit volume at the same time the company is carrying higher interest expense and integration demands.

The third risk is competition and take-rate pressure. Payments is a hard industry. Large acquirers, vertical software players, global payment platforms, and POS systems all want the same economics. Shift4 needs to prove that vertical integration protects spreads. If the company wins business mainly through price, free hardware, or incentives, growth may come with lower returns.

The fourth risk is acquisition complexity. Global Blue, Smartpay, Vectron, Finaro, and potential Worldline North America assets all add geographies, regulators, systems, employees, compliance regimes, and integration dependencies. The company can create value if these assets expand its platform. It can destroy value if they turn Shift4 into a sprawling payments conglomerate with too many moving parts.

Governance remains worth monitoring. The Up-C simplification helps, but founder influence, pledged shares, board transition, and the post-founder operating era all matter. This is especially true because the company is making large strategic moves while investor trust is fragile.

## Variant Perception

The positive variant view is that investors are overreacting to complexity and underweighting insider behavior, vertical strength, and 2026 cash generation. If the company hits guidance, integrates Global Blue, and begins deleveraging, the current multiple could look absurdly low in hindsight. The large founder purchases strengthen that argument.

The negative variant view is that the market has correctly identified a business whose reported growth is becoming harder to trust. High short interest is not random. Bears are focused on leverage, adjusted metrics, acquisition dependence, preferred dilution, and competition. The stock is cheap because investors are no longer willing to give Shift4 the benefit of the doubt.

The key question is whether the next year simplifies or further complicates the story. If the company exits 2026 with cleaner organic disclosure, lower leverage, successful Global Blue integration, and strong FCF conversion, the bull case becomes much easier to underwrite. If not, the low multiple may persist.

## Monitoring Checklist

The metrics to watch are payment volume, GRLNF, gross profit, blended spread, adjusted EBITDA, GAAP net income, operating cash flow, adjusted free cash flow, SBC, acquisition and integration add-backs, net debt, interest expense, preferred dilution, share count, Global Blue synergy commentary, SkyTab merchant growth, restaurant/hospitality volume trends, short interest, insider purchases, and any change in analyst estimates after earnings.

The thesis is improving if GRLNF and gross profit grow faster than volume, spreads remain stable, acquisition add-backs shrink, cash conversion improves, net leverage falls toward 2.5x or lower, and management provides clearer organic growth disclosure. The thesis weakens if growth is mostly acquired, Global Blue integration becomes noisy, leverage remains elevated, adjusted EBITDA keeps diverging from GAAP earnings, or competitive pressure forces take-rate compression.

## Conclusion

Shift4 is not a clean story, but it is a compelling one. The company has real assets: strong vertical positions, embedded payment workflows, restaurant and hospitality depth, enterprise venue relationships, Global Blue's luxury retail network, meaningful free cash flow, and a founder buying stock aggressively in the open market. At the current valuation, the market is not paying much for success.

The discount is not irrational. Leverage is elevated, the capital structure is more complicated, acquisitions have become central to the thesis, adjusted EBITDA requires scrutiny, and short interest is high. This is not the kind of company where a low multiple alone is enough. The investor has to believe management can simplify the story, integrate Global Blue, produce cleaner cash conversion, and reduce balance-sheet risk.

My research conclusion is cautiously positive but risk-aware. Shift4 looks potentially mispriced if 2026 guidance is credible and the company can prove deleveraging and organic growth. The current price seems to discount a lot of failure, while the base and bull cases offer substantial upside. But this should be underwritten as a complex, levered payments investment, not as a pristine software compounder.
